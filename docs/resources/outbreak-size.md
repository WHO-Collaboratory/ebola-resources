## Synthesizing evidence

### State of the evidence

Six analyses dated 22 May to 4 July 2026 address outbreak size or near-term trajectory: three peer-reviewed papers, two dashboards, and one agency report. The evidence base is growing but not yet stable, because later analyses use revised surveillance streams and at least one is explicitly re-run as new data arrive.[^abbott-2026-front-matter-5]

### How the estimates compare

The clearest operational signal is that the case under-detection rate appears lower in later estimates, but this cannot be read as surveillance catch-up alone because methods, geographies, and denominators change. Epistorm’s 22 May mobility-ABC snapshot implies very high under-detection against 101 confirmed cases, with posterior medians of 1,047–1,944 infections across importation-detection scenarios.[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-2][^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-3] BlueDot’s travel-based estimates move from about 3.5× confirmed cases on 22 May to about 2× against 626 confirmed cases on 9 June, while Abbott et al.’s joint Bayesian model estimates 2.4–5.0× against 1,561 confirmed cases on 4 July.[^bluedot-2026-key-takeaways---as-of-may-3][^bluedot-2026-update---as-of-june-9-3][^abbott-2026-results-1] Divergence is driven mainly by exportation assumptions, travel volumes, geographic source populations, and whether ascertainment is fixed, scenario-based, or jointly estimated.

### Key uncertainties and assumptions

The largest driver of uncertainty across analyses is how representative exported cases are of source incidence, including whether border detections are complete and whether travellers have the same exposure risk as residents.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-1][^bluedot-2026-assumptions-and-rationale-for-each-parameter-10] Death-based and renewal estimates also depend on borrowed CFR, delay, and generation-time parameters from prior BDBV or Ebola outbreaks, with current data often weakly informative.[^mccabe-2026-22][^abbott-2026-limitations-3] Geographic scope also matters: estimates change when the source is Ituri alone versus broader Ituri/Nord Kivu/South Kivu footprints.[^mccabe-2026-18][^bluedot-2026-assumptions-and-rationale-for-each-parameter-11]

### What would reduce uncertainty

The most important addition would be verified line-list data with symptom onset, confirmation, death, health zone, and travel history, because current aggregate report-date totals weakly identify timing and ascertainment.[^abbott-2026-limitations-6][^chamla-2026-discussion-6] Second would be updated cross-border movement data, including informal routes, linked to exposure timing and care-seeking pathways.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-4]

### What this means for the response

The evidence suggests substantial under-detection: confirmed counts should be treated as a lower bound, not as the operational size of the outbreak. It also suggests that under-detection estimates are still structurally uncertain, so response scaling should be read against multiplier ranges rather than single absolute case estimates. Geographic targeting remains centred on Ituri and connected eastern DRC provinces, with cross-border preparedness justified by repeated exported-case signals and documented importation risk corridors.[^epistorm-2026-analysis-under-the-current-expansion-2][^chamla-2026-results-7]

### Abbott et al. (2026) Estimating the current size of the 2026 DRC Bundibugyo virus outbreak

[Read the report](https://epiforecasts.io/BVDOutbreakSize/dev/analysis)

### Overview

This is a real-time joint Bayesian analysis estimating the true cumulative size of the 2026 BVD outbreak in DRC, including unreported infections, refreshed as new data arrive. It is a real-time joint Bayesian estimate of the current outbreak size, using a discrete-time renewal process on a daily grid that jointly fits multiple DRC surveillance streams — suspected cases, suspected deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and confirmed deaths — alongside cases and deaths exported to Uganda.[^abbott-2026-front-matter-1][^abbott-2026-front-matter-2] As of its 4 July 2026 data cutoff, the model estimates 3,718–7,864 cumulative infections (90% credible interval), against 1,561 laboratory-confirmed cases, implying confirmed cases capture roughly 2.4–5.0× fewer infections than the true count.[^abbott-2026-results-1]

### Estimate

**Headline joint estimate (data as of 4 July 2026):** Cumulative infections are estimated at 30% CI 4,943–5,898; 60% CI 4,484–6,577; 90% CI 3,718–7,864 infections to date, reported and unreported.[^abbott-2026-results-2] Against the 1,561 laboratory-confirmed cases at the cutoff, this is roughly 2.4–5.0× as many infections, so confirmed cases are estimated to capture only a small share of the outbreak.[^abbott-2026-results-3]

**Outbreak start and age:** The outbreak is estimated to have begun on 30% CI 2026-02-22–2026-03-03; 60% CI 2026-02-17–2026-03-08; 90% CI 2026-02-06–2026-03-16, with an elapsed age to the cutoff of 30% CI 123–132 days; 60% CI 118–137 days; 90% CI 110–148 days.[^abbott-2026-results-4]

**Reproduction number:** The initial reproduction number is estimated at 30% CI 1.48–1.69; 60% CI 1.38–1.84; 90% CI 1.26–2.14, and the latest to be 30% CI 1.26–1.53; 60% CI 1.11–1.74; 90% CI 0.86–2.18.[^abbott-2026-results-5]

**Case-fatality ratio:** Estimated at 30% CI 0.39–0.45; 60% CI 0.36–0.48; 90% CI 0.30–0.55.[^abbott-2026-results-6]

### Methods

**Real-time joint Bayesian renewal model:** This is a real-time joint Bayesian extension of the McCabe et al. approach, fitting all available DRC and Uganda surveillance streams simultaneously within a single posterior. The model is a discrete-time renewal process on a daily grid that fits the DRC suspected cases, suspected deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and confirmed deaths, and the cases and deaths exported to Uganda jointly.[^abbott-2026-front-matter-2] It estimates the latent infections, symptom onsets and deaths over time; the time-varying reproduction number with its growth rate and doubling time; and, alongside these, the case-fatality ratio, the ascertainment of each surveillance system, and a short-term forecast of each stream over the coming week.[^abbott-2026-front-matter-7] The model has evolved into a real-time joint Bayesian estimate of the current outbreak size, a discrete-time renewal model with a time-varying reproduction number fitted to more of the available data streams than the original McCabe et al. report.[^abbott-2026-origins-of-this-work-1] Rather than adopting the point-estimate onset-to-death delay used by McCabe et al., the model uses a Bayesian reanalysis of the 2012 Isiro Bundibugyo virus outbreak line list to re-estimate the delay with uncertainty, and samples every other delay from priors centred on published Ebola estimates, so delay uncertainty propagates through the posterior.[^abbott-2026-origins-of-this-work-2] Sensitivity analyses include fits using individual data streams separately (exports only, deaths only, confirmed cases only, etc.) to compare per-stream posteriors over outbreak size with the joint estimate, as well as a frozen re-fit cut to approximately one week before the current cutoff for forecast validation, a delay-distribution sensitivity run, and a molecular clock sensitivity run.

**Single-stream comparator models:** Alongside the joint model, single-stream models are fitted for each count-based stream — exported cases, suspected deaths, suspected cases, laboratory-confirmed cases, confirmed deaths, and deaths among exports — so each stream's posterior over the outbreak size can be compared with the joint.[^abbott-2026-methods-1] These models apply to the same DRC national scope, with Uganda exports as a separate stream.

### Assumptions

**Generation interval:** The generation interval is assumed Gamma-distributed, using the EVD serial interval from WHO Ebola Response Team 2014 (mean 15.3 days, SD 9.3 days) as a proxy; this maps to a Gamma with shape near 2.71 and scale near 5.65, and the priors are centred on those values with spreads carrying the source's reported uncertainty.[^abbott-2026-methods-14]

**Incubation period and delay distributions:** The incubation period uses the MacNeil et al. (2010) estimate from the 2007 Uganda BVD outbreak (mean 6.3 days, 95% CI 5.2–7.3, n=24); the mean prior reproduces that interval, and the SD prior is the authors' own choice.[^abbott-2026-methods-15] Onset-to-report follows a Gamma with priors centred on the Bayesian reanalysis of the 2012 Isiro line list (implied mean about 4 days).[^abbott-2026-methods-16] Onset-to-death is the convolution of onset-to-admission and admission-to-death, each a Gamma with priors centred on the reanalysis posteriors, with an implied combined mean of about 13 days.[^abbott-2026-methods-17]

**Case-fatality ratio:** The CFR prior is Beta(6.6, 13.4) with mean 0.33 and 95% interval roughly 15–54%, matching the CDC figure of 55/169 and the corrected central CFR in the 20 May McCabe et al. report.[^abbott-2026-methods-18] Nearly all of the delays, the CFR and the laboratory assumptions are set by priors informed at best by a handful of literature sources, often from other outbreaks, and the data do little to move them, so posteriors largely track their priors.[^abbott-2026-limitations-3]

**Ascertainment:** The DRC and Uganda ascertainment fractions share a logit-scale hyperprior centred on 75%, reflecting active case-finding under a declared Ebola response rather than baseline passive surveillance.[^abbott-2026-methods-19] The outbreak size and the fraction of cases each surveillance system reports are estimated jointly; McCabe et al. have no ascertainment component.[^abbott-2026-origins-of-this-work-3]

**Molecular clock and genetic TMRCA:** The TMRCA is placed at a mean of 25 March 2026 from a BEAST time tree of the first ten sequenced genomes, with the clock fixed at 1.2×10⁻³ substitutions/site/year from the West African EBOV epidemic.[^abbott-2026-methods-9] The TMRCA is treated as a lower bound on the outbreak age, since adding sequences can only push it earlier.[^abbott-2026-methods-20] The molecular clock is fixed; neither cross-outbreak nor clock uncertainty is propagated.[^abbott-2026-methods-21]

**Cryptic phase and outbreak seed:** The outbreak is assumed to have started from a single zoonotic seed, which grew deterministically through an unobserved cryptic exponential phase before the renewal process took over, doubling m times with a wide prior centred on three doublings: m ~ Normal⁺(3, 3).[^abbott-2026-methods-22][^abbott-2026-methods-23] The intervention ramp effect is weakly identified, since with only a few situation reports straddling it the ramp effect and the pre-ramp reproduction number are not well separated.[^abbott-2026-limitations-4]

**Cross-border travel:** The Ituri-side daily total of 1,871 travellers comes from McCabe et al., used as a Normal prior with SD 200 (~10% CV), truncated at zero; the source population of 4,392,200 is fixed at the census value.[^abbott-2026-methods-24]

**Stream independence:** Surveillance streams are fitted as conditionally independent given latent incidence but observe overlapping people, which can understate uncertainty; whether the streams imply mutually consistent outbreak sizes is not assessed.[^abbott-2026-limitations-5]

**Non-BVD background:** A non-BVD background rate is included in the suspected-case stream, with a half-normal prior on the natural scale, wide enough that laboratory positivity (~28% of analysed specimens) identifies the background, which is inferred to be the majority of the suspect pool.[^abbott-2026-methods-25][^abbott-2026-methods-26]

### Limitations

**Reliance on weakly-informed priors:** Nearly all quantities — delays, the CFR, and laboratory assumptions — are set by priors informed at best by a handful of literature sources, often from other outbreaks and in places by the authors' own prior judgement. The data do little to move these posteriors, so they largely track their priors.[^abbott-2026-limitations-3]

**Aggregate data and weak timing signal:** The model has no counts by symptom onset or any other epidemiologically relevant date, only cumulative totals at the report date. The timing of the underlying epidemic is therefore weakly identified and recovered only through the assumed delays.[^abbott-2026-limitations-6] The DRC data are situation-report totals of suspected cases and deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and deaths, and specimens received and analysed; there is no line list and no information on case definitions or reporting completeness.[^abbott-2026-limitations-7] Every estimate is a model-based extrapolation under strong assumptions, not a measurement.[^abbott-2026-limitations-8]

**Revisions not modelled:** Later situation reports can revise earlier totals up or down as suspects are reclassified and newly-reporting health zones are added, and ascertainment probably rose over the window. This revision process is not modelled.[^abbott-2026-limitations-9]

**Stream independence understating uncertainty:** The streams are fitted as conditionally independent given latent incidence but observe overlapping people, which can understate uncertainty. Whether the streams imply mutually consistent outbreak sizes is not assessed.[^abbott-2026-limitations-5]

**Inherited epidemiological simplifications:** The model inherits McCabe et al.'s assumptions: a single zoonotic seed, an assumed generation interval, no spatial structure beyond the Ituri/Nord Kivu split, and no depletion of susceptibles. The onset-to-death delay is grounded on Isiro 2012 and the genetic seeding bound on an external clock rate, neither propagating cross-outbreak or clock uncertainty.[^abbott-2026-limitations-10]

**National bed model cannot capture local saturation:** The treatment-centre model carries one national bed capacity and one national demand, so it cannot represent local saturation. On 13 June, Ituri was at 93.9% occupancy while Sud-Kivu was at 21.9%, meaning the national bed shortfall understates local unmet need.[^abbott-2026-limitations-11]

**Code not independently replicated:** The model code, priors, and analysis were drafted by a language model from the McCabe et al. report and the companion delay reanalysis, then reviewed and revised. The work has not been independently replicated against the authors' code.[^abbott-2026-limitations-12]

**Intervention ramp weakly identified:** With only a few situation reports straddling the response ramp, the ramp effect and the pre-ramp reproduction number are not well separated.[^abbott-2026-limitations-4]

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:** DRC data come from the situation reports of the Institut National de Santé Publique (INSP), with each report giving the national cumulative suspected cases and deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and deaths, and the specimens received and analysed by the laboratory at the report date.[^abbott-2026-methods-2] By the 4 July 2026 cutoff, the data record 1,561 confirmed cases and 506 confirmed deaths.[^abbott-2026-methods-3] The suspected case and death series were frozen at their last stable vintage on 26 May 2026, where they stood at 1,077 suspected cases and 246 suspected deaths.[^abbott-2026-methods-4] Uganda data are the cases and the one death exported across the border, taken from the WHO situation reports and Disease Outbreak News.[^abbott-2026-methods-5] Three exported cases were recorded (to 23 May 2026) and one export death (to 14 May 2026).[^abbott-2026-methods-6] From 13 June 2026 the reports add a patient-movement table for the treatment centres, and daily admissions, in-care deaths, rule-outs and absconded flows are read as four additional count streams.[^abbott-2026-methods-7]

**Auxiliary data:** The cross-border traveller volume and source population come from McCabe et al.; the source population is fixed and the traveller volume is given a Normal prior around the McCabe et al. figure of 1,871 daily outbound travellers with SD 200.[^abbott-2026-methods-8] A BEAST time tree of the first ten sequenced genomes places the TMRCA at a mean of 25 March 2026; the molecular clock is fixed at 1.2×10⁻³ substitutions/site/year from the 2013–2016 West African Ebola epidemic.[^abbott-2026-methods-9] The incubation period comes from the 2007 Uganda BVD outbreak estimate (mean 6.3 days, 95% CI 5.2–7.3, n=24; MacNeil et al. 2010).[^abbott-2026-methods-10] The onset-to-report and onset-to-death delays use a Bayesian reanalysis of the 2012 Isiro Bundibugyo virus outbreak line list (Funk and Abbott, 2026), with the onset-to-admission delay having an implied mean of about 4 days.[^abbott-2026-methods-11] The CFR prior draws on the CDC summary of 55 deaths in 169 cases (~33%) across the two previous BVD outbreaks, and the companion BDBV reanalysis reporting 0.47 (95% CrI 0.31–0.65) for non-HCW confirmed cases.[^abbott-2026-methods-12] The generation interval uses the EVD serial interval from the WHO Ebola Response Team 2014 (mean 15.3 days, SD 9.3 days) as a generation-time proxy.[^abbott-2026-methods-13]

### BlueDot (2026) Estimating true size of the Ebola Bundibugyo virus disease outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo – update, as of June 9, 2026

[Read the report](https://bluedot.global/estimating-true-size-of-the-ebola-bundibugyo-virus-disease-outbreak-in-the-democratic-republic-of-congo-as-of-june-9-2026/)

### Overview

This analysis estimates the true size of the 2026 Bundibugyo virus disease outbreak in the DRC by using confirmed exported cases in Uganda as a signal of undetected transmission at the source. A deterministic approach with sensitivity analyses is applied, using population movement data and the number of exported cases confirmed in neighbouring Uganda.[^bluedot-2026-key-takeaways---as-of-may-1] The baseline estimate is 359 cases (95% CI: 74–1,048) as of May 22, approximately 3.5 times the confirmed case count at that date; sensitivity analyses assuming some exported cases remain undetected push this to as high as 718 cases (95% CI: 264–1,561) under the most conservative scenario.[^bluedot-2026-key-takeaways---as-of-may-2] An updated estimate using 14 imported cases in Uganda as of June 9 raises the central estimate to approximately twice the confirmed case count under the primary scenario, and higher under a reduced-travel sensitivity run.

### Estimate

The analysis reports two sets of estimates corresponding to two reference dates.

### Assumptions

**Ascertainment of exported cases:** The model assumes the 3 baseline exported cases represent complete ascertainment; sensitivity analyses with 1 to 3 additional undetected exported cases test the effect of imperfect ascertainment at the border.[^bluedot-2026-estimation-of-the-ebola-virus-disease-4]

**Assumption of uniform risk across travellers and residents:** The model assumes equivalent risk of infection and exposure across travellers and residents, and the authors acknowledge the true incidence rates in these two populations may not be equivalent.[^bluedot-2026-assumptions-and-rationale-for-each-parameter-10]

### Chamla et al. (2026) Size of the 2026 Ebola outbreak and risk of cross-border spillover from Bundibugyo virus in Ituri Province, DR Congo, and its implications for preparedness: a recalibrated stochastic modelling study

[Read the report](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/laninf/article/PIIS1473-3099(26)00320-8/fulltext)

### Overview

This analysis estimates the short-term epidemic trajectory of the 2026 BDBV outbreak in Ituri Province, DRC, and quantifies the probability of cross-border spillover into neighbouring countries, to guide regional preparedness priorities. It uses a stochastic SEIRD (susceptible, exposed, infectious, recovered, and dead) ensemble model calibrated to laboratory-confirmed case data, anchored on 598 cumulative confirmed cases on June 8, 2026, with projections extending 12 weeks forward.[^chamla-2026-summary-1] Under the central transmissibility scenario (R₀=1.71), the model projects a median of 990 cumulative confirmed cases by week 12 (June 24, 2026; 90% PI 709–1293), with the observed confirmed-case trajectory most consistent with this central scenario.[^chamla-2026-results-1]

### Estimate

Under the central transmissibility scenario (mean accepted R₀=1.71), the ensemble projected a median of 648 cumulative confirmed cases by week 10 (June 8, 2026; 50% PI 560–732), rising to **990 by week 12** (June 24, 2026; 50% PI 858–1141 and 90% PI 709–1293), with a projected median of 174 deaths at week 12.[^chamla-2026-results-2]

Under the low-transmissibility scenario (mean accepted R₀=1.42), cumulative confirmed cases reached a median of 870 by week 12 (50% PI 752–1004 and 90% PI 641–1133; 160 deaths) and 3,662 by week 24 (50% PI 2,884–4,484 and 90% PI 2,094–6,037; 673 deaths).[^chamla-2026-results-3]

By week 24, the central scenario projected a median of 8,210 cumulative confirmed cases (50% PI 6,636–10,287 and 90% PI 5,063–13,498) and approximately 1,440 deaths if transmission were merely sustained.[^chamla-2026-results-4]

Under the high-transmissibility scenario (mean accepted R₀=2.08), confirmed cases reached a median of 1,364 (50% PI 1,154–1,579 and 90% PI 975–1,807; 202 deaths) by week 12, rising to 66,420 (50% PI 56,560–78,937 and 90% PI 41,718–95,881; 10,644 deaths) by week 24.[^chamla-2026-results-5] Because the observed confirmed-case trajectory is consistent with the central scenario, the high scenario is not the operative trajectory; it is retained as an upper-bound contingency should control measures lapse.[^chamla-2026-results-6]

The analysis does not estimate a detection multiplier (ratio of true cases to confirmed cases); it calibrates directly to laboratory-confirmed cases with a reporting fraction set at 1.0, so the model's output is expressed in confirmed-case terms rather than as a back-calculated total infection count that exceeds reported counts.

### Methods

**Stochastic SEIRD ensemble model:** A discrete-time SEIRD framework applied to the affected health zones in Ituri and North Kivu provinces, with daily transitions implemented as binomial draws using standard mass-action incidence. Three transmissibility scenarios are run (low R₀=1.42, central R₀=1.71, high R₀=2.08), with response-intensity reductions from May 15, 2026, of 20% (low), 25% (central), and 15% (high).[^chamla-2026-methods-4][^chamla-2026-methods-5][^chamla-2026-methods-6][^chamla-2026-methods-7] Calibration was performed by simulation-filtering (approximate rejection sampling, not formal Bayesian inference): replicates were accepted if cumulative laboratory-confirmed cases at day 68 (June 8, 2026) fell within ±30% of 598 (range 419–777), retaining 400 calibration-passing replicates per scenario; the ±30% tolerance was chosen pragmatically given residual data uncertainty.[^chamla-2026-methods-8] Sensitivity analyses examined travel-while-infectious (5–25%), reporting completeness (0.60–0.90), and catchment size (250,000–500,000).[^chamla-2026-methods-3]

**Daily-hazard spillover model:** Importation risk to Uganda, South Sudan, Rwanda, and Burundi was modelled as a daily Poisson hazard driven by calibrated infectious prevalence, country-specific cross-border mobility, point-of-entry detection sensitivity from WHO Joint External Evaluation assessments, and travel-while-infectious parameters (Uganda 12%; South Sudan 15%; Rwanda and Burundi 8%).[^chamla-2026-methods-9] Importation probability was calculated as 1 − exp(cumulative 12-week hazard), yielding the probability of at least one importation for each country.[^chamla-2026-methods-10] These parameters were not empirically derived for this outbreak and represent the principal sources of uncertainty in spillover projections.[^chamla-2026-methods-11]

### Assumptions

**Transmissibility scenarios:** Three R₀ values span published BDBV estimates from the 2007 Uganda and 2012 DR Congo outbreaks: low R₀=1.42, central R₀=1.71, and high R₀=2.08. Response-intensity reductions applied from May 15, 2026, are 20% (low), 25% (central), and 15% (high), representing differing operational effectiveness.[^chamla-2026-methods-6][^chamla-2026-methods-15]

**Latent and infectious periods:** The latent period is fixed at 9.7 days and the infectious period at 5.0 days, in line with the broader filovirus literature. These are not varied across scenarios.[^chamla-2026-methods-16]

**Case-fatality ratio and reporting fraction:** The CFR is drawn from a uniform distribution (0.15–0.25), centred on the observed confirmed-case ratio of approximately 19% (115 of 598). Because calibration is anchored on laboratory-confirmed cases, the reporting fraction is set at 1.0 — laboratory-confirmed cases are assumed to be fully ascertained by definition — removing the need for a suspected-case ascertainment fraction.[^chamla-2026-methods-17]

**Homogeneous mixing and spatial structure:** The model assumes a homogeneously mixing catchment of 350,000 people across seven affected health zones. Spatial heterogeneity is not captured; this is a stated limitation.[^chamla-2026-methods-18]

**Calibration tolerance:** Simulation-filtering accepts replicates whose cumulative laboratory-confirmed cases at day 68 fall within ±30% of 598 (range 419–777). The ±30% tolerance was chosen pragmatically given residual data uncertainty, not derived from a formal likelihood or identifiability analysis.[^chamla-2026-methods-19][^chamla-2026-methods-20]

**Response-intensity reduction:** The model incorporates a transmission reduction factor representing the combined effects of infection prevention and control, community engagement, safe and dignified burial, and contact tracing, applied uniformly from the date of outbreak declaration. The analysis acknowledges that real-world response effectiveness varies by place, time, access, and community acceptance.[^chamla-2026-methods-21]

**Cross-border spillover parameters:** Importation hazard is driven by country-specific travel-while-infectious parameters (Uganda 12%; South Sudan 15%; Rwanda and Burundi 8%) and point-of-entry detection sensitivity from WHO Joint External Evaluation assessments. These parameters were not empirically derived for this outbreak and represent the principal sources of uncertainty in spillover projections.[^chamla-2026-methods-2][^chamla-2026-methods-11]

**No vaccine compartment:** No vaccinated compartment was included, as there is no licensed BDBV-specific vaccine.[^chamla-2026-methods-22]

### Limitations

**Spatial homogeneity:** The model assumes homogeneous mixing across the catchment of seven affected health zones, which oversimplifies spatial heterogeneity across health zones. This is acknowledged as a stated limitation.[^chamla-2026-methods-23][^chamla-2026-discussion-2]

**Single confirmed-case anchor and informal calibration:** The model is anchored on a single recent confirmed-case datum (598 on June 8, 2026), making the fit sensitive to that anchor. Simulation-filtering is not formal inference, and health-care worker transmission was not separately modelled; response intensity was applied uniformly.[^chamla-2026-discussion-2]

**Imprecise mobility estimates:** Informal cross-border mobility estimates are imprecise, and these projections require iterative recalibration as new data emerge.[^chamla-2026-discussion-3]

**Uniform and static response-intensity reduction:** The response-intensity factor was applied uniformly from May 15, but real-world response effectiveness varies by place, time, access, and community acceptance. Live mobility data and behavioural adaptation were not incorporated.[^chamla-2026-discussion-4]

**Confounding of transmission and surveillance intensity:** Surveillance dynamics — particularly the post-declaration case-finding surge — probably contributed to the rapid apparent growth between May 15 and 19. The authors addressed this by lowering the reporting fraction and widening the calibration window, but acknowledge that residual confounding between transmission intensity and surveillance intensity remains.[^chamla-2026-discussion-5]

**Early-phase data quality:** The calibration initially relied on early-phase surveillance counts from the first weekly WHO External Situation Report; the cumulative suspected-case figure of 528 was only partially resolved into 35 laboratory-confirmed cases at the time of analysis, and the associated death count of 132 reflected surveillance signals rather than confirmed mortality alone. Misclassification, delayed laboratory throughput, and ongoing active case finding could each shift the calibration anchor in either direction. The projections should therefore be interpreted as an early situational assessment rather than a definitive forecast.[^chamla-2026-discussion-6]

**Exploratory rather than formally fitted:** These projections should be interpreted as exploratory preparedness-oriented estimates derived from a stochastic scenario-based modelling framework, rather than predictions generated from formally fitted epidemiological models using comprehensive parameter estimation and identifiability analyses.[^chamla-2026-summary-4]

**No sex or gender disaggregation:** Individual-level sex and gender data were not available in the aggregate surveillance reports used for this modelling study; disaggregated analysis by sex or gender was therefore not possible.[^chamla-2026-discussion-7]

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:** Case data were taken from the WHO AFRO External Situation Reports and the WHO AFRO internal tracker through to June 8, 2026. The laboratory-confirmed case series — rising from 33 on May 18, 2026, to 105 on May 24, 2026, and 598 on June 8, 2026 — was used as the primary calibration metric.[^chamla-2026-methods-12] WHO External Situation Report 01 (as of May 18, 2026) documented 528 cumulative suspected cases, 132 deaths, and 35 laboratory-confirmed cases (35 [6.6%] of 528) across DR Congo and Uganda.[^chamla-2026-introduction-1] Uganda reported 19 confirmed cases as of June 4, 2026, including five health-care worker infections and two deaths, used in the spillover model validation.[^chamla-2026-summary-3]

**Auxiliary data:** Historical BDBV outbreak parameters from the 2007 Uganda and 2012 DR Congo outbreaks informed the R₀ range (1.2–2.6) and the three transmissibility scenarios; the distributional choices were designed to span the range of published BDBV estimates from those outbreaks.[^chamla-2026-research-in-context-2][^chamla-2026-methods-13] The latent period was fixed at 9.7 days and the infectious period at 5.0 days, in line with the broader filovirus literature. The case-fatality ratio was drawn from a uniform distribution (0.15–0.25), centred on the observed confirmed-case ratio of approximately 19% (115 of 598 as of June 8, 2026), consistent with the historical BDBV range.[^chamla-2026-methods-14] Travel-while-infectious parameters (Uganda 12%; South Sudan 15%; Rwanda and Burundi 8%) and point-of-entry detection sensitivity from WHO Joint External Evaluation assessments were used in the spillover model.[^chamla-2026-methods-2]

### Overview

To produce early estimates of the epidemic size in the DRC as of May 27, 2026, the analysis conducts scenario-based analyses using two simple independent methods, applying scarce available information alongside plausible assumptions about surveillance, severity, and outbreak growth.[^mccabe-2026-1] The two methods yield broadly consistent results, suggesting that approximately 280–2,520 cases of Bundibugyo virus disease might have occurred in the DRC as of May 27, 2026, though with considerable uncertainty.[^mccabe-2026-2] The broad convergence of estimates from the two independent methods supports the conclusion of potentially substantial under-detection and the potential for wider transmission.[^mccabe-2026-3]

### Estimate

**Back-calculation from deaths** (main scenario, DRC, as of May 27, 2026): Assuming 30% of suspected and confirmed deaths are due to Ebola virus disease, a CFR of 33%, and a doubling time of 10 days yields a mean estimated outbreak size of 451 cases (95% CI 396–511).[^mccabe-2026-15] The estimated outbreak size increases with assumptions of a higher proportion of deaths due to Ebola, a lower CFR, and faster epidemic growth.[^mccabe-2026-16] The full range across scenarios for this method is 306–2,521 cases.[^mccabe-2026-2]

**Travel-based back-calculation** (main scenario, Ituri as source population, DRC, as of May 27, 2026): Assuming Ituri Province as the source population with a doubling time of 10 days yields a mean estimated outbreak size of 945 cases (95% CI 196–2,274).[^mccabe-2026-17] Expanding the source population to include Nord Kivu increases these estimates.[^mccabe-2026-18] The full range across scenarios for this method is 282–1,345 cases.[^mccabe-2026-2]

### Methods

**Back-calculation from deaths:** This method links reported suspected and confirmed deaths — of which a proportion is assumed to be due to Ebola — with estimates of the case fatality ratio (CFR, 33% [95% CI 26–40%]) and the time from symptom onset to death (gamma distribution, mean 11.37 days [SD 5.41]) derived from previous Bundibugyo virus outbreaks.[^mccabe-2026-9] Exponential epidemic growth after a single zoonotic spillover event is assumed.[^mccabe-2026-10] It applies to the DRC nationally, drawing on the total count of 240 suspected or confirmed deaths as of May 27, 2026. **Travel-based back-calculation (geographical spread model):** This method extends the approach described by Imai and colleagues (2020) during the COVID-19 pandemic, combining information on three confirmed imported BVD cases reported in Uganda as of May 27, 2026, estimates of daily international travel volumes from Ituri and Nord Kivu provinces to Uganda, assumptions about epidemic doubling time, and the presumption that all cases in symptomatic travellers will be detected at the time of recovery or death.[^mccabe-2026-12] The main analysis uses the Ituri Province population (n=4,392,200) as the source, with a sensitivity run expanding to Ituri plus Nord Kivu (n=12,593,600).[^mccabe-2026-13] A further sensitivity run assumes fewer travellers, which reduces the epidemic size estimate accordingly.[^mccabe-2026-14]

### Mooring et al. (2026) Modeled Scenario Projections for the Ebola Disease Outbreak Caused by Bundibugyo Virus, 2026

[Read the report](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/75/wr/mm7522e1.htm)

### Overview

This CDC analysis uses a branching process transmission model to project the future trajectory of the 2026 BVD outbreak over a 90-day horizon, estimating how large the outbreak could become under different assumptions about the current number of deaths and different levels of patient isolation. The model was calibrated to three putative cumulative death counts and projected for four possible intervention scenarios ranging from poor (20%) to extremely high (95%) levels of isolation and treatment of symptomatic persons.[^mooring-2026-abstract-1] The central finding is that without high levels of patient isolation, the outbreak is likely to grow very large: with poor isolation levels of patients with BVD (20%) and no other interventions, the likelihood of an outbreak that exceeds 20,000 cases within 3 months is 65%.[^mooring-2026-abstract-2]

### Estimate

This analysis produces forward projections rather than a current true case count estimate; it does not provide a detection multiplier or a point estimate of true cases at the data cutoff. The projections are expressed as probabilities of exceeding case or death thresholds within 90 days of May 24, 2026, under three death-calibration scenarios and four isolation scenarios.

Under the **50 deaths calibration**: assuming 20% of infected persons were successfully isolated beginning May 24, 2026, projections showed ≥20,000 cumulative cases in 65% of simulations, ≥10,000 cumulative cases in 85% of simulations, and ≥4,000 cumulative deaths in 69% of simulations.[^mooring-2026-results-1] At 70% isolation, 94% of simulations projected <10,000 cases and only 1% projected ≥20,000 cases; similarly, 90% of simulations projected <2,000 deaths and only 3% projected ≥4,000 deaths.[^mooring-2026-results-2]

Under the **100 deaths calibration**: very large outbreaks were likely in the scenario in which only 20% of patients were isolated (76% of simulations projected ≥20,000 cases and 87% projected ≥4,000 deaths).[^mooring-2026-results-3] In the scenario in which 70% of infected persons were isolated, 73% of simulations projected <2,000 cumulative deaths by August 22, 2026, and 10% projected ≥4,000 deaths.[^mooring-2026-results-4]

Under the **200 deaths calibration**: even with 70% of infected persons isolated, 42% of simulations projected ≥10,000 cases by August 22, 2026.[^mooring-2026-results-5]

The median basic reproductive number R₀ = 2.51 (IQI = 2.27−2.82) in the main analysis calibrated to 50 deaths.[^mooring-2026-discussion-1]

### Methods

### Epistorm (2026) Ebola Bundibugyo Virus — DRC · Spread Risk Assessment #2

[Read the report](https://epistorm.github.io/EBV2026/report_02_spread_risk.html)

### Overview

This analysis estimates the true size of the 2026 BVD outbreak in DRC by using detected international exportations as observational evidence within an Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC) framework coupled to a global mobility model. The report re-estimates the underlying outbreak size using detected importations within the ABC framework, across three importation-detection-efficiency scenarios (100%, 66%, and 50%, corresponding to 4, 6, and 8 true exported cases given the four observed).[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-1] Across these scenarios, the estimated underlying outbreak size ranges from a posterior median of 1,047 infections (90% CrI: 443–2,043) under 100% detection to 1,944 infections (90% CrI: 1,056–3,224) under 50% detection.[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-2]

### Estimate

Under the most conservative scenario (100% importation detection), the posterior median outbreak size is 1,047 infections (90% CrI: 443–2,043).[^epistorm-2026-size-of-the-epidemic-1] Under the mid-range scenario (66% importation detection), the median is 1,495 infections (90% CrI: 739–2,644).[^epistorm-2026-size-of-the-epidemic-2] Under the most expansive scenario (50% importation detection), the median rises to 1,944 infections (90% CrI: 1,056–3,224).[^epistorm-2026-size-of-the-epidemic-3] All estimates apply to the multi-province source footprint (Ituri, North Kivu, and Kampala) as of 22 May 2026. Across all three scenarios, the lower 5th percentile of the posterior remains well above 400 infections, consistent with the surveillance gaps documented in the WHO situation report and the reported healthcare-worker deaths.[^epistorm-2026-size-of-the-epidemic-4] The confirmed case count at the same cutoff date was 101 laboratory-confirmed cases, implying detection multipliers of approximately 10× at the conservative-scenario median, 15× at the mid-range median, and 19× at the expansive-scenario median relative to confirmed cases — though the analysis does not state these multipliers explicitly.

### Methods

**ABC outbreak-size inference coupled to a mobility model:** The number of infections at the origin is inferred using an Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC) approach. Given the observed international exportations as data, the posterior distribution over outbreak size is approximated by repeatedly simulating outbreaks of varying sizes, generating synthetic exportation events under the mobility model, and retaining parameter values whose simulated outputs are consistent with the observations.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-1] The modeling approach follows the GLEAM (Global Epidemic and Mobility) computational framework, which, for each potential destination, estimates the probability that an infected individual originating in the affected area travels there, conditional on at least one exported case occurring; the output is a relative risk distribution across all candidate destinations.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-2] Global air travel patterns are derived from IATA and OAG passenger-level origin-destination flows, complemented by short-scale commuting patterns capturing daily movements between adjacent subpopulations.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-3] The method is applied to the multi-province source footprint of Ituri, North Kivu, and Kampala. Three importation-detection-efficiency scenarios — 100%, 66%, and 50% — are reported to bound sensitivity to undercounting, corresponding to true export counts of 4, 6, and 8 given the four independent events observed.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-4]

### Assumptions

**Importation-detection efficiency:** The outbreak-size inference is sensitive to the assumed importation-detection-efficiency scenario; actual importation-detection efficiency in Uganda is not directly observed and may vary by port of entry, healthcare-seeking pathway, and clinical presentation.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-1] The three scenarios (100%, 66%, 50%) are not themselves derived from data but are chosen to bracket plausible ranges of undetected exportation.

**Incubation period:** The incubation period of BVD (2–21 days; average ~10 days based on the 2007 Uganda outbreak) conditions the inference, constraining the window within which an exported case could reasonably travel while infectious or pre-symptomatic.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-8] This parameter is fixed rather than varied across scenarios.

**Independence of importation events:** Case importations are modeled as statistically independent events. Real-world events involving multiple related cases traveling together are counted as a single importation event; to the extent that such clusters are common, the model will under-count the number of distinct exposure events while correctly counting the number of distinct geographic seedings.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-2]

**Traveler homogeneity:** The mobility model does not incorporate sociodemographic attributes of travelers (age, economic status, occupation, or pre-existing medical conditions), any of which may modulate both exposure risk at origin and care-seeking behavior at destination. Travel probability within a catchment area is treated as independent of these attributes and of specific location within the catchment.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-3]

**Scope of mobility data:** Mobility data may not fully capture informal cross-border movement, which is substantial in the Ituri–Uganda corridor.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-4]

**No downstream transmission modeled:** The model does not predict downstream transmission chains following importation events; risk estimates reflect the probability of a single importation and do not account for the probability of sustained local transmission in the receiving location. Relative importation risk estimates are conditional on at least one exported case occurring and describe how exportation probability is distributed across destinations given that exportation happens, not the absolute probability that any particular destination will receive a case.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-5]

### Limitations

**Sensitivity to unobserved detection efficiency:** The outbreak-size inference is sensitive to the assumed importation-detection-efficiency scenario, and actual importation-detection efficiency in Uganda is not directly observed and may vary by port of entry, healthcare-seeking pathway, and clinical presentation.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-1] The three scenarios bracket this uncertainty but cannot resolve it.

**Informal cross-border movement not fully captured:** Mobility data may not fully capture informal cross-border movement, which is substantial in the Ituri–Uganda corridor,[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-4] potentially understating the volume of exposure events that could produce exportations.

**Traveler heterogeneity not modelled:** The model does not incorporate sociodemographic attributes of travelers — age, economic status, occupation, or pre-existing medical conditions — any of which may modulate both exposure risk at origin and care-seeking behavior at destination. Travel probability within a catchment area is treated as independent of these attributes and of specific location within the catchment.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-3]

**Cluster travel counted as single importation:** Real-world events involving multiple related cases traveling together are counted as a single importation event. To the extent that such clusters are common, the model will under-count the number of distinct exposure events while correctly counting the number of distinct geographic seedings.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-6]

**No downstream transmission modelled:** The model does not predict downstream transmission chains following importation events: risk estimates reflect the probability of a single importation and do not account for the probability of sustained local transmission in the receiving location.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-7]

**Posterior intervals will remain wide until more exports are observed:** As additional exported cases are confirmed, posterior intervals will tighten and the most consistent ascertainment regime will become identifiable,[^epistorm-2026-size-of-the-epidemic-6] implying that the current wide credible intervals are a direct consequence of the small number of observed exportations.

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:** As of 22 May 2026, DRC had reported 101 laboratory-confirmed cases, 904 suspected cases, and 119 suspected deaths across health zones in Ituri (Bunia, Rwampara, Nyankunde, Mongbwalu) and North Kivu provinces.[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-3] Four independent importation events constitute the primary observational evidence for the ABC inference: three to Kampala (two travelers detected on 15–16 May and one retrospective case traced to 14 May travel) and one to Bukavu, South Kivu; two additional Kampala cases are local contacts of travelers and do not constitute independent importations.[^epistorm-2026-size-of-the-epidemic-5]

**Auxiliary data:** Global air travel patterns are drawn from IATA and OAG, providing passenger-level origin-destination flows between international airports.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-5] These are complemented by short-scale commuting patterns capturing daily movements between adjacent subpopulations.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-6] Two parameters condition the ABC inference: the incubation period of BVD (2–21 days; average ~10 days), drawn from the 2007 Uganda outbreak.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-7] The confirmed CFR of ~14% (DRC/Uganda combined) is cited as a pathogen characteristic, though it is not a direct input to the outbreak-size inference.[^epistorm-2026-front-matter-2]
