# Outbreak Size

:::{hint} Key findings
Latest direct joint-model estimate is 2,778–14,014 cumulative infections in DRC as of 14 June 2026 (90% credible interval), versus 808 laboratory-confirmed cases at the cutoff; earlier May back-calculations ranged from a few hundred to about 3,200 infections or cases depending on scope and export detection. The evidence base comprises six analyses from six groups, with partial independence and convergence on material under-detection but not on magnitude. Evidence suggests response planning should assume a substantially larger outbreak than confirmed counts indicate, prioritising eastern DRC and border preparedness; confidence low-to-moderate.

## Synthesizing evidence

### State of the evidence

The matrix contains six rapid analyses dated 22 May to 17 June 2026. Four directly estimate hidden burden using exportation, deaths, or joint renewal modelling; CDC/Mooring is mainly a 90-day branching-process projection, and Chamla models confirmed-case trajectories and spillover rather than true under-detected size.[^mooring-2026-abstract-1][^chamla-2026-summary-1] The evidence is growing rather than stable, because later estimates use later surveillance streams and cannot be read as simple growth alone.[^abbott-2026-front-matter-2][^abbott-2026-results-8]

### How the estimates compare

The direct May estimates all indicate under-detection but span different quantities and geographies: BlueDot estimated 359 cases, rising to 718 under export under-ascertainment sensitivity; Epistorm estimated posterior medians of 1,047–1,944 infections; and McCabe estimated about 280–2,520 cases as of 27 May.[^bluedot-2026-key-takeaways-1][^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-1][^mccabe-2026-2] Abbott’s later joint model is higher at 2,778–14,014 infections as of 14 June, or 3.4–17.3 times the 808 confirmed cases.[^abbott-2026-results-3][^abbott-2026-results-4] The divergence is mainly structural: export-only models are driven by a handful of detected travellers, death back-calculation depends on CFR and death attribution, and Abbott fits multiple surveillance streams jointly.

### Key uncertainties and assumptions

The highest-leverage shared uncertainties are export detection, cross-border mobility, geographic source population, death attribution, CFR, delay distributions, and the use of historical BDBV or broader filovirus parameters. Several analyses also rely on aggregate situation-report totals rather than onset-dated line lists, so epidemic timing is inferred through assumed delays rather than observed directly.[^abbott-2026-limitations-4][^abbott-2026-limitations-5] If traveller risk differs from resident risk, informal mobility is missed, or ascertainment is substantially lower than assumed, estimates would shift together rather than cancel out.[^bluedot-2026-key-takeaways-2][^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-2]

### What would reduce uncertainty

The most valuable additions would be onset-dated line lists, resolved case classifications, deaths linked to laboratory status, updated export counts, and genomic estimates of outbreak age. McCabe explicitly identifies epidemiological time series and genomic data as crucial, while Epistorm notes that additional exported cases would tighten posterior intervals and help identify the most consistent ascertainment scenario.[^mccabe-2026-26][^epistorm-2026-size-of-the-epidemic-4] Independent replication of the joint multi-stream model against cleaner surveillance extracts would also clarify whether streams imply compatible outbreak sizes.

### What this means for the response

The evidence suggests confirmed counts are a lower bound, not a planning denominator, and that under-detection is operationally large even under conservative assumptions. Geographic targeting should remain centred on Ituri and neighbouring eastern DRC provinces while maintaining Uganda-facing preparedness; Epistorm and Chamla also point to regional spillover concerns, especially Uganda and South Sudan.[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-4][^chamla-2026-research-in-context-3] Abbott’s bed-demand analysis adds that national averages can understate local unmet need, particularly where the epicentre is near saturation.[^abbott-2026-methods-24]

### Abbott et al. (2026) Estimating the current size of the 2026 DRC Bundibugyo virus outbreak

[Read the report](https://epiforecasts.io/BVDOutbreakSize/dev/analysis)

### Overview

This analysis estimates the true (infection-level) size of the ongoing 2026 BVD outbreak in the DRC, accounting for under-detection, using a real-time joint Bayesian discrete-time renewal model fitted simultaneously to multiple surveillance data streams from the DRC and Uganda. It estimates the latent infections, symptom onsets and deaths over time, the reported and confirmed cases, and the time-varying reproduction number with its growth rate and doubling time, alongside the case-fatality ratio, the ascertainment of each surveillance system, and a short-term forecast of each stream over the coming week.[^abbott-2026-front-matter-1] The headline finding is that the outbreak is estimated to have caused 4,662–6,932 infections (30% CrI) to date, reported and unreported, which is roughly 3.4–17.3× the 808 laboratory-confirmed cases recorded by the data cutoff, indicating that confirmed cases capture only a small share of the outbreak.[^abbott-2026-results-1]

### Estimate

**Headline (joint model, data as of 14 June 2026):** The outbreak is estimated to have caused 30% CrI 4,662–6,932, 60% CrI 3,785–9,133, 90% CrI 2,778–14,014 infections to date, reported and unreported.[^abbott-2026-results-3] Against the 808 laboratory-confirmed cases by the cutoff, that is roughly 3.4–17.3× as many infections, so confirmed cases are estimated to capture only a small share of the outbreak.[^abbott-2026-results-4]

**Sensitivity — onset-to-death delay (hospital vs community pathway):** Under the baseline hospital pathway, the 90% CrI is 2,778–14,014 infections; under the community pathway (shorter delay, implied mean about 8 days), the 90% CrI is 2,724–11,517 infections.[^abbott-2026-results-5]

**Sensitivity — molecular clock rate:** Under the baseline clock rate (1.2×10⁻³ substitutions/site/year), the 90% CrI is 2,778–14,014 infections; under the faster clock rate (1.9×10⁻³ substitutions/site/year), the 90% CrI is 2,511–11,261 infections.[^abbott-2026-results-6]

**Per-stream single-model estimates (90% CrI):** Exports alone: 790–11,335; DRC deaths alone: 2,103–10,633; DRC cases alone: 5,542–16,317; DRC confirmed cases alone: 7,184–26,512; DRC isolation: 3,049–34,951; joint: 2,778–14,014.[^abbott-2026-results-7]

**Frozen estimates at earlier cutoffs (90% CrI):** Frozen 20 May: 478–1,866; frozen 23 May: 585–2,728; frozen 27 May: 379–1,970; current data (14 June): 2,778–14,014.[^abbott-2026-results-8]

### Geography

**Primary scope:** The primary estimate covers the DRC outbreak, with cases also detected across the border in Uganda.[^abbott-2026-front-matter-1] The DRC estimate is national in scope. The model inherits a geographic split between Ituri and Nord Kivu, with no further spatial structure.[^abbott-2026-limitations-1]

**Sensitivity scope:** No additional geographic sensitivity analyses are explicitly described beyond the Ituri/Nord Kivu acknowledgment. The Uganda-side estimate is for exported cases only, not a separate DRC-equivalent outbreak size estimate for Uganda.

### Method

**Discrete-time renewal process (joint Bayesian):** The model is a discrete-time renewal process on a daily grid that fits the surveillance streams jointly in a single posterior: the DRC suspected cases, suspected deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and confirmed deaths, and the cases and deaths exported to Uganda.[^abbott-2026-front-matter-1] The generating infection process produces daily infection incidence via the discrete renewal equation $I_t = R_t \sum_{s=1}^{L} I_{t-s} g_s$, where $g$ is the discretised generation-interval PMF and $R_t$ is the per-day reproduction number; each data stream observes a thinned, delayed or transformed view of the same latent incidence.[^abbott-2026-methods-1] The model is in the class of EpiNow2-style time-varying renewal models, extended here to fit all streams jointly rather than in a pipeline. This work began as a replication of the McCabe et al. report and has since evolved into a real-time joint Bayesian estimate of the current outbreak size, a discrete-time renewal process with a time-varying reproduction number fitted to more of the available data streams than the original.[^abbott-2026-origins-of-this-work-1]

**Per-stream single-model fits (auxiliary):** Alongside the joint model, single-stream models are written for each count-based stream (exported cases, suspected deaths, suspected cases, laboratory-confirmed cases, confirmed deaths and deaths among exports), so each stream's posterior over the outbreak size can be compared with the joint.[^abbott-2026-methods-2]

### Assumptions

The outbreak is assumed to have started from a single seed case introduced by zoonotic spillover.[^abbott-2026-methods-17]

The outbreak is assumed to have grown deterministically through an unobserved cryptic exponential phase, doubling $m$ times before sustained transmission was established.[^abbott-2026-methods-18] The authors acknowledge that the cryptic phase and growth rate are only weakly identified by the data.

The molecular clock rate is fixed to the $1.2\times10^{-3}$ substitutions/site/year rate of the 2013–2016 West African Ebola epidemic, and the genetic TMRCA is treated as a lower bound on outbreak age.[^abbott-2026-methods-19] The authors note this propagates neither cross-outbreak nor clock uncertainty.

The model inherits a single zoonotic seed, an assumed generation interval, no spatial structure beyond the Ituri/Nord Kivu split, and no depletion of susceptibles.[^abbott-2026-limitations-1]

The ascertainment hyperprior is centred on 75%, reflecting the active case-finding of a declared Ebola response rather than baseline passive surveillance.[^abbott-2026-methods-20] If true ascertainment is systematically higher or lower, the estimated outbreak size shifts accordingly.

The onset-to-report delay is assumed to correspond to the onset-to-admission delay from the Isiro 2012 line list, not the longer onset-to-notification delay (mean ~20 days), on the assumption that the latter reflects a longer administrative pathway rather than the rapid surveillance report modelled here.[^abbott-2026-methods-21]

The outbreak response is modelled as a logistic ramp of about three weeks beginning at the first WHO situation report on 18 May 2026, and the effect is constrained to reduce (not increase) transmission.[^abbott-2026-methods-22] With only a few sitreps straddling the ramp, the ramp effect and the pre-ramp reproduction number are not well separated.[^abbott-2026-limitations-2]

Surveillance streams are assumed to be conditionally independent given latent incidence.[^abbott-2026-methods-23] The authors note this may understate uncertainty because streams observe overlapping people.

### Limitations

**Stated by authors:**

Nearly all of the delays, the case-fatality ratio and the laboratory assumptions are set by priors informed at best by a handful of literature sources, often from other outbreaks, and in places by prior judgement rather than anything from this outbreak; the data do little to move them, so posteriors largely track their priors.[^abbott-2026-limitations-3]

The analysis has no counts by symptom onset or any other epidemiologically relevant date, only cumulative totals at the report date; the timing of the underlying epidemic is therefore weakly identified and is recovered only through the assumed delays.[^abbott-2026-limitations-4]

The DRC data are situation-report totals of aggregate counts; there is no line list or information on case definitions or reporting completeness, so every estimate is a model-based extrapolation under strong assumptions, not a measurement.[^abbott-2026-limitations-5]

Later situation reports can revise earlier figures up or down as suspects are reclassified and newly-reporting health zones are added, and ascertainment probably rose over the window; the revision process is not modelled.[^abbott-2026-limitations-6]

The streams are fitted as conditionally independent given latent incidence but observe overlapping people, which can understate uncertainty; the analysis has not checked whether the streams imply conflicting outbreak sizes.[^abbott-2026-limitations-7]

The bed model is a single national model and cannot represent local saturation: on 13 June, Ituri was at 93.9% occupancy while Sud-Kivu was at 21.9%, so the national shortfall understates local unmet need.[^abbott-2026-methods-24]

The model code, priors and analysis were drafted by a language model from the McCabe et al. report and the companion delay reanalysis, then reviewed and revised, and have not been independently replicated against the authors' code.[^abbott-2026-limitations-8]

**Additional observations (inferred):**

The prior on ascertainment is centred on 75% with reference to an active Ebola response context. This prior reflects active case-finding of a declared Ebola response rather than baseline passive surveillance[^abbott-2026-methods-20]; if early response capacity was lower than this prior implies, the estimated outbreak size would shift upward.

The molecular clock rate is fixed to the West African EBOV epidemic rate without propagating cross-outbreak or clock uncertainty[^abbott-2026-methods-19]; the sensitivity analysis shows this choice materially affects the outbreak age and infection count, suggesting the fixed-clock assumption is a meaningful constraint on the estimate.

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:**

DRC counts come from the situation reports of the Institut National de Santé Publique (INSP); each report gives the national cumulative suspected cases and deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and deaths, and specimens received and analysed. Figures were extracted from PDF situation reports archived by INRB-UMIE using a language model with a second pass to re-read them.[^abbott-2026-front-matter-3][^abbott-2026-methods-11]

As of the latest stable vintage: 246 suspected deaths (as of 26 May 2026), 1,077 suspected cases (as of 26 May 2026), 808 confirmed cases (as of 14 June 2026), and 192 confirmed deaths (as of 14 June 2026).[^abbott-2026-methods-12]

The Uganda data are the cases and the one death exported across the border, taken from the WHO situation reports and Disease Outbreak News.[^abbott-2026-methods-11] Three exported cases (as of 23 May 2026) and one export death (as of 14 May 2026).[^abbott-2026-methods-13]

A genetic TMRCA bound of 25 March 2026 is used, derived from a BEAST time tree of the first ten sequenced genomes.[^abbott-2026-methods-14]

**Auxiliary data:**

The cross-border traveller volume and source population come from McCabe et al.; the source population is fixed and the traveller volume is given a Normal prior around the McCabe et al. figure of 1,871 daily outbound travellers.[^abbott-2026-methods-11]

The onset-to-report (onset-to-admission) delay is from a Bayesian reanalysis (Funk and Abbott, 2026) of the 2012 Isiro Bundibugyo virus outbreak line list (Rosello et al., 2015).[^abbott-2026-methods-15]

The generation interval prior is from the WHO Ebola Response Team 2014 (mean 15.3 days, SD 9.3 days, EVD serial interval used as generation-time proxy).[^abbott-2026-methods-3]

The incubation period is from MacNeil et al. 2010, based on the 2007 Uganda BVD outbreak ($n=24$).[^abbott-2026-methods-4]

The CFR prior is informed by the CDC summary of the two previous BVD outbreaks (55 deaths in 169 cases, ~33%) and the companion BDBV reanalysis.[^abbott-2026-methods-16]

### Chamla et al. (2026) Size of the 2026 Ebola outbreak and risk of cross-border spillover from Bundibugyo virus in Ituri Province, DR Congo, and its implications for preparedness: a recalibrated stochastic modelling study

[Read the report](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/laninf/article/PIIS1473-3099(26)00320-8/fulltext)

### Overview

This analysis combines epidemic modelling with spillover estimation to quantify regional risks associated with the 2026 Ituri BDBV outbreak, using laboratory-confirmed case data from WHO Situation Reports (33 cases as of May 18, 2026, to 598 cases as of June 8, 2026), with projections extending 12 weeks from May 18 to August 10, 2026.[^chamla-2026-research-in-context-1] A stochastic SEIRD ensemble model was calibrated to the laboratory-confirmed case series, anchoring on 598 cumulative confirmed cases on June 8, 2026, with a linked daily-hazard spillover model estimating importation probability for Uganda, South Sudan, Rwanda, and Burundi over a 12-week horizon.[^chamla-2026-summary-1] Under the central scenario (R₀ = 1.71), the ensemble projected a median of 990 cumulative confirmed cases by week 12 (90% PI 709–1293) and 174 deaths, while cross-border spillover into Uganda had already been established with 94.2% importation probability, and South Sudan faced a 69.3% importation probability.[^chamla-2026-summary-2]

---

### Estimate

Under the central scenario (mean accepted R₀ = 1.71), the ensemble projected a median of 648 cumulative confirmed cases by week 10 (June 8, 2026; 50% PI 560–732), rising to 990 by week 12 (June 24, 2026; 50% PI 858–1141 and 90% PI 709–1293), with a projected median of 174 deaths at week 12. By week 24, the central scenario projected a median of 8,210 cumulative confirmed cases (50% PI 6,636–10,287 and 90% PI 5,063–13,498) and approximately 1,440 deaths if transmission were merely sustained.[^chamla-2026-results-1]

Under the low-transmissibility scenario (mean accepted R₀ = 1.42), cumulative confirmed cases reached a median of 870 by week 12 (50% PI 752–1,004 and 90% PI 641–1,133; 160 deaths) and 3,662 by week 24 (50% PI 2,884–4,484 and 90% PI 2,094–6,037; 673 deaths). Under the high-transmissibility scenario (mean accepted R₀ = 2.08), confirmed cases reached a median of 1,364 (50% PI 1,154–1,579 and 90% PI 975–1,807; 202 deaths) by week 12, rising to 66,420 (50% PI 56,560–78,937 and 90% PI 41,718–95,881; 10,644 deaths) by week 24.[^chamla-2026-results-2]

Because the observed confirmed-case trajectory is consistent with the central scenario, the high scenario is not the operative trajectory; it is retained as an upper-bound contingency should control measures lapse.[^chamla-2026-results-2] The analysis does not separately report an estimate of true outbreak size after accounting for under-detection; its projections are anchored on laboratory-confirmed cases with a reporting fraction of 1.0.

### Method

**Stochastic compartmental model (SEIRD):** The analysis used a discrete-time SEIRD (susceptible, exposed, infectious, recovered, and dead) framework, recalibrated to cumulative reported cases in Ituri Province on May 18, 2026 (day 48) and projected forward over a 24-week horizon to September 16, 2026.[^chamla-2026-methods-5] Three transmissibility scenarios (low R₀ = 1.42; central R₀ = 1.71; high R₀ = 2.08) spanned published BDBV estimates, and calibration was performed by simulation-filtering (approximate rejection sampling, not formal Bayesian inference), accepting replicates where cumulative laboratory-confirmed cases at day 68 fell within ±30% of 598 (range 419–777), retaining 400 calibration-passing replicates per scenario.[^chamla-2026-methods-6]

**Daily-hazard spillover model (travel-based):** Importation risk to Uganda, South Sudan, Rwanda, and Burundi was modelled as a daily Poisson hazard driven by calibrated infectious prevalence, country-specific cross-border mobility, point of entry detection sensitivity from WHO Joint External Evaluation assessments, and travel-while-infectious parameters (Uganda 12%; South Sudan 15%; Rwanda and Burundi 8%).[^chamla-2026-methods-3]

---

### Assumptions

**Reporting fraction = 1.0 for laboratory-confirmed cases:** Because the analysis is anchored on laboratory-confirmed cases, the reporting fraction was set at 1.0 (laboratory-confirmed cases are, by definition, ascertained); the earlier suspected-case reporting fraction assumption is no longer required.[^chamla-2026-methods-7] This assumption means the model treats the confirmed case series as fully observed — it does not estimate an additional layer of undetected cases beyond what is laboratory-confirmed.

**Historical BDBV parameters apply to the current outbreak:** The R₀ distributions were chosen to span the range of published BDBV estimates from the 2007 Uganda and 2012 DR Congo outbreaks, and the CFR was drawn from a uniform distribution centred on the observed confirmed-case ratio of approximately 19% and consistent with the historical BDBV range.[^chamla-2026-methods-7]

**Homogeneous mixing within catchment:** The model assumes homogeneous mixing within a catchment of 350,000 across seven affected health zones, including Ituri and North Kivu; spatial heterogeneity is not captured and is noted as a stated limitation.[^chamla-2026-methods-2]

**Uniform response-intensity reduction from declaration date:** The transmission reduction factor was applied uniformly from May 15, 2026; the analysis acknowledges that real-world response effectiveness varies by place, time, access, and community acceptance, and that live mobility data and behavioural adaptation were not incorporated.[^chamla-2026-methods-8][^chamla-2026-discussion-2]

**Travel-while-infectious parameters are representative:** The travel-while-infectious parameters (Uganda 12%; South Sudan 15%; Rwanda and Burundi 8%) were not empirically derived for this outbreak and represent the principal sources of uncertainty in spillover projections.[^chamla-2026-methods-3]

**The June 8 confirmed-case anchor is reliable:** The model is anchored on a single recent confirmed-case datum (598 on June 8, 2026), so the fit is sensitive to that anchor; misclassification, delayed laboratory throughput, and ongoing active case finding could each shift the calibration anchor in either direction.[^chamla-2026-discussion-2]

### Limitations

**Stated by authors:**

Homogeneous mixing oversimplifies spatial heterogeneity across health zones; health-care worker transmission was not separately modelled; response intensity was applied uniformly; the model is anchored on a single recent confirmed-case datum (598 on June 8, 2026), making the fit sensitive to that anchor; simulation-filtering is not formal inference; and informal cross-border mobility estimates are imprecise.[^chamla-2026-discussion-2]

The response-intensity factor was applied uniformly from May 15, but real-world response effectiveness varies by place, time, access, and community acceptance. Live mobility data and behavioural adaptation were not incorporated. Surveillance dynamics — particularly the post-declaration case-finding surge — probably contributed to the rapid apparent growth between May 15 and 19; residual confounding between transmission and surveillance intensity remains. Most importantly, the calibration relied on early-phase surveillance counts, with the cumulative suspected-case figure of 528 only partially resolved into 35 laboratory-confirmed cases at the time of initial analysis, and the associated death count of 132 reflected surveillance signals rather than laboratory-confirmed mortality alone.[^chamla-2026-discussion-2]

Individual-level sex and gender data were not available in the aggregate surveillance reports, so disaggregated analysis by sex or gender was not possible. Race and ethnicity data were not collected.[^chamla-2026-discussion-2]

These projections should be interpreted as exploratory preparedness-oriented estimates derived from a stochastic scenario-based modelling framework, rather than predictions generated from formally fitted epidemiological models using comprehensive parameter estimation and identifiability analyses.[^chamla-2026-summary-3]

**Additional observations (inferred):**

The analysis sets the reporting fraction at 1.0 and anchors on laboratory-confirmed cases, treating confirmed counts as fully ascertained. This means the model does not estimate undetected cases beyond the confirmed series — a consequential design choice for the question of true outbreak size. A 6-week interval between the estimated index case and laboratory confirmation allowed transmission chains to compound silently before any response was initiated,[^chamla-2026-introduction-2] which implies that some cases from the pre-declaration period may never have been confirmed or captured in the calibration anchor, leaving a potential floor of undetected cases that the model does not explicitly quantify. This is a direct implication of the 6-week pre-declaration interval the authors themselves describe, but the authors do not characterise it as a limitation on their outbreak size estimate.

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:** Case data were taken from the WHO AFRO External Situation Reports and the WHO AFRO internal tracker through to June 8, 2026; the laboratory-confirmed case series was used as the primary calibration metric, with confirmed cases rising from 33 on May 18, 2026, to 105 on May 24, 2026, and 598 on June 8, 2026.[^chamla-2026-methods-1] WHO External Situation Report 01 (as of May 18, 2026) documented 528 cumulative suspected cases, 132 deaths, and 35 laboratory-confirmed cases across DR Congo and Uganda.[^chamla-2026-introduction-1] As of June 4, 2026, Uganda reported 19 confirmed cases, including five health-care worker infections and two deaths.[^chamla-2026-results-3]

**Auxiliary data:** R₀ values were drawn from published BDBV estimates from the 2007 Uganda and 2012 DR Congo outbreaks; the latent period (9.7 days) and infectious period (5.0 days) were taken from the broader filovirus literature.[^chamla-2026-methods-7] Point of entry detection sensitivity parameters were sourced from WHO Joint External Evaluation assessments; travel-while-infectious parameters were not empirically derived for this outbreak.[^chamla-2026-methods-3] A PubMed and WHO Disease Outbreak News search (conducted May 19, 2026) identified prior outbreak-modelling studies, with previous outbreaks suggesting R₀ estimates ranging from 1.2 to 2.6.[^chamla-2026-research-in-context-2]

### McCabe et al. (2026) Estimation of the Ebola outbreak size in the Democratic Republic of the Congo

[Read the report](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/laninf/article/PIIS1473-3099(26)00299-9/fulltext)

### Overview

This analysis produces early estimates of the epidemic size in the Democratic Republic of the Congo as of May 27, 2026, using two independent, scenario-based methods with available information and plausible assumptions about surveillance, severity, and epidemic growth.[^mccabe-2026-1] The first method back-calculates cases from reported deaths combined with a case fatality ratio and onset-to-death distribution; the second estimates the source-population outbreak size from confirmed cases imported into Uganda using travel volumes. The two methods yield broadly consistent results suggesting that approximately 280–2,520 cases of Bundibugyo virus disease might have occurred in the DRC as of May 27, 2026, with considerable uncertainty around these estimates, supporting a conclusion of potentially substantial underdetection.[^mccabe-2026-2]

---

### Estimate

**Method 1 (back-calculation from deaths):** Assuming 30% of suspected and confirmed deaths are due to Ebola virus disease, a CFR of 33%, and a doubling time of 10 days, the mean estimated outbreak size is 451 cases (95% CI 396–511) as of May 27, 2026.[^mccabe-2026-6] Across all CFR and growth-rate scenarios, estimates range from 306 (95% CI 269–346; slow growth, CFR 40%) to 756 (95% CI 664–855; fast growth, CFR 26%).[^mccabe-2026-10]

**Method 2 (geographic spread model):** Assuming Ituri Province as the source population with a doubling time of 10 days, the mean estimated outbreak size is 945 cases (95% CI 196–2,274).[^mccabe-2026-5] Across growth-rate scenarios with Ituri as the source, estimates range from 847 (95% CI 176–2,037; slow growth) to 1,100 (95% CI 228–2,647; fast growth). Expanding to Ituri plus Nord Kivu yields 1,042 (95% CI 216–2,508) to 1,354 (95% CI 281–3,260) across the same growth scenarios.[^mccabe-2026-11]

**Cross-method synthesis:** The two methods together suggest approximately 280–2,520 cases of Bundibugyo virus disease might have occurred in the DRC as of May 27, 2026 (estimate range 306–2,521 with the back-calculation model and 282–1,345 with the geographic spread model), with considerable uncertainty.[^mccabe-2026-2]

---

### Geography

The primary scope of Method 2 (geographic spread model) uses Ituri Province as the source population.[^mccabe-2026-5] Expanding the source population to include Nord Kivu Province is explored as a sensitivity analysis for Method 2, though findings from this expansion should be interpreted with caution.[^mccabe-2026-5] Method 1 (back-calculation from deaths) draws on suspected and confirmed deaths reported from across the DRC outbreak area.[^mccabe-2026-6] Both methods presume that transmission is largely concentrated in Ituri and Nord Kivu provinces.[^mccabe-2026-7] Uganda is referenced as the destination for exported cases used as the input to Method 2, but the analysis does not estimate outbreak size within Uganda itself.

---

### Method

**Method 1 — Back-calculation from deaths:** This method uses an analytical framework linking reported suspected and confirmed deaths — assuming a proportion are attributable to Ebola — with estimates of the case fatality ratio (33%, 95% CI 26–40%) and the time from symptom onset to death (gamma distribution, mean 11.37 days, SD 5.41 days) derived from previous Bundibugyo virus outbreaks, and further assumes exponential epidemic growth after a single zoonotic spillover event.[^mccabe-2026-6]

**Method 2 — Geographic spread / travel-based back-calculation:** This method extends the approach described by Imai and colleagues (2020) during the COVID-19 pandemic, combining information on three confirmed imported Bundibugyo virus disease cases reported in Uganda with estimates of daily international travel volumes from Ituri and Nord Kivu provinces to Uganda, assumptions about epidemic doubling time, and the presumption that all cases in symptomatic travellers will be detected at the time of recovery or death.[^mccabe-2026-5]

---

### Assumptions

**Transmission is largely concentrated in Ituri and Nord Kivu provinces.** The analysis treats these two provinces as the primary source population. Violation of this assumption — for example, if substantial transmission is occurring elsewhere — would affect both methods' estimates.[^mccabe-2026-7]

**Travel patterns to Uganda are accurately captured in point-of-entry data.** The geographic spread model relies on the assumption that scale and patterns of population movement from these provinces to Uganda are correctly reflected in the data used.[^mccabe-2026-7]

**The proportion of deaths attributable to Ebola virus disease is between 30% and 100%.** In the main scenario, only 30% of suspected and confirmed deaths are assumed to be truly due to Ebola; this proportion materially affects the back-calculation estimate.[^mccabe-2026-6]

**CFR and onset-to-death interval from prior Bundibugyo outbreaks apply to the current outbreak.** Historical parameter estimates are assumed to be transferable, though the analysis acknowledges each of these assumptions carries substantial uncertainty.[^mccabe-2026-7]

**Epidemic growth rate is between 7 and 14 days doubling time**, loosely based on early genomic analyses; this assumption drives the spread of estimates across scenarios.

**The imported Uganda cases represent routine travel** (i.e., that symptomatic travellers would be detected at recovery or death). <cite index="1-4">The analysis explicitly acknowledges that this assumption may be violated because at least two of the three imported cases reportedly travelled to Uganda specifically to seek medical care, and findings from the expanded Nord Kivu source-population scenario should be interpreted with caution for this reason.[^mccabe-2026-7]

---

### Limitations

**Stated by authors:**

The estimates carry considerable uncertainty, reflected in wide confidence intervals — particularly for Method 2, where the main scenario spans 95% CI 196–2,274 cases from a numerator of only three imported cases.[^mccabe-2026-2]

The routine-travel assumption underlying Method 2 is likely violated: at least two of the three imported Uganda cases reportedly travelled specifically to seek medical care, which the authors acknowledge requires cautious interpretation of those estimates.[^mccabe-2026-5]

Each key assumption — proportion of deaths due to Ebola, transferability of historical CFR and onset-to-death parameters, accuracy of travel data, and epidemic growth rate — carries substantial uncertainty that may influence the estimates.[^mccabe-2026-7]

The analysis is explicitly described as an early assessment primarily conducted to inform operational planning, and the authors note that estimates may change as more data and information become available.[^mccabe-2026-7]

**Additional observations (inferred):**

The acknowledged violation of the routine-travel assumption in Method 2 has a directional consequence that the authors do not fully quantify: if cases were travelling to Uganda specifically for medical care rather than for routine purposes, the effective detection probability among travellers is higher than among the general case population, which would cause Method 2 to underestimate the true outbreak size (since symptomatic healthcare-seeking travellers are more likely to be detected than the average case in DRC). The authors note that "assuming fewer travellers reduces the epidemic size accordingly," but the direction of bias from healthcare-seeking travel is not resolved.[^mccabe-2026-5]

Suspected cases and deaths are no longer reported in recent situation reports, and deaths are harder to retrospectively assess with investigations ongoing — this data-completeness constraint directly limits the reliability of the death-based inputs to Method 1.[^mccabe-2026-4]

---

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:**

Reported suspected and confirmed cases (1,031) and deaths (240) from 14 health zones across three provinces (Ituri, Nord Kivu, and Sud Kivu) in the DRC, as of May 27, 2026; updated confirmed case and death counts (381 confirmed cases, 64 confirmed deaths) as of June 3, 2026.[^mccabe-2026-4]

Estimates of daily international travel volumes from Ituri and Nord Kivu provinces to Uganda (point-of-entry data), used as inputs to Method 2.[^mccabe-2026-5]

Method 2 extends the framework described by Imai and colleagues (2020), developed during the COVID-19 pandemic.[^mccabe-2026-5]

Epidemic doubling time loosely based on early genomic analyses of the current outbreak.[^mccabe-2026-7]

:::{tab-item} Mooring et al. (24 May 2026)
### Mooring et al. (2026) Modeled Scenario Projections for the Ebola Disease Outbreak Caused by Bundibugyo Virus, 2026

[Read the report](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/75/wr/mm7522e1.htm)

### Overview

CDC simulated scenario projections for the ongoing BVD outbreak to understand possible future morbidity and mortality, using a branching process model calibrated to three putative cumulative death counts and projected under four intervention scenarios ranging from poor (20%) to extremely high (95%) isolation and treatment of symptomatic persons.[^mooring-2026-abstract-1] The analysis is primarily forward-looking — projecting outbreak trajectories over a 90-day horizon — rather than estimating a current "true size," though calibration to different death counts implicitly encodes uncertainty about present outbreak magnitude. With poor isolation levels (20%) and no other interventions, the likelihood of an outbreak exceeding 20,000 cases within 3 months is 65%; if a high proportion of patients (70%) were to enter isolation, only a one-in-20 chance is projected for an outbreak with ≥10,000 cases within 3 months.[^mooring-2026-abstract-1]

### Estimate

This analysis is primarily a forward projection, not a back-calculation of current true size. The estimates below are projected cumulative case and death counts over 90 days from May 24 to August 22, 2026, under different calibration and intervention scenarios.

**Calibrated to 50 deaths (primary/headline scenario):** Assuming 20% isolation, projections showed ≥20,000 cumulative cases in 65% of simulations, ≥10,000 cumulative cases in 85% of simulations, and ≥4,000 cumulative deaths in 69% of simulations. At 50% isolation, 17% of simulations projected ≥20,000 cases and 22% projected ≥4,000 deaths. At 70% isolation, 94% of simulations projected <10,000 cases and only 1% projected ≥20,000 cases; 90% projected <2,000 deaths and only 3% projected ≥4,000 deaths.[^mooring-2026-results-1]

**Calibrated to 100 deaths:** With 20% isolation, 76% of simulations projected ≥20,000 cases and 87% projected ≥4,000 deaths. At 70% isolation, 73% of simulations projected <2,000 cumulative deaths by August 22, 2026, and 10% projected ≥4,000 deaths.[^mooring-2026-results-2]

**Calibrated to 200 deaths:** Even with 70% of infected persons isolated, 42% of simulations projected ≥10,000 cases by August 22, 2026.[^mooring-2026-results-3]

**Effect of R₀:** Simulated outbreaks with R₀ values higher than the median R₀ typically reached ≥10,000 cumulative cases and ≥2,000 cumulative deaths by August 22, 2026, in scenarios with ≤50% isolation, even assuming only 50 cumulative deaths by May 24.[^mooring-2026-results-4]

**Inferred spillover dates** (implicit indicator of current outbreak size): The 50-death calibration estimated a spillover on approximately February 19, 2026 (IQI = February 1–March 8); the 100-death calibration inferred a median spillover date of February 8, 2026 (IQI = January 21–February 27); the 200-death calibration inferred a median spillover date of January 29, 2026 (IQI = January 9–February 18).[^mooring-2026-results-5]

### Geography

**Primary scope:** The outbreak is centered in the Ituri province of the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC).[^mooring-2026-front-matter-3] As of June 2, 2026, a total of 378 confirmed cases (363 in DRC and 15 in Uganda) and 63 confirmed deaths (62 in DRC and one in Uganda) have been recorded.[^mooring-2026-introduction-1] The model is not explicitly scoped to a sub-national administrative unit within DRC; it is calibrated to DRC-wide (or outbreak-wide) death counts from situation reports. **Sensitivity scope:** The analysis does not define distinct geographic sensitivity scenarios (e.g., Ituri only versus Ituri plus Nord Kivu). Uganda is referenced as part of the ongoing outbreak context but is not modeled separately; the branching process treats the outbreak as a single entity initialized from one spillover event.

### Method

**Method family:** Branching process (stochastic transmission model). Each simulated outbreak was initialized with one infected person representing the spillover event; this person infected a randomly generated number of additional persons based on assumptions about R₀. Any infected persons were added to the simulation at times selected according to the distribution of intervals from one infection to the next and, in turn, were able to cause further infections. The simulation continued until either none of the infected persons in a generation caused any secondary infections (indicating termination) or the simulation reached 5,000 deaths (indicating a very large, exponentially growing outbreak).[^mooring-2026-methods-2]

**Calibration approach:** A simulated outbreak was considered compatible with the real-world outbreak if it reached the assumed number of cumulative deaths by May 24, 2026, and if the first death occurred on or before April 24, 2026. Outbreaks were simulated until 500 simulations met these criteria, and these accepted simulations served as the basis for scenario projections.[^mooring-2026-methods-3]

**Scenario projection:** Four intervention scenarios were assessed for each calibration — 20% (poor), 50% (moderate), 70% (high), and 95% (extremely high) isolation — where isolation represents the percentage of symptomatic infected persons detected, isolated, and treated. The intervention was assumed to start on May 24, 2026, with an average delay of 2 days until isolation and treatment.[^mooring-2026-methods-4]

The model was adapted from one applied to previous viral hemorrhagic fever outbreaks, including a Marburg virus disease outbreak in Ethiopia in 2025.[^mooring-2026-methods-2]

### Assumptions

**Outbreak initiated by a single spillover event:** Each simulated outbreak was initialized with one infected person, who represented the person first infected from a zoonotic source (a spillover event).[^mooring-2026-methods-2] The analysis does not discuss the possibility of multiple independent spillovers.

**Calibration death counts represent true BVD mortality:** Assumptions for cumulative deaths as of May 24, 2026, were based on publicly available situation reports from DRC.[^mooring-2026-methods-1] The true number of BVD deaths that occurred through May 24, 2026, is unknown — some deaths from BVD might not have been confirmed, and it is possible that other deaths might have been incorrectly attributed to BVD.[^mooring-2026-discussion-3] This uncertainty is the reason three calibration targets (50, 100, 200) were used.

**Historical R₀ estimates apply to this outbreak:** Basic reproductive number estimates for Ebola disease vary widely across outbreaks, and the true value of R₀ for this outbreak might be higher or lower than the values used in this analysis.[^mooring-2026-discussion-3]

**Isolated persons cause no onward transmission:** Simulated persons in isolation were prevented from causing any onward transmission; the model implicitly assumed that isolated persons who died were safely buried.[^mooring-2026-methods-7]

**Behavioral change not modeled:** Changes in behavior that reduce risk for infection (e.g., avoiding contact with ill persons) were not included in the model and might help limit outbreak size.[^mooring-2026-discussion-3]

**Population immunity does not accumulate meaningfully over the projection period:** The model did not account for transmission reductions attributable to an increase in the proportion of the population with infection-induced immunity. Given the population size of the communities where this outbreak is occurring, this limitation is unlikely to affect the validity of the projections over the time span and numerical ranges of cases presented in this analysis; however, the model could project unrealistically large outbreaks if applied to longer periods.[^mooring-2026-discussion-3]

**No relapses after recovery:** The model did not include infection relapses after recovery. This limitation is unlikely to affect this analysis, but relapses could be important drivers of the course of the epidemic over a longer period.[^mooring-2026-discussion-3]

### Limitations

**Stated by authors:**

The true number of BVD deaths through May 24, 2026, is unknown — some deaths from BVD might not have been confirmed, and some deaths might have been incorrectly attributed to BVD.[^mooring-2026-discussion-3] R₀ estimates for Ebola disease vary widely across outbreaks, and the true value for this outbreak might be higher or lower than values used; high-quality data on changes in the number of cases and deaths over time are essential to more precisely estimate R₀.[^mooring-2026-discussion-3] Behavioral changes that reduce infection risk were not included in the model and might help limit outbreak size.[^mooring-2026-discussion-3] Transmission reductions from growing population immunity were not modeled; this is unlikely to affect short-term projections given community population sizes but could cause the model to project unrealistically large outbreaks over longer periods.[^mooring-2026-discussion-3] Infection relapses after recovery were not included; while unlikely to affect this analysis, relapses could be important drivers of the epidemic over a longer period.[^mooring-2026-discussion-3]

**Additional observations (inferred):**

The analysis does not estimate the current true size of the outbreak directly; it projects future trajectories from a calibration point, making it less directly comparable to back-calculation analyses that produce a point estimate of under-detection. The three calibration death counts (50, 100, 200) encode a wide uncertainty range about current outbreak magnitude — the DRC public health institute reported 10 confirmed deaths and 223 suspected deaths as of May 24, 2026[^mooring-2026-discussion-2] — and the analysis does not adjudicate which calibration is most plausible, leaving the implied current true size ambiguous. Additionally, the model was initialized with a single spillover event[^mooring-2026-methods-2], and if multiple independent zoonotic spillovers or undetected transmission chains exist, the inferred outbreak start date and size at the calibration date could be underestimated.

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:** Publicly available situation reports from DRC were used to inform calibration assumptions about cumulative deaths. The DRC national public health institute reported 10 confirmed deaths and 223 suspected deaths as of May 24, 2026; a subsequent report with data as of June 2, 2026, reported 62 confirmed deaths in DRC.[^mooring-2026-methods-1][^mooring-2026-discussion-2] As of June 2, 2026, 378 confirmed cases (363 in DRC and 15 in Uganda) and 63 confirmed deaths (62 in DRC and one in Uganda) had been recorded.[^mooring-2026-introduction-1]

**Auxiliary data (parameters from prior outbreaks):** Assumptions about parameters were based on published estimates from previous Ebola outbreaks, with estimates specific to BVD used when available.[^mooring-2026-methods-5] The model was adapted from one previously applied to viral hemorrhagic fever outbreaks, including a Marburg virus disease outbreak in Ethiopia in 2025.[^mooring-2026-methods-2] Specific cited parameter sources are listed in a Supplementary Box referenced in the paper but not reproduced in the main text.

### Epistorm (2026) Ebola Bundibugyo Virus — DRC · Spread Risk Assessment #2

[Read the report](https://epistorm.github.io/EBV2026/report_02_spread_risk.html)

### Overview

This analysis estimates the true size of the 2026 BVD outbreak at the source (DRC: Ituri and North Kivu provinces, plus Kampala as a co-active source) by working backwards from detected international exportation events. It uses an Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC) approach: given the observed international exportations as data, the model repeatedly simulates outbreaks of varying sizes and retains parameter values whose simulated outputs are consistent with the observations, conditioned on the incubation period of BVD and the importation-detection efficiency abroad.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-1] Across three importation-detection-efficiency scenarios, the analysis estimates the underlying outbreak size between a posterior median of 1,047 infections (100% importation detection; 90% CrI: 443–2,043) and 1,944 infections (50% importation detection; 90% CrI: 1,056–3,224).[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-1]

---

### Estimate

Under the most conservative scenario (100% importation detection), the posterior median outbreak size is **1,047 infections (90% CrI: 443–2,043)**. Under the mid-range scenario (66% importation detection), the median is **1,495 infections (90% CrI: 739–2,644)**. Under the most expansive scenario (50% importation detection), the median rises to **1,944 infections (90% CrI: 1,056–3,224)**.[^epistorm-2026-size-of-the-epidemic-2]

All estimates pertain to the combined multi-province source footprint (Ituri, North Kivu, and Kampala) as of 22 May 2026. Across all three scenarios, the lower 5th percentile of the posterior remains well above 400 infections, consistent with the surveillance gaps documented in the WHO situation report and the reported healthcare-worker deaths.[^epistorm-2026-size-of-the-epidemic-3] No single scenario is designated by the authors as the primary or headline estimate; the three together bound the plausible range.

---

### Geography

**Primary scope:** The outbreak size estimate covers the multi-province source footprint spanning Ituri, North Kivu, and Kampala (treated as co-active sources of exportation), with the ABC inference producing posterior estimates of the underlying outbreak size at this combined source.[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-3][^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-1]

**Sensitivity scope:** Three importation-detection-efficiency scenarios (100%, 66%, and 50%) are used to bound the inference, corresponding to 4, 6, and 8 true exported cases given the four observed; these are scenario variants rather than distinct geographic scopes.[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-3]

The analysis does not produce a separate outbreak size estimate for Uganda; Uganda (Kampala) appears as both a detected exportation destination and, subsequently, a co-active source in the relative importation risk model. The exported cases consist of five laboratory-confirmed cases in Kampala (three importations from DRC and two local contacts) and one importation in Bukavu, South Kivu.[^epistorm-2026-front-matter-2]

---

### Method

**Method family:** Geographic spread / travel-based back-calculation, implemented via Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC) coupled to a mobility model.

**Method description:** The number of infections at the origin is inferred using an ABC approach: given observed international exportations as data, the posterior distribution over outbreak size is approximated by repeatedly simulating outbreaks of varying sizes, generating synthetic exportation events under the mobility model, and retaining parameter values whose simulated outputs are consistent with the observations. Two parameters condition the inference: the incubation period of BVD and the importation-detection efficiency abroad.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-1]

The mobility model follows the GLEAM (Global Epidemic and Mobility) computational framework, drawing on global air travel patterns from IATA and OAG for international passenger flows, complemented by short-scale commuting patterns capturing daily movements between adjacent subpopulations.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-2]

For each potential destination, the model estimates the probability that an infected individual originating in the affected area travels there, conditional on at least one exported case occurring, producing a relative risk distribution across all candidate destinations.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-3]

---

### Assumptions

Case importations are modeled as statistically independent events. Real-world events involving multiple related cases (for example, a family cluster traveling together) are counted as a single importation event.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-1] The analysis acknowledges that to the extent such clusters are common, the model will under-count the number of distinct exposure events while correctly counting the number of distinct geographic seedings.

Mobility data are assumed to capture cross-border movement adequately, though the analysis acknowledges that informal cross-border movement — which is substantial in the Ituri–Uganda corridor — may not be fully captured.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-2]

The importation-detection efficiency abroad is held fixed within each scenario (100%, 66%, or 50%), because actual importation-detection efficiency in Uganda is not directly observed[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-1] and may vary by port of entry, healthcare-seeking pathway, and clinical presentation.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-2]

Travel probability within a catchment area is treated as independent of traveler sociodemographic attributes (age, economic status, occupation, or pre-existing medical conditions), any of which may modulate both exposure risk at origin and care-seeking behavior at destination.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-3]

The incubation period distribution from the 2007 outbreak is assumed to apply to the current outbreak, constraining the window within which an exported case could travel while infectious or pre-symptomatic.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-4]

Relative importation risk estimates are conditional on at least one exported case occurring; they describe how exportation probability is distributed across destinations given that exportation happens, not the absolute probability that any particular destination will receive a case.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-5]

---

### Limitations

**Stated by authors:**

The mobility model may not fully capture informal cross-border movement, which is substantial in the Ituri–Uganda corridor, potentially affecting both the relative risk distribution and the outbreak size inference.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-2]

The outbreak-size inference is sensitive to the assumed importation-detection-efficiency scenario; actual importation-detection efficiency is not directly observed and may vary by port of entry, healthcare-seeking pathway, and clinical presentation.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-2]

The model does not incorporate sociodemographic attributes of travelers (age, economic status, occupation, or pre-existing medical conditions), which may modulate both exposure risk at origin and care-seeking behavior at destination.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-3]

Modeling importations as statistically independent events means that family clusters or group travelers traveling together are counted as a single importation event, potentially under-counting distinct exposure events.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-1]

The model does not predict downstream transmission chains following importation events; risk estimates reflect the probability of a single importation and do not account for the probability of sustained local transmission in the receiving location.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-4]

As additional exported cases are confirmed, posterior intervals will tighten and the most consistent ascertainment regime will become identifiable, meaning current uncertainty bounds remain wide pending further data.[^epistorm-2026-size-of-the-epidemic-4]

**Additional observations (inferred):**

The incubation period parameters are drawn from the 2007 Uganda outbreak. The analysis credits the 2007 Uganda outbreak as the source for the BVD incubation period (2–21 days, average ~10 days)[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-1] but does not discuss whether this distribution is appropriate for the 2026 DRC outbreak context, which involves different healthcare settings and case ascertainment conditions; divergence could shift the inferred outbreak size.

The estimate covers cumulative infections at the multi-province source, but the primary surveillance data distinguishes only confirmed cases (101) and suspected cases (904)[^epistorm-2026-front-matter-5] without a published line list. The ABC inference is therefore driven entirely by the small number of exportation events (four), which keeps credible intervals wide regardless of the surveillance totals; this is an inherent constraint of the back-calculation approach when few exportations are observed.

---

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:**

DRC Ministry of Health / WHO situation reports (as of 22 May 2026): 101 laboratory-confirmed cases, 904 suspected cases, and 119 suspected deaths across health zones in Ituri (Bunia, Rwampara, Nyankunde, Mongbwalu) and North Kivu provinces, plus one confirmed case with travel history in Bukavu, South Kivu.[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-2]

Uganda confirmed exported cases (as of 22 May 2026): five laboratory-confirmed cases in Kampala — three independent importations from DRC (two travelers detected 15–16 May plus one retrospective case traced to 14 May travel) and two local contacts.[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-2]

Four independent importation events in total (3 to Kampala, 1 to Bukavu) are used as the observational data input to the ABC inference.[^epistorm-2026-size-of-the-epidemic-1]

**Auxiliary data:**

Global air travel: IATA and OAG origin-destination passenger flows providing passenger-level origin-destination flows between international airports.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-2]

Local mobility: sub-national daily mobility data capturing short-scale commuting patterns between adjacent subpopulations, within the GLEAM framework.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-2]

BVD incubation period parameters (2–21 days, average ~10 days): sourced from the 2007 Uganda outbreak.[^epistorm-2026-front-matter-3][^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-1]

(2026) "Discussion": "Our  analysis  has  some  limitations;  homogeneous mixing oversimplifies spatial heterogeneity across health zones; health-care worker transmission was not separately  modelled;  response  intensity  was  applied uniformly;  the  model  is  anchored  on  a  single  recent confirmed-case datum (598 on June 8, 2026), so the fit is sensitive to that anchor; simulation-filtering is not formal inference  (appendix  3  p  6);  and  informal  cross-border mobility estimates are imprecise. These  projections require iterative recalibration as new data emerge. The response-intensity  factor  was  applied  uniformly  from May 15, but real-world response effectiveness varies by place,  time,  access,  and  community  acceptance.  Live mobility data and behavioural adaptation were not incorporated. Surveillance dynamics,  particularly the post-declaration case-finding surge, probably contributed to the rapid apparent growth between May 15 and 19; we addressed  this  by  lowering  the  reporting  fraction  and widening the calibration window, but residual confounding  between  transmission  and  surveillance intensity remains. Misclassification,  delayed  laboratory  throughput,  and ongoing active case finding could each shift the calibration  anchor  in  either  direction.  The  projections presented  here  should  therefore  be  interpreted  as  an early  situational  assessment  rather  than  a  definitive forecast  and  will  be  updated  as  verified  line-list  data become available. Future iterations should integrate line lists, contact-tracing data, and dynamic mobility estimates. Individual-level sex and gender data were not available  in  the  aggregate  surveillance  reports  used  for this  modelling  study;  disaggregated  analysis  by  sex  or gender  was  therefore  not  possible.  Race  and  ethnicity data  were  not  collected,  as  this  is  a  population-level modelling study using aggregate surveillance data."
