# Outbreak Size

:::{important} Key findings
The most recent current-size analysis estimates 2,778–14,014 infections (90% credible interval) as of 14 June, versus 808 laboratory-confirmed cases; earlier analyses from May 2026 estimated roughly 215–3,224 cases or infections across scenario intervals. Five analyses are currently incorporated, all of which converge on material under-detection despite differing dates, scopes, and methods. The evidence suggests response planning should assume a substantially larger outbreak than confirmed counts alone indicate, with low-to-moderate confidence.
:::

## Synthesizing evidence

### State of the evidence

The evidence base is thin but growing: five rapid analyses dated 22 May to 17 June 2026, including travel-based back-calculations, death back-calculation, ABC mobility inference, a joint Bayesian renewal model, and one CDC branching-process projection rather than a direct current-size estimate.[^epistorm-2026-front-matter-1][^bluedot-2026-front-matter-1][^mccabe-2026-2][^abbott-2026-front-matter-2][^mooring-2026-abstract-1] The analyses use rely upon overlapping surveillance and exported case data, but the main modelling approaches are sufficiently different to make convergence meaningful.

### How the estimates compare

Late-May current-size estimates mostly put the true burden in the hundreds to low thousands: BlueDot estimated 359 cases, rising to 718 under export under-detection sensitivity; McCabe et al. estimated about 280–2,520; and Epistorm estimated posterior medians of 1,047–1,944 infections with 90% credible intervals up to 3,224.[^bluedot-2026-key-takeaways-1][^mccabe-2026-2][^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-1] Abbott et al.’s later 14 June estimate is larger, 2,778–14,014 infections, and is not directly comparable to May estimates because it uses later data and fits multiple surveillance streams jointly.[^abbott-2026-results-1] Mooring et al. do not estimate current true size, but its projections are consistent with an outbreak already large enough to generate high short-term risk under a low isolation scenario.[^mooring-2026-abstract-1]

### Key uncertainties and assumptions

The largest uncertainties are the number and detectability of exported cases, cross-border movement volumes, geographic source population, CFR and delay distributions borrowed from past BVD or Ebola outbreaks, and the growth trajectory before detection.[^mccabe-2026-7][^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-2] A shared structural limitation is that several estimates depend on aggregate situation-report counts rather than line lists with onset, detection, death, and location dates.[^abbott-2026-limitations-5] Confidence is low-to-moderate because method convergence is offset by sparse export events, changing surveillance definitions, and strong prior assumptions.

### What would reduce uncertainty

The most useful update would be a reconciled line list linking suspected, confirmed, fatal, recovered, exported, and hospitalized cases by onset, report, death or recovery date, health zone, and classification history. Better point-of-entry and informal mobility data, investigation of whether exports were routine travellers or healthcare-seeking cases, and additional genomic dating would directly test the assumptions that these estimates rely upon.[^mccabe-2026-13][^epistorm-2026-size-of-the-epidemic-4]

### What this means for the response

The evidence suggests confirmed counts are a lower bound and that operational planning should assume substantial undetected transmission in eastern DRC, especially around Ituri and connected cross-border corridors.[^mccabe-2026-12][^bluedot-2026-key-takeaways-1] It also suggests cross-border preparedness remains central, with Epistorm flagging elevated onward-importation risk to nearby Ugandan and regional destinations and Abbott et al. estimating bed demand above national reported capacity.[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-4][^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-5][^abbott-2026-results-9]

[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-1]: "The number of infections at the origin is inferred using an Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC) approach. Given the observed international exportations as data D, the posterior distribution P(θ|D) over outbreak size θ is approximated by repeatedly simulating outbreaks of varying sizes, generating synthetic exportation events under the mobility model, and retaining parameter values whose simulated outputs are consistent with the observations. Two parameters condition the inference: the incubation period of BVD (2-21 days; average ~10 days based on the 2007 Uganda outbreak) and the importation-detection efficiency abroad. Three importation-detection-efficiency scenarios - 100%, 66%, and 50% - are reported to bound sensitivity to undercounting, corresponding to true export counts of 4, 6, and 8 given the four independent events observed (three to Kampala plus one to Bukavu, South Kivu)." (Epistorm 2026, "Methods Overview")
[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-2]: "Global air travel patterns are derived from the International Air Transport Association (IATA) and the Official Airline Guide (OAG), providing passenger-level origin-destination flows between international airports. These data are complemented by short-scale commuting patterns capturing daily movements between adjacent subpopulations, representing the local spread of disease through routine human activity within and across health zones. The modeling approach follows the GLEAM (Global Epidemic and Mobility) computational framework." (Epistorm 2026, "Methods Overview")
[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-3]: "For each potential destination Y, the model estimates the probability P(Y) that an infected individual originating in the affected area travels to Y, conditional on at least one exported case occurring. The output is a relative risk distribution across all candidate destinations, normalized so that values represent the share of importation probability assigned to each location." (Epistorm 2026, "Methods Overview")
