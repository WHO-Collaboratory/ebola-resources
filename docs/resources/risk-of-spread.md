# Risk of Spread

*Latest analysis data cutoff: 5 June 2026. Page refreshed: 9 July 2026.*

:::{hint} At a glance
The modeled risk of international spread from the 2026 Bundibugyo virus disease outbreak suggests significant introduction risk to nearby regions, particularly Uganda and Rwanda. Five analyses, converge that mobility concentrates importation risk in connected regional corridors and air hubs, but the top destinations change with source geography and modeling scope. Evidence suggests targeting surveillance at high-probability importation pathways. Confidence is low-to-moderate because location rankings are more stable than the absolute probabilities assigned to them, but mobility patterns, case numbers, and detection levels contain uncertainty.

## Synthesizing evidence

### State of the evidence

The evidence base is thin but operationally useful: five independent analyses, all preprint or ad hoc reports, dated from 4 to 25 June 2026 with data cutoffs from 29 May to 5 June. They cover complementary questions rather than one shared endpoint: DRC health-zone introduction and amplification potential, Uganda district spread, conditional global importation ranking, and airline-mediated importation.[^luvande-2026-abstract-1][^walekhwa-2026-abstract-1][^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-7][^kinoshita-2026-abstract-1][^fanelli-2026-abstract-1] The strongest convergence is on rankings and pathways, not on point probabilities.

### How the estimates compare

The ranking of locations by importation risk depends on the method applied. Epistorm characterizes the risk of international spread as primarily borne by nearby countries and international travel hubs, led by Tanzania, South Sudan, Kenya, and the UAE, with its top five city destinations accounting for roughly 74% of conditional international risk.[^epistorm-2026-destination-analysis-1] Kinoshita et al. instead identify major air hubs as highest risk — Belgium, France, South Africa, Kenya, and the UAE — because the model starts from airline effective distance from Kinshasa, while Fanelli et al. find France, Italy, and the UK persistently among the most exposed European countries unless the epicenter of the outbreak shifts to regional capitals.[^kinoshita-2026-abstract-2][^fanelli-2026-abstract-2][^fanelli-2026-abstract-3] Within DRC and Uganda, Luvande et al. identify eight DRC health zones with high probability of both importation and sustained-transmission, including in Kinshasa, Nord/Sud-Kivu, and Tshopo zones, while Walekhwa et al. project Uganda’s burden concentrating in Kampala and Wakiso rather than only at border districts.[^luvande-2026-body-2][^walekhwa-2026-results-3]

### Key uncertainties and assumptions

The shared uncertainty is not whether mobility matters, but whether observed or historical mobility represents current mobility trends, especially informal crossings, airport travel, and behaviour change.[^epistorm-2026-limitations-amp-assumptions-8][^walekhwa-2026-discussion-3] Sustained-transmission estimates also depend on case detection, isolation, contact tracing, and IPC.[^luvande-2026-body-12][^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-22]

### What would reduce uncertainty

First, verified information on exported cases, including date, source location, route, and detection timing would reduce uncertainty, because it would recalibrate both airline and land-border rankings.[^kinoshita-2026-conclusion-1] Second would be current mobility data for informal land crossings, domestic/regional aviation after Bunia airport reopening, and key trade routes. Organizations like [Flowminder](https://www.flowminder.org/resources/publications-reports/drc-reports-publications/mouvements-de-population-vers-et-depuis-les-zones-de-sante-affectees-fondes-sur-l-analyse-confidentielle-de-donnees-operateurs-mobiles-de-vodacom-congo-rdc-30-june-2026) aim to support the response effort by providing more real-time data on mobility trends.

### What this means for the response

The evidence suggests surveillance should follow two tracks: (1) likely importation corridors and (2) places where an introduction could amplify outbreak size. For international importation, the near-term focus is regional, and secondarily air travel hubs; for sustained transmission, urban and highly connected inland centres remain important even when they are not closest to the outbreak.[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-12][^luvande-2026-body-17] For Uganda, the evidence suggests pairing border screening at high-risk importation hubs with inland readiness in Kampala and Wakiso, while recognizing that importation does not entail sustained transmission where detection and isolation are fast.[^walekhwa-2026-abstract-6][^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-26]

### Walekhwa et al. (2026) Network-based modelling of Bundibugyo Ebola virus disease importation and spread in Uganda using Displacement Tracking Matrix flow data and non-pharmaceutical intervention compliance scenarios

[Read the report](https://www.medrxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.22.26356210v1)

### Overview

This analysis estimates how the 2026 Bundibugyo virus disease (BVD) outbreak in Uganda, seeded by cross-border introductions from DRC, is likely to spread across Ugandan districts over a 90-day horizon. The authors built a data-driven directed mobility network from IOM Displacement Tracking Matrix flows and 2024 Uganda census data, then ran a stochastic metapopulation SEIR model across 135 Ugandan districts and two DRC provinces, incorporating pre-symptomatic transmission and movement of both exposed and infectious individuals.[^walekhwa-2026-abstract-1] The headline finding is that Uganda's agile 7-1-7 response is projected to contain the outbreak to a median of 69–70 cases and 3 deaths over 90 days, with Kampala bearing the highest predicted burden (median 22 cases, 100% probability of exceeding 10 cases), and that additional non-pharmaceutical interventions produced no statistically significant reduction in cases.[^walekhwa-2026-abstract-2][^walekhwa-2026-discussion-1]

### Estimate

The primary headline estimate, under the baseline agile 7-1-7 response with no additional NPIs, covers Uganda over 90 days from 5 June 2026. Under baseline mobility, the model projected a median of 69–70 cumulative cases (95% CrI: 57–98) and 3 deaths over 90 days.[^walekhwa-2026-abstract-5]

Across the three NPI compliance scenarios (20%, 40%, 60%), the estimates were nearly identical: 20% compliance: 70 cases (95% CrI: 58–98), 3 deaths (0–7), 11 HCW infections (9–15), 1 HCW death (1–2); 40% compliance: 70 cases (95% CrI: 58–95), 3 deaths (0–6), 11 HCW infections (9–15), 1 HCW death (1–2); 60% compliance: 69 cases (95% CrI: 57–95), 3 deaths (0–6), 11 HCW infections (9–15), 1 HCW death (1–2).[^walekhwa-2026-results-1] The 95% credible intervals overlapped substantially across compliance levels, indicating no statistically significant difference between compliance scenarios.[^walekhwa-2026-results-2]

At the district level (20% compliance scenario): Kampala had the highest predicted burden with a median of 22 cases (95% CrI: 20–43) and 100% probability of local outbreak (>10 cases); Wakiso followed with a median of 11 cases (95% CrI: 10–23) and an outbreak probability of 64.9%; border districts showed lower predictions: Bundibugyo (median 5 cases, 95% CrI: 4–11), Kasese (median 3 cases, 95% CrI: 3–8), Kisoro (median 2 cases, 95% CrI: 2–7), and Mbarara (median 2 cases, 95% CrI: 2–5); Mbale had a median of 1 case (95% CrI: 1–3).[^walekhwa-2026-results-3]

### Methods

**Data-driven directed weighted mobility network:** The authors constructed a directed, weighted network representing human movement between DRC and Uganda and within Uganda. The network was built from IOM Displacement Tracking Matrix flows collected 15–24 May 2026 (11,245 observed movements) and the 2024 Uganda census (45.9 million people).[^walekhwa-2026-abstract-4] Both undirected and directed weighted networks were built from the DTM flow data, combining eight cross-border flow records (from DRC provinces Ituri and Nord-Kivu to six border districts) and 23 internal flows among Ugandan districts; the directed network preserved original flow directions to compute in-strength (import risk) and out-strength (export risk) separately.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-5] Bootstrap resampling with 1,000 replicates was performed to quantify uncertainty in ten global network metrics due to the limited 10-day sampling window.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-6]

**Stochastic metapopulation SEIR model:** A discrete-time stochastic SEIR model was run at the node (district) level for all 135 Ugandan districts plus two DRC provinces. Each node maintained five compartments (S, E, I, R, D); the latent period was set to 7.5 days and the infectious period to 8.5 days; pre-symptomatic transmission was modelled by setting relative infectiousness of exposed individuals to 0.5, based on estimates from early EVD outbreaks and the empirical observation that six early secondary cases in Kampala tested positive while under quarantine.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-7] DRC provinces were modelled as explicit nodes with their own SEIR dynamics, recalibrated to reported data as of 25 May 2026 (381 confirmed cases, 2,987 exposed contacts, 64 deaths), with DRC response parameters representing a moderately delayed response; cross-border spread occurred naturally through the movement matrix rather than through a separate import process.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-8] Five hundred stochastic simulations were run for each of three NPI compliance scenarios, and 1,000 simulations for the baseline, each covering 90 days from 5 June 2026.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-9]

**NPI compliance scenario analysis:** Three compliance levels (20%, 40%, 60%) were tested for a multi-dimensional NPI model disaggregating community contact reduction, healthcare protection, and mobility restriction. NPIs included risk communication and standard operating procedures (targeting community contact reduction, baseline effectiveness 30%), avoidance of handshaking and enhanced hygiene (healthcare protection, 20%), and restrictions on mass gatherings and market closures (mobility reduction, 40%).[^walekhwa-2026-methods-10]

**Global Sobol sensitivity analysis:** A variance-based Sobol sensitivity analysis was applied to identify which of 12 parameters most influenced total cumulative case count. The design used quasi-random Sobol sequences with N = 500 samples; for each parameter set, the output was averaged over 3 stochastic replicates, and Sobol indices were estimated with 200 bootstrap replicates to obtain 95% confidence intervals.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-11]

### Assumptions

**Mobility data representativeness and scaling:** The DTM flow data from the 10-day period are assumed to be representative of average daily movement patterns, after scaling by 36.5 to approximate annual flows.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-19] Movement is assumed independent each day (no memory); only exposed and infectious individuals move, with equal movement probability for both compartments — an assumption made due to lack of detailed information on EVD status among travellers.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-20]

**Within-district mixing and spatial structure:** Due to lack of district-specific mobility information, homogeneous mixing within districts is assumed, which the authors acknowledge likely overestimates transmission in low-density areas and underestimates it in high-density slums.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-21]

**Detection and isolation:** A detection delay of 3 days is applied uniformly to all infected individuals, and quarantine after detection is assumed perfect — isolated individuals no longer transmit.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-22] Reported incidence is the number of new cases on the previous day multiplied by a detection probability p_detect = 1.0, assuming all symptomatic cases are detected.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-23]

**Contact tracing coverage:** Contact tracing coverage is dynamic: starting at 40% when cumulative cases are ≤3, rising to 70% when cases are 4–10, and reaching 85% when cumulative cases exceed 10.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-24]

**DRC epidemic independence:** The DRC epidemic curve is assumed independent of Uganda's interventions and drives importations solely through the movement matrix.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-25] DRC response parameters are set to represent a moderately delayed response: detection delay 7 days, notification delay 3 days, response delay 5 days, contact tracing coverage 30%, detection probability 0.7, and no behavioural response (k = 1).[^walekhwa-2026-methods-26]

**R₀ and biological parameters:** The baseline R₀ = 2.76 was calibrated to the early growth of the outbreak (16 cases in 21 days).[^walekhwa-2026-methods-18] The case fatality rate (2/16 = 0.125) and HCW attack rate (3/16 = 0.188) are held constant throughout.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-27] Sobol sensitivity ranges tested R₀ from 1.5 to 3.5 and CFR from 0.05 to 0.2.

**Pre-symptomatic transmission:** Pre-symptomatic transmission was modelled by setting the relative infectiousness of exposed individuals to 0.5 compared to infectious individuals, based on estimates from early EVD outbreaks and the empirical observation that six of the secondary cases in Kampala tested positive while under quarantine.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-28]

**Superspreading:** New infections are drawn from a negative binomial distribution with dispersion parameter k_disp = 0.3 to capture superspreading.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-29]

**Behavioural response:** The behavioural response exponent k = 3 and NPI intrinsic effectiveness values (30%, 20%, 40%) are assumed appropriate for the Bundibugyo strain and Ugandan context.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-30] Compliance with NPIs is assumed constant over the 90 days and homogeneous across districts.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-31]

**Geographic scope of importations:** No importations from countries other than DRC are considered.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-4]

### Limitations

**Mobility data coverage and representativeness:** The DTM data cover only a 10-day window and eight border crossing points; movements through unofficial crossings or after the outbreak escalated may not be captured, a limitation common to mobility-based epidemiological studies.[^walekhwa-2026-discussion-3] While annual estimates were imputed using time-dependent decay, real mobility patterns can change unpredictably.[^walekhwa-2026-discussion-4]

**Within-district homogeneous mixing:** The homogeneous mixing assumption within districts likely overestimates transmission in low-density areas and underestimates it in high-density slums.[^walekhwa-2026-discussion-5]

**Sobol sensitivity quantitative precision:** The Sobol confidence intervals for some parameters remained wide because the small outbreak size (median <70 cases) produces low output variance, making precise quantitative ranking unstable — hence the qualitative interpretation adopted by the authors.[^walekhwa-2026-discussion-6]

**Behavioural response function:** The behavioural response function lacks strain-specific validation, though low sensitivity indices partially mitigate this concern.[^walekhwa-2026-discussion-7]

**Hospital and funeral transmission:** Hospital and funeral transmission are not explicitly modelled — a limitation given that nosocomial transmission and unsafe burials have been identified as major amplifiers in Ebola outbreaks.[^walekhwa-2026-discussion-8]

**Importations from countries other than DRC:** Importations from neighbouring countries other than DRC were not considered, though the 79.9% importation risk estimated by Chalma et al. (2026) focused specifically on DRC-origin introductions.[^walekhwa-2026-discussion-9]

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:** The Ministry of Health EVD situation report as of 5 June 2026 provided the initial outbreak conditions: 15 cumulative cases (plus 1 probable), 2 deaths, 5 recoveries, and 720 cumulative contacts listed (455 active under follow-up).[^walekhwa-2026-methods-2] DRC dynamics were recalibrated using actual reported data as of 25 May 2026: 381 confirmed cases, 2,987 exposed contacts, and 64 deaths.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-12]

**Auxiliary data — mobility:** IOM DTM Flow Monitoring report covering 15–24 May 2026 recorded cross-border movements from DRC into Uganda and internal movements among Ugandan districts at eight border crossing points, with 11,245 total movements; the percentage distribution across eight flow monitoring points was extracted (Ntoroko Main 28%, Mpondwe 18%, Busunga 18%, Bunagana 12%, Cyanika 8%, Goli 8%, Vurra 3%, Busanza 3%).[^walekhwa-2026-introduction-1][^walekhwa-2026-methods-13] Internal movement flows were manually transcribed from the report's schematic diagram (e.g., Kisoro–Kampala = 480, Kasese–Kampala = 420).[^walekhwa-2026-methods-14]

**Auxiliary data — demographic:** The official 2024 Uganda Population and Housing Census final report (UBOS, December 2024) provided de facto population for each of the 135 districts, with a total national population of 45,905,417, drawn from Tables 2.4A–2.4D.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-15] For DRC provinces, an estimated population of 1.5 million each was used for eastern high-risk provinces, sourced from USAID 2024.[^walekhwa-2026-methods-16]

**Auxiliary data — parameters from prior literature:** The latent period (7.5 days) and infectious period (8.5 days) were drawn from Legrand et al. (2007) and Althaus (2014)/Riad et al. (2019).[^walekhwa-2026-methods-17] The baseline transmission rate β was derived from R₀ = 2.76, calibrated to the early growth of the outbreak (16 cases in 21 days).[^walekhwa-2026-methods-18] A 79.9% importation risk for EVD given the intensity of cross-border travel from DRC, estimated by Chalma et al. (2026), is cited as contextual prior work.[^walekhwa-2026-discussion-2]

### Luvande, Iroungou, & Valdano (2026) Introduction and sustained-transmission risk across DRC health zones during the Bundibugyo virus disease outbreak

[Read the report](https://www.medrxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.11.26355237v1)

### Overview

This analysis estimates introduction risk and sustained-transmission potential for each health zone (HZ) across the DRC during the ongoing Bundibugyo virus disease outbreak, with the goal of identifying priority zones — including those far from currently affected areas — where rapid amplification could follow an introduction and where response efforts should be focused.[^luvande-2026-abstract-1] The approach applies a published method to rank all DRC health zones by their sustained-transmission potential, using population and mobility data to parameterise spatial transmission across a broad range of assumptions on disease transmissibility and contact patterns, then compares these ranks with introduction-risk ranks estimated from mobility data.[^luvande-2026-body-1] The main finding is that eight health zones combine both high introduction risk and high sustained-transmission potential: Binza Ozone and Binza Meteo in Kinshasa; Karisimbi in Nord-Kivu; Kadutu and Ibanda in Sud-Kivu; and Makiso-Kisangani, Kabondo, and Mangobo in Tshopo.[^luvande-2026-body-2]

### Estimate

This analysis does not report outbreak size estimates (e.g., total case counts or incidence figures). Introduction risk was highest in health zones close to the reported foci — specifically in Ituri, Nord-Kivu, and Haut-Uele — consistent with expected mobility patterns, with non-negligible introduction risk also appearing in distant urban centres including the Kinshasa area.[^luvande-2026-body-9] High sustained-transmission potential was distributed broadly across DRC, including Kinshasa, Kasaï-Oriental, Kongo-Central, Tshopo, Nord-Kivu, Haut-Katanga, Sud-Kivu, Kasaï, and Kasaï-Central — several of which are far from the current outbreak.[^luvande-2026-body-10] Crossing the two rankings identified eight health zones combining high introduction risk and high sustained-transmission potential: Binza Ozone and Binza Meteo in Kinshasa; Karisimbi in Nord-Kivu; Kadutu and Ibanda in Sud-Kivu; and Makiso-Kisangani, Kabondo, and Mangobo in Tshopo.[^luvande-2026-body-2] No point probabilities, quantitative thresholds, or numerical uncertainty intervals are reported in the main text.

### Methods

### Overview

### Estimate

This analysis produces importation probability estimates rather than outbreak size estimates. Airport-level arrival probabilities, interpreted as the probability of at least one airline-mediated importation until the end of June 2026 (t = 44), ranged from 0.247 to 0.937, with a mean of 0.431.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-10] Higher arrival probabilities were concentrated among airports with shorter effective distances from FIH and stronger connectivity through major transit hubs: for example, 0.889 at Brussels Airport, 0.888 at Paris Charles de Gaulle Airport, and 0.871 at Johannesburg O.R. **Effective-distance airline importation model:** Potential airline-mediated importation risk was projected using a hazard-based model informed by contemporary airline transportation networks and a hazard parameter calibrated from the 2013–2016 West African Ebola virus disease epidemic, one of the few EBOD outbreaks with documented long-distance airline-mediated international exportations.[^kinoshita-2026-abstract-4] A directed, weighted air-traffic network was constructed using airport-level origin-destination passenger-flow data from OAG for May 2024, comprising 11,264 airports, 569,741 routes, and 329,326,078 passengers.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-4] The shortest-path effective distance from FIH to each destination airport was computed using Dijkstra's algorithm on the directed network.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-5] The arrival hazard at each destination airport was modelled as λⱼ = θ/Dⱼ (where θ = 0.19, the primary hazard parameter), and the arrival probability within t days was computed as pⱼ(t) = 1 − exp(−tλⱼ).[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-6] Country-level arrival risk was summarized as the maximum airport-level probability within each country.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-7] Sensitivity analyses tested θ = {0.06, 0.32} based on the 95% confidence interval of the historical parameter estimate.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-8] An additional sensitivity analysis varied the effective-distance scaling parameter δ using epidemic growth rates r = {0.034, 0.05, 0.1} estimated as of 17 May 2026.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-9]

**Importation versus transmission:** Estimated arrival probability is interpreted as importation risk rather than the risk of local transmission after arrival, on the basis that EBOD requires direct contact with infectious body fluids or contaminated materials, and that timely detection, isolation, contact tracing, and infection prevention and control can reduce onward transmission.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-22]

### Limitations

**Reliance on a borrowed, cross-pathogen hazard parameter:** Because no airline-mediated importation had been reported as of 28 May 2026, the hazard parameter was estimated from the 2013–2016 West African EVD epidemic rather than from current BVD data, and the model assumes this historical relationship is transferable to the present outbreak.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-23]

**Static airline data and absence of intervention effects:** The projections rely on May 2024 airline network data and therefore may not capture subsequent route changes, passenger volume shifts, airspace restrictions, travel advisories, or the effect of post-PHEIC interventions, enhanced screening, and behavioural changes.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-24]

### Data sources

Using International Air Transport Association (IATA) origin-destination passenger flows, this analysis estimates the relative risk of Bundibugyo virus disease importation into Europe under six outbreak expansion scenarios reflecting plausible pathways of geographical spread, including cross-border transmission and amplification in highly connected regional capitals.[^fanelli-2026-abstract-1] Relative exposure patterns across European countries remained largely unchanged under localized transmission in eastern DRC and border-spillover scenarios, but expansion into South Sudan generated a first structural increase in importation pressure to Europe, while hypothetical amplification in Kampala, Kigali, and Kinshasa substantially increased importation pressure and reshaped exposure patterns.[^fanelli-2026-abstract-2] Across all scenarios, France, Italy, and the United Kingdom remained among the most exposed countries.[^fanelli-2026-abstract-3]

This analysis does not produce an outbreak size estimate (i.e., a count of cases or infections). Its outputs are relative importation risk rankings for European countries and fold-change increases in importation pressure by European subregion under each scenario. Potential spread across the Ugandan border produced no change in either the relative exposure of European countries or their overall importation pressure compared with the currently reported localized transmission in eastern DRC, with Italy, France, Belgium, and the UK being the most exposed.[^fanelli-2026-text-3] Expansion to South Sudan produced a first structural increase in importation pressure through Juba (5-fold increase for Northern Europe and 6-fold increase for Eastern Europe relative to localized DRC transmission), while still leaving the UK, France, and Italy among the highest-risk countries.[^fanelli-2026-text-4] Kampala and Kigali amplification increased exposure in Northern Europe, with Kampala producing a >120-fold increase in importation pressure relative to localized DRC transmission; Kinshasa amplification primarily reinforced exposure in Western Europe (~80-fold increase).[^fanelli-2026-text-5] In the sensitivity analysis using full-year 2024 travel data, the Kampala amplification scenario produces approximately 190-fold increase for Northern Europe.[^fanelli-2026-appendix-6]

### Methods

**Air-travel importation risk model using IATA origin-destination flows:** The analysis uses origin-destination passenger flows derived from the 2024 IATA database, which captures complete passenger itineraries including traffic travelling via connecting flights rather than only direct flight segments.[^fanelli-2026-appendix-2] The relative importation risk to each European country is estimated as the proportion of total outgoing passenger flow from the selected seed airports directed to that country, where passenger flow from each origin airport in a given scenario is summed across all destination airports in the receiving country, then normalized to total passenger flow from source airports to all of Europe.[^fanelli-2026-appendix-3] Risks are then stratified by European subregion.[^fanelli-2026-appendix-4] This approach produces relative (not absolute) importation risk rankings and does not incorporate incubation period, detection probability, or infectious prevalence at source. ### Assumptions

**Proportional travel as a proxy for importation risk:** The model assumes that the relative proportion of outbound passenger volume from seed airports to each European country is a valid proxy for relative importation risk. **Travel data representativeness:** The analysis uses cumulative passenger traffic from May, June, and July 2024; this period showed moderate seasonal variability, with mean volumes ranging from 3% below to 16% above the monthly 2024 average across scenarios.[^fanelli-2026-appendix-8] The analysis implicitly assumes 2024 travel patterns are representative of 2026 patterns during the outbreak period, including any travel disruptions or demand changes associated with the outbreak itself.

**Capital-city amplification as a hypothetical:** Scenarios 4–6 explore hypothetical amplification assuming community transmission in major urban hubs — Kampala, Kigali, and Kinshasa.[^fanelli-2026-text-7] The analysis does not assign probabilities to these scenarios occurring; they are treated as illustrative of what importation pressure would look like if amplification did occur, rather than as probabilistic forecasts.

**Hypothetical nature of amplification scenarios:** Scenarios 4–6 explore hypothetical amplification assuming community transmission in major urban hubs.[^fanelli-2026-text-7] The analysis does not assign likelihoods to these scenarios or model the transmission dynamics required to reach that state, limiting their direct operational applicability.

**Auxiliary data:** Origin-destination passenger flows are derived from the 2024 IATA database, which captures complete passenger itineraries including connecting flights.[^fanelli-2026-appendix-2] Analyses are based on cumulative passenger traffic reported for May, June, and July 2024.[^fanelli-2026-appendix-1] Relative to annual traffic to Europe, mean passenger volumes during May–July ranged from 3% below to 16% above the monthly 2024 average across scenarios.[^fanelli-2026-appendix-7] No line list, demographic data, or parameters borrowed from prior outbreaks are used as model inputs; the method relies solely on the IATA travel volumes and the airport-level scenario seeding definitions.

:::{dropdown} Methods overview

These pages are produced by a semi-automated evidence-synthesis pipeline that pairs AI models with human review at each major checkpoint: one model reads each source analysis on its own and grounds every extracted claim in a verbatim quoted passage; a second model takes the resulting structured comparison as input, and drafts the narrative. Human analysts then review and edit the output to ensure accuracy and completeness prior to publishing.

**Identifying analyses.** Analyses are identified through continuous monitoring of preprint servers, peer-reviewed publications, and grey literature. An analysis is included if it directly addresses the 2026 Bundibugyo virus disease (BVD) outbreak and provides a quantitative estimate relevant to a given topic (e.g., outbreak size, risk of spread). Each included source is logged, and if the authors later release an updated version, the newer version supersedes the earlier one.

**Preparing the source text.** Each source text, regardless of source type (e.g., PDF, HTML), is converted to Markdown. A human analyst then curates the converted file and removes sections that carry no evidentiary content, such as references, acknowledgments, author contributions, affiliations, and declarations of interest, to keep extraction focused and reduce the number of input tokens used.

**Extracting structured evidence.** The curated text is split into individually citeable units, one per sentence, and passed to Claude Sonnet 4.6. For each of a fixed set of dimensions (e.g., estimates, methods, limitations), the model answers from that one source alone and must ground every factual claim in a verbatim span of its text.

**Synthesizing the narrative.** OpenAI's GPT-5.5 model reads the dimensions extracted for each analysis and synthesizes the information, covering the state of the evidence, how the estimates compare and why they diverge, the key uncertainties and assumptions shared across analyses, what would most reduce that uncertainty, and what it implies for the response.

**Human review and publication.** Human analysts edit the draft for accuracy, completeness, and tone prior to publishing.

**Limitations.** This pipeline surfaces only publicly available analyses; works that have not been made public are not captured.
