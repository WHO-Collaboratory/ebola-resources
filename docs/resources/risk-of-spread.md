# Risk Spread

:::{important} Key findings
The estimated risk of international spread from the 2026 Bundibugyo virus disease outbreak suggests significant introduction risk to nearby regions, particularly Uganda and Rwanda, with substantial probability but variable mechanisms across the analyses. Three analyses, using spatial and airline importation models, converge on high-risk zones and prominent gateway countries by airline travel. Operationally, the evidence suggests an urgent need for enhanced preparedness and cross-border surveillance, particularly for Uganda and key African air travel hubs. Confidence in these estimates is moderate due to uncertain detection efficiencies and reliance on historical models.
:::


## Synthesizing evidence

### State of the evidence
The current evidence comprises three analyses: one preprint exploring spatial and intra-country risks (Luvande et al. 2026) and two estimating regional and international importation risks — Epistorm (2026) via cross-border and Epistorm with Kinoshita et al. (2026) focusing on airline pathways. The analysis dates range from May to June 2026. The evidence base is rapidly growing, but limitations in data completeness and differing methodologies contribute to moderate confidence.

### How the estimates compare
The three analyses converge on significant importation risk to Uganda and Rwanda, with specific health zones in DRC (Luvande) and major East African cities (Epistorm). Divergence arises primarily from methodological approaches: Luvande emphasizes local sustained transmission potential; Epistorm provides cross-border risk estimates using reported international cases; Kinoshita applies an airline importation model extending to European and African hubs. Each reflects different data types and assumptions, primarily the geographic scope and the baseline data used.

### Key uncertainties and assumptions
All analyses are limited by assumptions about mobility patterns and disease transmissibility. Luvande assumes accurate human mobility data, while Epistorm models importation detection efficiency scenarios, creating uncertainty about underlying outbreak size[^epistorm-2026-abstract-1]. Kinoshita assumes airline data from 2024 reflects 2026 travel patterns and calibrates hazard parameters from the prior West African Ebola outbreak[^kinoshita-2026-interpretation-and-limitations-1].

### What would reduce uncertainty
Improved detection of importation cases would substantively reduce uncertainty in outbreak size estimates, as stated in Epistorm[^epistorm-2026-box-4-why-detected-importations-carry-1]. Real-time updates of airline and local mobility patterns would refine risk assessments for Kinoshita and Luvande's models. Enhanced surveillance data from regional health zones in DRC might also allow better parameter estimation for local transmission risk.

### What this means for the response
The evidence suggests prioritizing response preparations in Uganda and Rwanda and reinforcing surveillance and preparedness in Kinshasa and key highlighted health zones within the DRC, as well as major European and African air travel hubs[^kinoshita-2026-estimated-importation-risk-2]. Operationally, this necessitates robust detection and isolation systems in high-risk zones and regions identified as major travel gateways for global spread[^epistorm-2026-figure-1-relative-importation-risk-ituri-1][^luvande-2026-body-2]. Cross-border preparedness should be a focus to mitigate elevated transmission risk from porous land borders, as indicated by Epistorm[^epistorm-2026-abstract-4].
