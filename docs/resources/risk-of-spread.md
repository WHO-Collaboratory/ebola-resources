## Synthesizing evidence

### State of the evidence

As of mid-July, there have been over 2,000 DRC cases, 20 Uganda cases, and one France case [^who-don-1]. The evidence base is broad but still emerging, comprising four peer-reviewed works, five preprints, and one dashboard, with analyses dated from 20 May to 17 July 2026. The evidence base continues to grow, with later analyses incorporating information on wider DRC spread, Uganda's controlled spread, airport reopening, and one imported case in France.

### How the estimates compare

The clearest point of convergence is the ranking of neighbouring-country risk, with Uganda and South Sudan ranked highest, while intercontinental risk remains much lower and concentrated through travel hubs. Chamla et al. and Gashema & Muvunyi give the clearest neighbouring-country ordering: Uganda highest, South Sudan second, Rwanda and Burundi much lower, with Chamla estimating 94.2%, 69.3%, 8.6%, and 2.0%, respectively, over 12 weeks.[^chamla-2026-summary-4][^gashema-2026-2] Epistorm shifts the "international beyond immediate spillover" ranking toward Tanzania, South Sudan, Kenya, and the UAE after excluding Rwanda and Burundi when accounting for the reopening of Bunia airport.[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-12] Kinoshita et al. instead rank Belgium, France, South Africa, Kenya, and the UAE highest because their model is airline-mediated from Kinshasa, while Fanelli et al. find France, Italy, and the UK persistently among Europe's most exposed under Europe-only scenarios.[^kinoshita-2026-abstract-2][^fanelli-2026-abstract-3] Rather than contradicting each other, they reflect different origin-destination assumptions and transport modes.

### Key uncertainties and assumptions

The main uncertainty is structural rather than statistical, driven by incomplete observation of current cross-border movements, informal crossings, source incidence, reporting delay, and destination preparedness. Chamla et al. identify travel-while-infectious and cross-border mobility parameters as principal spillover uncertainties, while Walekhwa et al. note that their mobility data, drawn from IOM Displacement Tracking Matrix (DTM) flow monitoring at eight border crossing points over a 10-day window, may miss unofficial or changed movements.[^chamla-2026-methods-15][^walekhwa-2026-4-discussion-3] Importation risk and sustained-transmission risk are distinct considerations, as Luvande et al. show by finding that DRC zones near affected areas rank high for risk of introduction while distant urban areas show higher potential for amplification.[^luvande-2026-body-16] Kinoshita et al. make the same distinction, cautioning that a country receiving an imported case at one of its airports does not mean the virus will spread onward within that country.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-22]

### What would reduce uncertainty

First, verified, onset-dated line lists with travel, exposure, contact-list status, and onset-to-isolation fields would reduce uncertainty by allowing analyses to distinguish true transmission from changing ascertainment.[^chamla-2026-discussion-13][^bouhentala-2026-4-discussion-12] Second would be repeated, current mobility data across formal and informal border corridors and airports.[^walekhwa-2026-4-discussion-3][^walekhwa-2026-4-discussion-14] Organizations such as [Flowminder](https://www.flowminder.org/resources/publications-reports/drc-reports-publications) aim to fill this gap by providing more real-time data on mobility trends. Third would be destination readiness data tied to laboratory access, IPC, isolation, and contact tracing, because sustained-transmission risk depends on response performance, not just importation pressure.[^chamla-2026-discussion-10]

### Open questions

Several analyses call for iterative updating rather than one-off estimates. Chamla et al. name future integration of line lists, contact-tracing data, and dynamic mobility estimates, while Walekhwa et al. call for weekly updating with new DTM data and case counts.[^chamla-2026-discussion-13][^walekhwa-2026-4-discussion-14] Bouhentala & Kadir leave unresolved $R_t$, serial interval, onset-to-isolation delay, household secondary attack rates, and health-zone growth because public aggregate data were insufficient.[^bouhentala-2026-4-discussion-12] There is also convergence on the need to refine biological parameters for BDBV specifically, particularly infectious period and serial interval.[^walekhwa-2026-4-discussion-15]

### What this means for the response

The evidence suggests the operational priority can be thought of as a two-axis map, not a single risk score: Uganda and South Sudan sit high on importation risk, but Uganda's experience shows importation and healthcare-associated secondary transmission need not become sustained community transmission when detection, isolation, contact management, and care function well.[^bouhentala-2026-4-discussion-9] South Sudan is repeatedly flagged because its importation probability is high and preparedness gaps could make secondary spread larger than in Uganda.[^chamla-2026-discussion-10] For DRC, the evidence suggests maintaining attention beyond the eastern hotspots, including Kinshasa and other urban health zones where amplification potential is high despite distance from the hotspots.[^luvande-2026-body-17] For intercontinental destinations, the evidence suggests focused preparedness, including travel-history recording, diagnostics, isolation capacity, and occupational-exposure protocols, rather than broad border closures, because historical exportation outside Africa is rare and mostly occupational or response-linked.[^van-zandvoort-2026-discussion-10][^bouhentala-2026-abstract-4]

### Bouhentala & Kadir (2026) Epidemiological Analysis of the 2026 Bundibugyo Virus Disease Outbreak and Rapid Risk Assessment for North Africa and Europe

[Read the report](https://www.medrxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.19.26358411v1)

### Overview

This analysis assesses the risk of introduction and onward transmission of the 2026 BDBV outbreak into North Africa and Europe, using publicly available aggregate surveillance data synthesized through mid-July 2026. Risk was assessed across four dimensions: introduction likelihood, conditional onward-transmission likelihood, impact, and confidence.[^bouhentala-2026-abstract-1] Introduction likelihood was assessed as very low to low for North Africa and very low for the general European population, with delayed recognition in routine healthcare identified as the principal scenario for limited secondary transmission.[^bouhentala-2026-abstract-2]

### Estimate

This analysis does not produce a quantitative outbreak-size estimate for DRC. It reports observed surveillance counts as descriptive figures rather than model-derived estimates. As of 15 July 2026, the DRC had reported 2,124 laboratory-confirmed cases, 828 deaths among confirmed cases, 390 reported recoveries, and 725 patients hospitalised in isolation, across 46 of 140 health zones in five provinces.[^bouhentala-2026-3-results-4] The headline epidemiological trend finding is a notification-rate ratio: the aggregate rate was 35.9 per day during 2–29 June (970 notifications over 27 days; exact counting interval 33.7–38.3) and 47.4 per day during 1–15 July (664 over 14 days; 43.9–51.2), yielding a July-to-June notification-rate ratio of 1.32 (counting-model 95% interval 1.20–1.46).[^bouhentala-2026-3-results-5] For importation risk, the analysis cites an external ECDC estimate rather than producing its own: ECDC estimated approximately one importation per 23,000–24,000 travellers from the principal outbreak region (90% uncertainty interval one per 13,000–54,000), and under a hypothetical scenario of 100 travellers during 11–25 June, estimated a 0.45% probability of at least one importation (90% uncertainty interval 0.20%–0.85%).[^bouhentala-2026-3-results-6]

### Methods

**Rapid public-health risk assessment using aggregate surveillance data:** This was a rapid public-health analysis of publicly available aggregate surveillance data and official risk assessments, drawing on WHO Disease Outbreak News, WHO/IHR statements and technical guidance, ECDC surveillance and modelling reports, WHO regional offices, national ministry-of-health dashboards, and peer-reviewed primary studies for historical BDBV outbreaks, viral persistence, and candidate countermeasures.[^bouhentala-2026-2-methods-4] Airline sources were used only to establish the existence of plausible routes and not to estimate passenger volumes.[^bouhentala-2026-2-methods-5] This approach covers DRC nationally (with provincial detail for Ituri) as the source population, and North Africa and Europe as destination geographies.

**Interval-specific notification-rate analysis:** Cumulative confirmed DRC counts were indexed by reporting date rather than symptom-onset date, and interval-specific average notification rates were calculated as the change in cumulative confirmed cases divided by the number of days between reports, with exact 95% Poisson counting intervals calculated using the Garwood method.[^bouhentala-2026-2-methods-6] The notification-rate ratio comparing 1–15 July with 2–29 June was calculated on the log scale using SE[log(RR)] = √(1/C₁ + 1/C₀).[^bouhentala-2026-2-methods-7] No reproduction number was estimated because onset-date line-list data were unavailable.[^bouhentala-2026-2-methods-8]

**Structured qualitative risk rating:** Risk was separated into four dimensions — introduction likelihood, conditional onward-transmission likelihood after an importation, impact if detection was delayed, and confidence in the judgement — with categories treated as comparative reference classes rather than calibrated probabilities; boundary ratings such as 'very low to low' were retained when the evidence did not support a more precise classification.[^bouhentala-2026-2-methods-9] The ratings were calibrated against WHO's 6 June assessment, which classified risk as very high in the DRC, high in Uganda and bordering countries, low in the remainder of the WHO African Region, and low globally.[^bouhentala-2026-2-methods-10]

### Assumptions

**Reporting-date indexing and its limitations:** The Poisson counting intervals describe counting variation only and do not incorporate systematic uncertainty from backlogs, under-ascertainment, changing testing, or reporting delays.[^bouhentala-2026-2-methods-13] The observed increase in notification rates cannot be interpreted as a direct measure of biological acceleration, as WHO noted that expanded surveillance, testing, and diagnostic capacity contributed to rising reported counts, and newly reported cases could include backlogs.[^bouhentala-2026-3-results-11]

**Risk ratings as comparative reference classes rather than calibrated probabilities:** Risk categories are comparative reference classes rather than calibrated probabilities.[^bouhentala-2026-2-methods-14] Passenger-volume and confidential readiness data were unavailable[^bouhentala-2026-3-results-12], so country ratings rest on route availability, occupational exposure patterns, and qualitative preparedness indicators rather than quantified traveller volumes.

**Transmission requires direct contact:** A recognised symptomatic case can usually be contained because transmission requires direct contact with blood or other body fluids, contaminated materials, or the body of a deceased case rather than ordinary casual proximity.[^bouhentala-2026-4-discussion-1] This structural assumption underpins the low conditional sustained-transmission rating for North Africa and Europe.

**Border screening ineffectiveness during incubation:** An incubating traveller can cross a border while asymptomatic because people are not considered infectious before symptom onset and the incubation period can extend to 21 days; temperature screening may detect some symptomatic travellers but cannot exclude infection during incubation.[^bouhentala-2026-4-discussion-2]

**Itinerary concealment risk:** A final departure airport may conceal earlier travel to eastern DRC; triage therefore requires a complete 21-day itinerary and exposure history.[^bouhentala-2026-3-results-13]

**Cumulative doubling time not treated as a transmission signal:** Arithmetic cumulative doubling times were treated as descriptive properties of the cumulative curve and not as evidence of changes in transmission.[^bouhentala-2026-2-methods-15]

### Limitations

**Dependence on public aggregate data:** The main limitation is dependence on public aggregate data; reporting dates do not represent symptom onset, and the analysis could not estimate time-varying reproduction numbers, serial intervals, onset-to-isolation delays, household secondary-attack rates, health-zone-specific growth, or intervention effects.[^bouhentala-2026-4-discussion-3]

**Incomplete auxiliary inputs for importation assessment:** Passenger volumes, traveller occupations, deployment rosters, medevac activity, and country-specific laboratory and referral readiness were incomplete; country ratings were therefore comparative judgements rather than calibrated probabilities.[^bouhentala-2026-4-discussion-4]

**Counting intervals understating true uncertainty:** Notification rates remain composite measures of infection, care seeking, alert investigation, specimen transport, laboratory capacity, retrospective death investigation, and reporting; the exact Poisson intervals are therefore deliberately described as counting-model intervals rather than complete epidemiological uncertainty intervals.[^bouhentala-2026-4-discussion-5]

**CFR interpretation caution:** The rise in confirmed-case CFR is compatible with maturation of outcomes among previously active cases, retrospective confirmation of deaths, delayed presentation, preferential detection of severe disease, delayed recovery reporting, and changes in testing or death investigation; it should not be interpreted as a time-resolved measure of clinical virulence.[^bouhentala-2026-3-results-14]

**Unresolved status discrepancy in DRC counts:** The published status totals did not fully reconcile — 828 deaths, 390 recoveries, and 725 hospitalised patients summed to 1,943, leaving 181 of 2,124 confirmed cases outside those categories — and public reports did not define the status of these cases; potential explanations include pending admission or outcome reconciliation, transfers, and reporting lags, but no category could be assigned without line-list data.[^bouhentala-2026-3-results-15]

**Time-stamped analysis:** The analysis was time-stamped and may be superseded by subsequent official reports.[^bouhentala-2026-4-discussion-6]

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:** Public surveillance reports from WHO, ECDC, Africa CDC, national ministries of health, and peer-reviewed sources were synthesised through 15–17 July 2026.[^bouhentala-2026-abstract-3] The main DRC trajectory used laboratory-confirmed cases and deaths among confirmed cases; suspected, probable, and confirmed categories were not summed across reports because individuals and deaths can be reclassified as investigations progress.[^bouhentala-2026-2-methods-11] Uganda's official dashboard as of 17 July reported 20 confirmed cases, two confirmed deaths, 18 recoveries, and no current admissions.[^bouhentala-2026-3-results-7] WHO's jurisdictional accounting on 17 July comprised 2,145 confirmed cases globally: 2,124 in the DRC, 20 in Uganda, and one in France, with 830 confirmed-case deaths.[^bouhentala-2026-3-results-8]

**Auxiliary data:** Peer-reviewed primary studies were used for historical BDBV outbreaks, viral persistence, and candidate countermeasures.[^bouhentala-2026-2-methods-12] Airline sources were used only to establish the existence of plausible routes and not to estimate passenger volumes.[^bouhentala-2026-2-methods-5] ECDC Monte Carlo importation summaries (approximately one importation per 23,000–24,000 travellers, with 90% uncertainty interval one per 13,000–54,000) were referenced as separately generated estimates and were not treated as exact reciprocals.[^bouhentala-2026-3-results-9][^bouhentala-2026-3-results-10]

### Gashema & Muvunyi (2026) Can stochastic modelling predict cross-border spread of the Bundibugyo Ebola virus outbreak in Africa?

[Read the report](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/laninf/article/PIIS1473-3099(26)00352-X/fulltext)

### Overview

A modelling study by Dick Chamla and colleagues uses fitted stochastic SEIRD (susceptible, exposed, infectious, recovered, deceased) ensemble modelling to leverage limited data to predict cross-border outbreak spread and inform regional preparedness.[^gashema-2026-1] The analysis estimates the probability of regional spillover to neighbouring countries, finding a high probability of spread to Uganda (94.2%) and South Sudan (69.3%), while projected risks for Rwanda (8.6%) and Burundi (2.0%) remain comparatively low.[^gashema-2026-2] By linking outbreak dynamics with spillover risk, the analysis moves beyond forecasting disease burden toward informing operational preparedness.[^gashema-2026-3]

### Estimate

The central estimates of regional spillover probability are: Uganda 94.2%, South Sudan 69.3%, Rwanda 8.6%, and Burundi 2.0%.[^gashema-2026-2] These are presented as point estimates of spillover probability rather than case counts or incidence figures. No uncertainty bounds, confidence intervals, or sensitivity ranges around these probabilities are reported in this document. No outbreak size estimate (case count or incidence) is provided.

### Methods

**Fitted stochastic SEIRD ensemble modelling:** The analysis describes a stochastic susceptible, exposed, infectious, recovered, and deceased (SEIRD) ensemble modelling approach developed by Chamla and colleagues, fitted to limited outbreak data to predict cross-border outbreak spread.[^gashema-2026-1] The method produces country-level spillover probability estimates for Uganda, South Sudan, Rwanda, and Burundi. The method is described only at a high level in this commentary; no calibration procedure, specific parameter values, or detailed description of how mobility or epidemiological inputs are incorporated are provided in the document reviewed here.

### Assumptions

**Homogeneous mixing and uniform intervention effectiveness:** The stochastic models assume homogeneous population mixing and uniform intervention effectiveness, potentially overlooking important spatial, health-care, and socioeconomic heterogeneities that shape transmission dynamics.[^gashema-2026-7]

**Data quality and mobility approximations:** Projections are constrained by the quality of early surveillance data and approximated mobility patterns, particularly in low- or limited-resource settings where data gaps are common.[^gashema-2026-6]

**Static behavioural assumptions:** The models are limited in their ability to capture behavioural adaptation and community engagement, including changes in risk perception, health-care-seeking behaviour, intervention uptake, and public trust, all of which can substantially influence outbreak trajectories.[^gashema-2026-8]

**Detection delay:** An estimated 6-week interval between the probable index case and formal outbreak declaration is noted, representing an undetected transmission window that shapes the model's starting conditions.[^gashema-2026-9]

### Limitations

**Incomplete information during evolving outbreaks:** Models developed during unfolding outbreaks inevitably operate under conditions of incomplete information, evolving surveillance systems, and changing response capacities, which can affect their ability to accurately predict transmission dynamics. Accordingly, these models are not intended to provide a precise forecast, but rather to identify reasonable transmission trajectories and inform preparedness decisions.[^gashema-2026-10]

**Homogeneous mixing assumption:** Stochastic models assume homogeneous population mixing and uniform intervention effectiveness, potentially overlooking important spatial, health-care, and socioeconomic heterogeneities that shape transmission dynamics.[^gashema-2026-7]

**Data quality and mobility data gaps:** Projections are further constrained by the quality of early surveillance data and approximated mobility patterns, particularly in low- or limited-resource settings where data gaps are common.[^gashema-2026-6]

**Behavioural dynamics not captured:** The models remain restricted in their ability to capture behavioural adaptation and community engagement, including changes in risk perception, health-care-seeking behaviour, intervention uptake, and public trust, all of which can substantially influence outbreak trajectories.[^gashema-2026-8]

**Scenario-based estimates requiring continuous refinement:** These limitations underscore the importance of interpreting model outputs as scenario-based estimates that require continuous refinement as more comprehensive epidemiological, mobility, and behavioural data become available.[^gashema-2026-11]

### Data sources

The document does not specify the primary outbreak data sources or auxiliary data inputs used in the Chamla and colleagues modelling study in sufficient detail to enumerate them. The study is described as integrating epidemiological, laboratory, and mobility data into real-time risk assessments.[^gashema-2026-5] Projections are noted to be constrained by early surveillance data and approximated mobility patterns, particularly in low- or limited-resource settings where data gaps are common,[^gashema-2026-6] but specific dataset names, time periods covered, or sample sizes are not provided in this commentary.

### Chamla et al. (2026) Size of the 2026 Ebola outbreak and risk of cross-border spillover from Bundibugyo virus in Ituri Province, DR Congo, and its implications for preparedness: a recalibrated stochastic modelling study

[Read the report](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/laninf/article/PIIS1473-3099(26)00320-8/fulltext)

### Overview

This analysis aims to establish a short-term epidemic trajectory of the 2026 BDBV outbreak and quantify the probability of cross-border spillover into neighbouring countries at elevated importation risk, to guide preparedness priorities. It uses a stochastic SEIRD ensemble model calibrated to laboratory-confirmed DRC cases, anchored at 598 cumulative confirmed cases on 8 June 2026, linked to a daily-hazard spillover model estimating importation probability for Uganda, South Sudan, Rwanda, and Burundi over a 12-week horizon.[^chamla-2026-summary-1][^chamla-2026-summary-2] Under the central scenario (R0 = 1.71), the model projects a median of 990 cumulative confirmed cases by week 12 (24 June 2026; 90% PI 709 to 1,293) and 174 deaths, with cross-border importation probabilities of 94.2% for Uganda, 69.3% for South Sudan, 8.6% for Rwanda, and 2.0% for Burundi.[^chamla-2026-summary-3][^chamla-2026-summary-4]

### Estimate

**Central scenario (R0 = 1.71):** The ensemble projects a median of 648 cumulative confirmed cases by week 10 (8 June 2026; 50% PI 560 to 732), rising to 990 by week 12 (24 June 2026; 50% PI 858 to 1,141 and 90% PI 709 to 1,293), with a projected median of 174 deaths at week 12.[^chamla-2026-results-2] By week 24, the central scenario projects a median of 8,210 cumulative confirmed cases (50% PI 6,636 to 10,287 and 90% PI 5,063 to 13,498) and approximately 1,440 deaths if transmission is merely sustained.[^chamla-2026-results-3]

### Methods

**Stochastic SEIRD ensemble model:** A discrete-time stochastic SEIRD (susceptible, exposed, infectious, recovered, dead) framework using binomial draws under standard mass-action incidence, applied to the affected health zones in Ituri and North Kivu provinces. No vaccinated compartment is included, as no licensed BDBV-specific vaccine exists.[^chamla-2026-methods-3][^chamla-2026-methods-4] Three transmissibility scenarios are modelled (low R0 = 1.42; central R0 = 1.71; high R0 = 2.08), with response-intensity reductions applied from 15 May 2026 of 20% (low), 25% (central), and 15% (high).[^chamla-2026-methods-5][^chamla-2026-methods-6] Calibration was performed by simulation filtering (approximate rejection sampling, not formal Bayesian inference), accepting replicates if cumulative laboratory-confirmed cases at day 68 fell within ±30% of 598 (range 419 to 777); 400 calibration-passing replicates per scenario were retained.[^chamla-2026-methods-7]

**Daily-hazard spillover model:** Importation risk to Uganda, South Sudan, Rwanda, and Burundi was modelled as a daily Poisson hazard driven by calibrated infectious prevalence, country-specific cross-border mobility, point-of-entry detection sensitivity from WHO Joint External Evaluation assessments, and travel-while-infectious parameters (Uganda 12%; South Sudan 15%; Rwanda and Burundi 8%), with full equations and parameter values given in appendix 3.[^chamla-2026-methods-8] The importation probability for each country is computed as 1 − exp(cumulative 12-week hazard), yielding the probability of at least one importation over the horizon.[^chamla-2026-methods-9] Sensitivity analyses examined travel-while-infectious (5% to 25%), reporting completeness (0.60 to 0.90), and catchment size (250,000 to 500,000).[^chamla-2026-methods-10]

### Assumptions

**Transmissibility (R0):** Three scenarios span published BDBV estimates from the 2007 Uganda and 2012 DR Congo outbreaks: low R0 = 1.42, central R0 = 1.71, and high R0 = 2.08.[^chamla-2026-methods-5][^chamla-2026-methods-12] The confirmed-case trajectory is described as most consistent with the central scenario.[^chamla-2026-summary-8]

**Delay distributions:** The latent period is fixed at 9.7 days and the infectious period at 5.0 days, in line with the broader filovirus literature.[^chamla-2026-methods-13]

**Case-fatality ratio:** Drawn from a uniform distribution (0.15 to 0.25), centred on the observed confirmed-case ratio of approximately 19% (115 of 598 as of 8 June 2026) and consistent with the historical BDBV range.[^chamla-2026-methods-14]

**Reporting fraction:** Set at 1.0 for laboratory-confirmed cases, on the basis that laboratory-confirmed cases are by definition ascertained; no separate suspected-case reporting fraction is assumed.[^chamla-2026-methods-16]

**Response effectiveness:** The model incorporates a transmission reduction factor representing the combined effects of IPC measures, community engagement, safe and dignified burial, and contact tracing, with reduction factors varying by scenario to reflect differing operational effectiveness from the date of outbreak declaration onward.[^chamla-2026-methods-17] The response-intensity factor was applied uniformly from 15 May, though real-world response effectiveness varies by place, time, access, and community acceptance.[^chamla-2026-discussion-1]

**Spatial structure:** The model assumes a homogeneously mixing catchment of 350,000 across seven affected health zones; spatial heterogeneity is not captured, which is stated as a limitation.[^chamla-2026-methods-18]

**Cross-border spillover parameters:** Importation hazard is driven by calibrated infectious prevalence, country-specific cross-border mobility, WHO Joint External Evaluation–derived point-of-entry detection sensitivity, and travel-while-infectious parameters fixed at 12% for Uganda, 15% for South Sudan, and 8% for Rwanda and Burundi. **Calibration procedure:** Simulation filtering (approximate rejection sampling) is used rather than formal Bayesian inference; the ±30% tolerance window was chosen pragmatically given residual data uncertainty.[^chamla-2026-methods-19][^chamla-2026-methods-20]

### Limitations

**Spatial homogeneity and model structure:** Homogeneous mixing oversimplifies spatial heterogeneity across health zones; healthcare-worker transmission was not separately modelled; and response intensity was applied uniformly across the catchment rather than varying by subgeography or population group.[^chamla-2026-discussion-2]

**Single-point calibration anchor:** The model is anchored on a single recent confirmed-case datum (598 on 8 June 2026), making the fit sensitive to that anchor value.[^chamla-2026-discussion-2]

**Informal inference procedure:** Simulation filtering is not formal inference, and informal cross-border mobility estimates are imprecise.[^chamla-2026-discussion-2]

**Response uniformity:** The response-intensity factor was applied uniformly from 15 May, but real-world response effectiveness varies by place, time, access, and community acceptance. Live mobility data and behavioural adaptation were not incorporated.[^chamla-2026-discussion-3]

**Surveillance and reporting dynamics:** Post-declaration case-finding surges probably contributed to rapid apparent growth between 15 and 19 May; the authors addressed this by lowering the reporting fraction and widening the calibration window, but residual confounding between transmission intensity and surveillance intensity remains.[^chamla-2026-discussion-4]

**Early data quality:** Calibration relied on early-phase surveillance counts from the first weekly WHO External Situation Report (data as of 18 May), where the cumulative suspected-case figure of 528 was only partially resolved into 35 laboratory-confirmed cases, and the death count of 132 reflected surveillance signals rather than laboratory-confirmed mortality alone. Misclassification, delayed laboratory throughput, and ongoing active case finding could each shift the calibration anchor in either direction.[^chamla-2026-discussion-5]

**Exploratory rather than definitive projections:** The projections require iterative recalibration as new data emerge, and should be interpreted as an early situational assessment rather than a definitive forecast.[^chamla-2026-discussion-6][^chamla-2026-discussion-7]

**Absence of sex and gender disaggregation:** Individual-level sex and gender data were not available in the aggregate surveillance reports, making disaggregated analysis by sex or gender impossible.[^chamla-2026-discussion-8]

### Walekhwa et al. (2026) Network-based modelling of Bundibugyo Ebola virus disease importation and spread in Uganda using Displacement Tracking Matrix flow data

### Overview

This analysis aims to quantify district-level importation and spread risk of the 2026 Bundibugyo virus disease outbreak in Uganda, originating from the ongoing DRC epidemic, in order to inform where surveillance and response resources should be concentrated. It constructs a data-driven directed weighted mobility network from IOM Displacement Tracking Matrix flows (11,245 observed movements, 15–24 May 2026) and the 2024 Uganda census, then runs a stochastic metapopulation SEIR model over 90 days across 135 Ugandan districts and two DRC provinces.[^walekhwa-2026-abstract-1] Under the containment scenario, the model projects a median of 22 cumulative cases (95% CrI: 22–24) and 2 deaths concentrated entirely in Kampala over 90 days, with all other 134 districts projecting a median of 0 cases, and no statistically significant reduction from adding non-pharmaceutical interventions at any compliance level.[^walekhwa-2026-abstract-2]

### Estimate

Under the containment scenario (effective R = 0.35), the model projected a median of 22 cumulative cases (95% CrI: 22–24) and 2 deaths (95% CrI: 2–4) over 90 days, all concentrated in Kampala, with 3 HCW infections and 0 HCW deaths; 95% credible intervals overlapped substantially across compliance scenarios, indicating no statistically significant difference between them.[^walekhwa-2026-3-results-1]

The three compliance scenarios corresponded to the following parameterisations: containment (effective R₀ = 0.35, mobility scale 0.05), moderate compliance (R₀ = 0.40, mobility scale 0.08), and low compliance (R₀ = 0.45, mobility scale 0.12).[^walekhwa-2026-2-methods-12] Across all three scenarios, the median total case count remained 22, with no scenario producing a statistically distinct outcome.

### Methods

**Directed weighted mobility network:** Both undirected and directed weighted networks were built from DTM flow data using the igraph package in R; for the directed network, original flow directions were preserved to allow separate computation of in-strength (import risk) and out-strength (export risk) for each node, covering all 135 Ugandan districts and two DRC provinces.[^walekhwa-2026-2-methods-4] To impute 12-month movement estimates from the 10-day observed flows, observed weights were multiplied by 36.5, and uncertainty from the limited sampling window was assessed via bootstrap resampling with 1,000 replicates.[^walekhwa-2026-2-methods-5]

**Stochastic metapopulation SEIR model:** A discrete-time stochastic SEIR model is maintained at the node (district) level, with five compartments — Susceptible, Exposed, Infectious, Recovered, Dead — a latent period of 7.5 days, and an infectious period of 8.5 days.[^walekhwa-2026-2-methods-6] Pre-symptomatic transmission is modelled by setting the relative infectiousness of exposed individuals to 0.5 compared to infectious individuals, based on estimates from early EVD outbreaks and the empirical observation that six secondary cases in Kampala tested positive while under quarantine.[^walekhwa-2026-2-methods-7] The baseline transmission rate was derived from R₀ = 2.76, calibrated to the early growth of the outbreak (16 cases in 21 days).[^walekhwa-2026-2-methods-8] DRC provinces were modelled as explicit nodes with their own SEIR dynamics, recalibrated to actual reported data as of May 25, 2026 (381 confirmed cases, 2,987 exposed contacts, and 64 deaths).[^walekhwa-2026-2-methods-9] The model was run for three NPI compliance scenarios (20%, 40%, 60%) with 1,000 stochastic simulations each. The 7-1-7 agile response was explicitly modelled with dynamic contact tracing coverage (40% → 70% → 85%).[^walekhwa-2026-abstract-5]

**Sobol global sensitivity analysis:** To quantify the influence of parameter uncertainty on total cumulative case count, a global sensitivity analysis using the Sobol method was performed, with first-order indices measuring variance explained by each parameter alone and total-order indices measuring variance including all interactions.[^walekhwa-2026-2-methods-10] The design used quasi-random Sobol sequences with N = 500 samples (6,500 model evaluations total), with each parameter set averaged over 3 stochastic replicates to reduce noise, and Sobol indices estimated with 200 bootstrap replicates for 95% confidence intervals.[^walekhwa-2026-2-methods-11]

**Mobility data representativeness and scaling:** The DTM flow data covering a 10-day period are assumed to be representative of average daily movement patterns after scaling by 36.5 to impute annual estimates.[^walekhwa-2026-2-methods-17] Movement is assumed independent each day (no memory), with only exposed and infectious individuals moving, and the probability of moving identical for both compartments.[^walekhwa-2026-2-methods-18] The analysis acknowledges that movements through unofficial crossings or after the outbreak escalated may not be captured, a limitation common to mobility-based epidemiological studies.[^walekhwa-2026-4-discussion-1]

**Homogeneous mixing within districts:** The homogeneous mixing assumption within districts likely overestimates transmission in low-density areas and underestimates it in high-density slums.[^walekhwa-2026-4-discussion-2]

### Limitations

**Homogeneous mixing within districts:** The homogeneous mixing assumption within districts likely overestimates transmission in low-density areas and underestimates it in high-density slums.[^walekhwa-2026-4-discussion-2]

**Effective-distance airline importation model:** Potential airline-mediated importation risk was projected using a hazard-based model informed by contemporary airline transportation networks and a hazard parameter calibrated from the 2013–2016 West African Ebola virus disease epidemic, one of the few EBOD outbreaks with documented long-distance airline-mediated international exportations.[^kinoshita-2026-abstract-4] A directed, weighted air-traffic network was constructed using airport-level origin-destination passenger-flow data from OAG for May 2024, comprising 11,264 airports, 569,741 routes, and 329,326,078 passengers.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-4] The shortest-path effective distance from FIH to each destination airport was computed using Dijkstra's algorithm on the directed network.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-5] The arrival hazard at each destination airport was modelled as λⱼ = θ/Dⱼ (where θ = 0.19, the primary hazard parameter), and the arrival probability within t days was computed as pⱼ(t) = 1 − exp(−tλⱼ).[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-6] Country-level arrival risk was summarized as the maximum airport-level probability within each country.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-7] Sensitivity analyses tested θ = {0.06, 0.32} based on the 95% confidence interval of the historical parameter estimate.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-8] An additional sensitivity analysis varied the effective-distance scaling parameter δ using epidemic growth rates r = {0.034, 0.05, 0.1} estimated as of 17 May 2026.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-9]

### Assumptions

**Transferability of historical hazard parameter:** The model assumes that the relationship between effective distance and EVD importation observed during the 2013–2016 West African epidemic is transferable to the current BVD outbreak. **Effective-distance scaling:** In the main analysis, the distance-scaling parameter δ is set to 1, assuming each additional travel step contributes one unit of distance (the Brockmann-Helbing scale), which has been empirically shown to predict global spread patterns of infectious diseases.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-17] An alternative δ formulation incorporating the epidemic growth rate and outbound passenger flow from FIH is tested in sensitivity analysis, but the authors note this increases uncertainty.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-18]

**Hazard parameter sensitivity:** The primary hazard value θ = 0.19 is varied to {0.06, 0.32} (the 95% CI bounds of the historical estimate) in sensitivity analyses. These sensitivity runs change absolute arrival probabilities but broadly preserve the overall geographic pattern, because effective distance determines the spatial pattern while the hazard parameter mainly scales the probabilities.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-8][^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-19]

**Static airline network:** The estimates rely on 2024 airline network data and therefore may not capture subsequent changes in routes, passenger volumes, airspace restrictions, or travel advisories, including those affecting routes through Russia and the Middle East.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-20]

**No post-PHEIC interventions:** The projections do not explicitly account for post-PHEIC interventions, enhanced screening, or behavioural changes.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-21]

**Source airport scope:** The analysis models global air travel originating from FIH as the primary source, treating EBB only in sensitivity analysis, and does not explicitly account for land-based spread within DRC or cross-border movements to neighbouring countries.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-3]

**Importation versus transmission:** Estimated arrival probability is interpreted as importation risk rather than the risk of local transmission after arrival, on the basis that EBOD requires direct contact with infectious body fluids or contaminated materials, and that timely detection, isolation, contact tracing, and infection prevention and control can reduce onward transmission.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-22]

### Limitations

**Reliance on a borrowed, cross-pathogen hazard parameter:** Because no airline-mediated importation had been reported as of 28 May 2026, the hazard parameter was estimated from the 2013–2016 West African EVD epidemic rather than from current BVD data, and the model assumes this historical relationship is transferable to the present outbreak.[^kinoshita-2026-airline-flow-based-network-and-effective-distance-for-23]

### Overview

### Estimate

This analysis does not estimate outbreak size (total cases, infections, or deaths). It quantifies conditional relative importation risk to international destinations, given that at least one international export occurs, and is intended to indicate surveillance priority ranking among international destinations by relative likelihood. Absolute likelihood of any international export, downstream local transmission, and outbreak burden at source are explicitly out of scope.[^epistorm-2026-executive-summary-7]

### Methods

**GLEAM mobility-based relative risk model:** This computational framework estimates, for each potential destination, the probability that an infected individual originating in the affected area travels there, conditional on at least one exported case occurring. The output is a relative risk distribution across all candidate destinations, normalised so that values represent the share of importation probability assigned to each location.[^epistorm-2026-methods-overview-1]

**Air travel and local mobility data integration:** Global air travel patterns are derived from IATA and OAG, providing passenger-level origin-destination flows between international airports. ### Assumptions

**Sociodemographic homogeneity:** The mobility model does not incorporate sociodemographic attributes of travellers — age, economic status, occupation, or pre-existing medical conditions — any of which may modulate both exposure risk at origin and care-seeking behaviour at destination. ### Overview

This analysis reviews the historical record of confirmed Ebola disease cases presenting outside Africa to contextualize the intercontinental spread risk posed by the 2026 Bundibugyo virus disease (BVD) outbreak in DRC. The authors conducted internet searches — both manual and AI-assisted — to identify all laboratory-confirmed Ebola disease cases presenting outside Africa, without any time limit.[^van-zandvoort-2026-ebola-disease-cases-outside-africa-1] The main finding is that intercontinental transmission risk remains low, based on the full historical record of such cases.[^van-zandvoort-2026-summary-1]

### Estimate

The analysis does not produce an outbreak size estimate for the 2026 BVD outbreak itself. Instead it reports importation risk metrics derived from the historical record.

The crude overall risk since 2000 is 0.81 cases outside Africa per 1,000 Ebola disease cases in a source outbreak (including medically evacuated, latent, and secondary cases); focusing on latent cases and their secondary cases only, the rate is 0.17 cases outside Africa per 1,000 source outbreak cases.[^van-zandvoort-2026-low-overall-risk-of-exportation-2] This represents the empirically observed risk across all countries outside Africa combined; per-country risks are smaller — for example, for the UK alone, the estimate is 0.03 latent cases per 1,000 source outbreak cases.[^van-zandvoort-2026-low-overall-risk-of-exportation-3]

From the time-varying analysis of the 2014–16 epidemic, exportation risk per reported source case decreased by 4.6% per week (95% CI: 1.7–7.4%; p = 0.0097), and was substantially lower after the epidemic peak (0.46; 95% CI: 0.17–0.73 exported cases per 1,000 source cases) than before it (1.7; 95% CI: 0.95–2.7 exported cases per 1,000 source cases).[^van-zandvoort-2026-decrease-in-exportation-risk-over-the-3]

### Methods

**Historical case review and crude risk calculation:** The authors identified and analysed all known Ebola disease cases outside Africa to contextualise intercontinental spread, using manual and AI-assisted internet searches of scientific articles, public health bulletins, and news reports.[^van-zandvoort-2026-summary-3] As a crude risk measure, they calculated the number of cases occurring outside Africa per 1,000 reported cases in the originating epidemic for outbreaks since 2000, producing separate estimates including and excluding medically evacuated cases.[^van-zandvoort-2026-low-overall-risk-of-exportation-1] This calculation applies globally across all destination countries outside Africa combined, with a UK-specific example provided at the national level.

**Time-varying Poisson regression:** For cases exported from the 2014–16 West African epidemic specifically, the authors conducted a time-varying risk analysis using Poisson regression of the weekly count of exported cases by country (Guinea, Liberia, Sierra Leone) against the number of cases reported that week and a linear time trend.[^van-zandvoort-2026-decrease-in-exportation-risk-over-the-1] This produced an estimate of how exported case counts scaled with source-country incidence, with no significant deviation from linear scaling found.[^van-zandvoort-2026-decrease-in-exportation-risk-over-the-2] This method applies to the 2014–16 epidemic as a historical comparator and is not applied directly to the 2026 BVD outbreak.

### Assumptions

**Linear scaling of exportation risk with source incidence:** The Poisson regression assumes that exported case counts scale linearly with weekly source-country incidence, an assumption the analysis found consistent with the 2014–16 data (estimated scaling: 0.98; 95% CI: 0.60–1.36; p = 0.86).[^van-zandvoort-2026-decrease-in-exportation-risk-over-the-2] This assumption underpins the use of crude per-1,000-cases risk ratios to project forward.

**Crude risk ratio as applicable across outbreaks:** The crude risk measure is calculated for Ebola disease outbreaks since the year 2000[^van-zandvoort-2026-low-overall-risk-of-exportation-4], implicitly assuming that the historical ratio of exported-to-source cases is informative for the current 2026 BVD outbreak. The analysis does not explicitly state whether this assumption holds given differences in travel patterns, response capacity, or the specific virus species involved.

**Distinction between medically evacuated and latent cases:** The analysis distinguishes between medically evacuated cases, who represent known risks manageable with strict biosecurity, and latent cases arriving via commercial flights who need to be diagnosed before isolation.[^van-zandvoort-2026-discussion-2] The risk ratios are computed separately for these two categories, with the latent-only figure treated as the more policy-relevant measure of uncontrolled importation risk.

**Completeness of historical case ascertainment:** Agreement among search methods and with a prior clinical review supports that the case list is likely complete, but potential under-ascertainment may arise from underrepresentation of public health bulletins not published in English, and from possible geographic biases in web-based search tools.[^van-zandvoort-2026-discussion-3]

### Limitations

**Potential under-ascertainment of exported cases:** Public health bulletins not published in English were underrepresented despite targeted native-language searches, and web-based search tools may have returned geographically biased results, potentially leading to missed cases.[^van-zandvoort-2026-discussion-4]

**Historical data may not reflect current outbreak conditions:** The analysis applies risk ratios derived primarily from the 2014–16 West African epidemic — a very large, prolonged epidemic with substantial international humanitarian response — to the 2026 BVD context. The authors note that the decrease in exportation risk over the 2014–16 epidemic is likely most consistent with improvement in infection prevention and control measures coinciding with the stepping-up of response efforts in late 2014[^van-zandvoort-2026-decrease-in-exportation-risk-over-the-5], but do not formally assess whether this dynamic applies to the current, structurally different outbreak.

**Per-country risks not estimated:** The crude risk estimate represents the empirically observed risk across all countries outside Africa; per-country risks are smaller[^van-zandvoort-2026-low-overall-risk-of-exportation-5], and the analysis provides only the aggregate outside-Africa figure plus a single UK example, without ranking or estimating risk for individual destination countries.

**Latent case detection depends on screening:** The four latent cases identified occurred among 300,000 screened travellers and were all asymptomatic at exit and entry screening; their eventual identification relied on screening protocols that included guidance on self-monitoring of symptoms.[^van-zandvoort-2026-discussion-5] The analysis does not assess what the latent case rate would have been in the absence of such protocols.

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:** The analysis identified 28 confirmed Ebola disease cases outside Africa in total, of which 27 occurred during the 2014–16 West African epidemic and one has occurred so far during the 2026 BVD epidemic.[^van-zandvoort-2026-ebola-disease-cases-outside-africa-6] Sources consulted included 27 scientific articles, 9 public health bulletins, 82 news articles, and 9 other sites.[^van-zandvoort-2026-ebola-disease-cases-outside-africa-7]

**Auxiliary data:** Weekly case counts for Guinea, Liberia, and Sierra Leone (total 28,592 confirmed, probable, and suspected cases) covering the 2014–16 epidemic were used as the source-country incidence series in the Poisson regression.[^van-zandvoort-2026-decrease-in-exportation-risk-over-the-4] The authors also reference the approximately 300,000 travellers screened by CDC-supported exit screening programmes in Guinea, Liberia, and Sierra Leone during the 2014–16 epidemic, as context for the four identified latent cases.[^van-zandvoort-2026-discussion-1] Findings were checked against existing lists of Ebola disease cases.[^van-zandvoort-2026-ebola-disease-cases-outside-africa-8]
