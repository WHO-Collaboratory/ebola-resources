Risk of Spread

Latest analysis data cutoff: 5 June 2026. Page refreshed: 9 July 2026.

At a glance

The modeled risk of international spread from the 2026 Bundibugyo virus disease outbreak suggests significant introduction risk to nearby regions, particularly Uganda and Rwanda. Five analyses, converge that mobility concentrates importation risk in connected regional corridors and air hubs, but the top destinations change with source geography and modeling scope. Evidence suggests targeting surveillance at high-probability importation pathways. Confidence is low-to-moderate because location rankings are more stable than the absolute probabilities assigned to them, but mobility patterns, case numbers, and detection levels contain uncertainty.

Synthesizing evidence¶

State of the evidence¶

The evidence base is thin but operationally useful: five independent analyses, all preprint or ad hoc reports, dated from 4 to 25 June 2026 with data cutoffs from 29 May to 5 June. They cover complementary questions rather than one shared endpoint: DRC health-zone introduction and amplification potential, Uganda district spread, conditional global importation ranking, and airline-mediated importation.[1][2][3][4][5] The strongest convergence is on rankings and pathways, not on point probabilities.

How the estimates compare¶

The ranking of locations by importation risk depends on the method applied. Epistorm characterizes the risk of international spread as primarily borne by nearby countries and international travel hubs, led by Tanzania, South Sudan, Kenya, and the UAE, with its top five city destinations accounting for roughly 74% of conditional international risk.[6] Kinoshita et al. instead identify major air hubs as highest risk — Belgium, France, South Africa, Kenya, and the UAE — because the model starts from airline effective distance from Kinshasa, while Fanelli et al. find France, Italy, and the UK persistently among the most exposed European countries unless the epicenter of the outbreak shifts to regional capitals.[7][8][9] Within DRC and Uganda, Luvande et al. identify eight DRC health zones with high probability of both importation and sustained-transmission, including in Kinshasa, Nord/Sud-Kivu, and Tshopo zones, while Walekhwa et al. project Uganda’s burden concentrating in Kampala and Wakiso rather than only at border districts.[10][11]

Key uncertainties and assumptions¶

The shared uncertainty is not whether mobility matters, but whether observed or historical mobility represents current mobility trends, especially informal crossings, airport travel, and behaviour change.[12][13] Sustained-transmission estimates also depend on case detection, isolation, contact tracing, and IPC.[14][15]

What would reduce uncertainty¶

First, verified information on exported cases, including date, source location, route, and detection timing would reduce uncertainty, because it would recalibrate both airline and land-border rankings.[16] Second would be current mobility data for informal land crossings, domestic/regional aviation after Bunia airport reopening, and key trade routes. Organizations like Flowminder aim to support the response effort by providing more real-time data on mobility trends.

What this means for the response¶

The evidence suggests surveillance should follow two tracks: (1) likely importation corridors and (2) places where an introduction could amplify outbreak size. For international importation, the near-term focus is regional, and secondarily air travel hubs; for sustained transmission, urban and highly connected inland centres remain important even when they are not closest to the outbreak.[17][18] For Uganda, the evidence suggests pairing border screening at high-risk importation hubs with inland readiness in Kampala and Wakiso, while recognizing that importation does not entail sustained transmission where detection and isolation are fast.[19][20]

Walekhwa et al. (2026) Network-based modelling of Bundibugyo Ebola virus disease importation and spread in Uganda using Displacement Tracking Matrix flow data and non-pharmaceutical intervention compliance scenarios¶

Read the report

Overview¶

This analysis estimates how the 2026 Bundibugyo virus disease (BVD) outbreak in Uganda, seeded by cross-border introductions from DRC, is likely to spread across Ugandan districts over a 90-day horizon. The authors built a data-driven directed mobility network from IOM Displacement Tracking Matrix flows and 2024 Uganda census data, then ran a stochastic metapopulation SEIR model across 135 Ugandan districts and two DRC provinces, incorporating pre-symptomatic transmission and movement of both exposed and infectious individuals.[2] The headline finding is that Uganda’s agile 7-1-7 response is projected to contain the outbreak to a median of 69–70 cases and 3 deaths over 90 days, with Kampala bearing the highest predicted burden (median 22 cases, 100% probability of exceeding 10 cases), and that additional non-pharmaceutical interventions produced no statistically significant reduction in cases.[21][22]

Estimate¶

The primary headline estimate, under the baseline agile 7-1-7 response with no additional NPIs, covers Uganda over 90 days from 5 June 2026. Under baseline mobility, the model projected a median of 69–70 cumulative cases (95% CrI: 57–98) and 3 deaths over 90 days.[23]

Across the three NPI compliance scenarios (20%, 40%, 60%), the estimates were nearly identical: 20% compliance: 70 cases (95% CrI: 58–98), 3 deaths (0–7), 11 HCW infections (9–15), 1 HCW death (1–2); 40% compliance: 70 cases (95% CrI: 58–95), 3 deaths (0–6), 11 HCW infections (9–15), 1 HCW death (1–2); 60% compliance: 69 cases (95% CrI: 57–95), 3 deaths (0–6), 11 HCW infections (9–15), 1 HCW death (1–2).[24] The 95% credible intervals overlapped substantially across compliance levels, indicating no statistically significant difference between compliance scenarios.[25]

At the district level (20% compliance scenario): Kampala had the highest predicted burden with a median of 22 cases (95% CrI: 20–43) and 100% probability of local outbreak (>10 cases); Wakiso followed with a median of 11 cases (95% CrI: 10–23) and an outbreak probability of 64.9%; border districts showed lower predictions: Bundibugyo (median 5 cases, 95% CrI: 4–11), Kasese (median 3 cases, 95% CrI: 3–8), Kisoro (median 2 cases, 95% CrI: 2–7), and Mbarara (median 2 cases, 95% CrI: 2–5); Mbale had a median of 1 case (95% CrI: 1–3).[11]

Methods¶

Data-driven directed weighted mobility network: The authors constructed a directed, weighted network representing human movement between DRC and Uganda and within Uganda. The network was built from IOM Displacement Tracking Matrix flows collected 15–24 May 2026 (11,245 observed movements) and the 2024 Uganda census (45.9 million people).[26] Both undirected and directed weighted networks were built from the DTM flow data, combining eight cross-border flow records (from DRC provinces Ituri and Nord-Kivu to six border districts) and 23 internal flows among Ugandan districts; the directed network preserved original flow directions to compute in-strength (import risk) and out-strength (export risk) separately.[27] Bootstrap resampling with 1,000 replicates was performed to quantify uncertainty in ten global network metrics due to the limited 10-day sampling window.[28]

Stochastic metapopulation SEIR model: A discrete-time stochastic SEIR model was run at the node (district) level for all 135 Ugandan districts plus two DRC provinces. Each node maintained five compartments (S, E, I, R, D); the latent period was set to 7.5 days and the infectious period to 8.5 days; pre-symptomatic transmission was modelled by setting relative infectiousness of exposed individuals to 0.5, based on estimates from early EVD outbreaks and the empirical observation that six early secondary cases in Kampala tested positive while under quarantine.[29] DRC provinces were modelled as explicit nodes with their own SEIR dynamics, recalibrated to reported data as of 25 May 2026 (381 confirmed cases, 2,987 exposed contacts, 64 deaths), with DRC response parameters representing a moderately delayed response; cross-border spread occurred naturally through the movement matrix rather than through a separate import process.[30] Five hundred stochastic simulations were run for each of three NPI compliance scenarios, and 1,000 simulations for the baseline, each covering 90 days from 5 June 2026.[31]

NPI compliance scenario analysis: Three compliance levels (20%, 40%, 60%) were tested for a multi-dimensional NPI model disaggregating community contact reduction, healthcare protection, and mobility restriction. NPIs included risk communication and standard operating procedures (targeting community contact reduction, baseline effectiveness 30%), avoidance of handshaking and enhanced hygiene (healthcare protection, 20%), and restrictions on mass gatherings and market closures (mobility reduction, 40%).[32]

Global Sobol sensitivity analysis: A variance-based Sobol sensitivity analysis was applied to identify which of 12 parameters most influenced total cumulative case count. The design used quasi-random Sobol sequences with N = 500 samples; for each parameter set, the output was averaged over 3 stochastic replicates, and Sobol indices were estimated with 200 bootstrap replicates to obtain 95% confidence intervals.[33]

Assumptions¶

Mobility data representativeness and scaling: The DTM flow data from the 10-day period are assumed to be representative of average daily movement patterns, after scaling by 36.5 to approximate annual flows.[34] Movement is assumed independent each day (no memory); only exposed and infectious individuals move, with equal movement probability for both compartments — an assumption made due to lack of detailed information on EVD status among travellers.[35]

Within-district mixing and spatial structure: Due to lack of district-specific mobility information, homogeneous mixing within districts is assumed, which the authors acknowledge likely overestimates transmission in low-density areas and underestimates it in high-density slums.[36]

Detection and isolation: A detection delay of 3 days is applied uniformly to all infected individuals, and quarantine after detection is assumed perfect — isolated individuals no longer transmit.[37] Reported incidence is the number of new cases on the previous day multiplied by a detection probability p_detect = 1.0, assuming all symptomatic cases are detected.[38]

Contact tracing coverage: Contact tracing coverage is dynamic: starting at 40% when cumulative cases are ≤3, rising to 70% when cases are 4–10, and reaching 85% when cumulative cases exceed 10.[39]

DRC epidemic independence: The DRC epidemic curve is assumed independent of Uganda’s interventions and drives importations solely through the movement matrix.[40] DRC response parameters are set to represent a moderately delayed response: detection delay 7 days, notification delay 3 days, response delay 5 days, contact tracing coverage 30%, detection probability 0.7, and no behavioural response (k = 1).[41]

R₀ and biological parameters: The baseline R₀ = 2.76 was calibrated to the early growth of the outbreak (16 cases in 21 days).[42] The case fatality rate (2/16 = 0.125) and HCW attack rate (3/16 = 0.188) are held constant throughout.[43] Sobol sensitivity ranges tested R₀ from 1.5 to 3.5 and CFR from 0.05 to 0.2.

Pre-symptomatic transmission: Pre-symptomatic transmission was modelled by setting the relative infectiousness of exposed individuals to 0.5 compared to infectious individuals, based on estimates from early EVD outbreaks and the empirical observation that six of the secondary cases in Kampala tested positive while under quarantine.[44]

Superspreading: New infections are drawn from a negative binomial distribution with dispersion parameter k_disp = 0.3 to capture superspreading.[45]

Behavioural response: The behavioural response exponent k = 3 and NPI intrinsic effectiveness values (30%, 20%, 40%) are assumed appropriate for the Bundibugyo strain and Ugandan context.[46] Compliance with NPIs is assumed constant over the 90 days and homogeneous across districts.[47]

Geographic scope of importations: No importations from countries other than DRC are considered.[48]

Limitations¶

Mobility data coverage and representativeness: The DTM data cover only a 10-day window and eight border crossing points; movements through unofficial crossings or after the outbreak escalated may not be captured, a limitation common to mobility-based epidemiological studies.[13] While annual estimates were imputed using time-dependent decay, real mobility patterns can change unpredictably.[49]

Within-district homogeneous mixing: The homogeneous mixing assumption within districts likely overestimates transmission in low-density areas and underestimates it in high-density slums.[50]

Sobol sensitivity quantitative precision: The Sobol confidence intervals for some parameters remained wide because the small outbreak size (median <70 cases) produces low output variance, making precise quantitative ranking unstable — hence the qualitative interpretation adopted by the authors.[51]

Behavioural response function: The behavioural response function lacks strain-specific validation, though low sensitivity indices partially mitigate this concern.[52]

Hospital and funeral transmission: Hospital and funeral transmission are not explicitly modelled — a limitation given that nosocomial transmission and unsafe burials have been identified as major amplifiers in Ebola outbreaks.[53]

Importations from countries other than DRC: Importations from neighbouring countries other than DRC were not considered, though the 79.9% importation risk estimated by Chalma et al. (2026) focused specifically on DRC-origin introductions.[54]

Data sources¶

Primary outbreak data: The Ministry of Health EVD situation report as of 5 June 2026 provided the initial outbreak conditions: 15 cumulative cases (plus 1 probable), 2 deaths, 5 recoveries, and 720 cumulative contacts listed (455 active under follow-up).[55] DRC dynamics were recalibrated using actual reported data as of 25 May 2026: 381 confirmed cases, 2,987 exposed contacts, and 64 deaths.[56]

Auxiliary data — mobility: IOM DTM Flow Monitoring report covering 15–24 May 2026 recorded cross-border movements from DRC into Uganda and internal movements among Ugandan districts at eight border crossing points, with 11,245 total movements; the percentage distribution across eight flow monitoring points was extracted (Ntoroko Main 28%, Mpondwe 18%, Busunga 18%, Bunagana 12%, Cyanika 8%, Goli 8%, Vurra 3%, Busanza 3%).[57][58] Internal movement flows were manually transcribed from the report’s schematic diagram (e.g., Kisoro–Kampala = 480, Kasese–Kampala = 420).[59]

Auxiliary data — demographic: The official 2024 Uganda Population and Housing Census final report (UBOS, December 2024) provided de facto population for each of the 135 districts, with a total national population of 45,905,417, drawn from Tables 2.4A–2.4D.[60] For DRC provinces, an estimated population of 1.5 million each was used for eastern high-risk provinces, sourced from USAID 2024.[61]

Auxiliary data — parameters from prior literature: The latent period (7.5 days) and infectious period (8.5 days) were drawn from Legrand et al. (2007) and Althaus (2014)/Riad et al. (2019).[62] The baseline transmission rate β was derived from R₀ = 2.76, calibrated to the early growth of the outbreak (16 cases in 21 days).[42] A 79.9% importation risk for EVD given the intensity of cross-border travel from DRC, estimated by Chalma et al. (2026), is cited as contextual prior work.[63]

Luvande, Iroungou, & Valdano (2026) Introduction and sustained-transmission risk across DRC health zones during the Bundibugyo virus disease outbreak¶

Read the report

Overview¶

This analysis estimates introduction risk and sustained-transmission potential for each health zone (HZ) across the DRC during the ongoing Bundibugyo virus disease outbreak, with the goal of identifying priority zones — including those far from currently affected areas — where rapid amplification could follow an introduction and where response efforts should be focused.[1] The approach applies a published method to rank all DRC health zones by their sustained-transmission potential, using population and mobility data to parameterise spatial transmission across a broad range of assumptions on disease transmissibility and contact patterns, then compares these ranks with introduction-risk ranks estimated from mobility data.[64] The main finding is that eight health zones combine both high introduction risk and high sustained-transmission potential: Binza Ozone and Binza Meteo in Kinshasa; Karisimbi in Nord-Kivu; Kadutu and Ibanda in Sud-Kivu; and Makiso-Kisangani, Kabondo, and Mangobo in Tshopo.[10]

Estimate¶

This analysis does not report outbreak size estimates (e.g., total case counts or incidence figures). Introduction risk was highest in health zones close to the reported foci — specifically in Ituri, Nord-Kivu, and Haut-Uele — consistent with expected mobility patterns, with non-negligible introduction risk also appearing in distant urban centres including the Kinshasa area.[65] High sustained-transmission potential was distributed broadly across DRC, including Kinshasa, Kasaï-Oriental, Kongo-Central, Tshopo, Nord-Kivu, Haut-Katanga, Sud-Kivu, Kasaï, and Kasaï-Central — several of which are far from the current outbreak.[66] Crossing the two rankings identified eight health zones combining high introduction risk and high sustained-transmission potential: Binza Ozone and Binza Meteo in Kinshasa; Karisimbi in Nord-Kivu; Kadutu and Ibanda in Sud-Kivu; and Makiso-Kisangani, Kabondo, and Mangobo in Tshopo.[10] No point probabilities, quantitative thresholds, or numerical uncertainty intervals are reported in the main text.

Methods¶

Sustained-transmission potential ranking: This method applies the approach developed in a previously published reference (Ref. 2) to estimate, for each DRC health zone, the potential for an introduced case to generate sustained transmission.[67] Population and mobility data are used to parameterise spatial transmission, and a broad range of assumptions on disease transmissibility and on how local and between-community contact patterns can be inferred from these data are tested.[68] Population data come from WorldPop and mobility data from FlowMinder.[69]

Introduction-risk ranking: Introduction risk is estimated separately for each DRC health zone using mobility data, and health zones are ranked by this measure.[70] Risk rankings — rather than raw risk estimates — are emphasised because they are robust across scenarios and assumptions, allowing them to inform action despite limited knowledge of transmissibility and contact patterns.[71]

The two rankings (sustained-transmission potential and introduction risk) are then crossed to identify health zones that score highly on both dimensions.[70] The method is described only briefly in the main text; full methodology, additional data sources, and additional results are stated to be in an Appendix.

Assumptions¶

Transmissibility and contact patterns: The analysis tests a broad range of assumptions on disease transmissibility and on how local and between-community contact patterns can be inferred from population and mobility data. Specific parameter values are not reported in the main text and are described as available in the Appendix.[68]

Ranking robustness: A structural assumption central to the analysis is that risk rankings — as opposed to raw risk estimates — are robust across scenarios and assumptions, and that this robustness justifies using rankings to inform action despite limited knowledge of transmissibility and contact patterns. This is stated as a key design choice rather than a tested sensitivity.[71]

Data availability: The analysis is explicit that it operates under limited data on human mobility, contact patterns, disease transmissibility, and tracing and isolation capacity; these constraints shape what can be estimated and drive the reliance on ranking rather than point estimates.[14]

Mobility and population data: Spatial transmission is parameterised using WorldPop population data and FlowMinder mobility data. No statement is made about the vintage, resolution, or representativeness of these datasets for the current outbreak context.[69]

Limitations¶

Limited input data: The analysis is explicit that it operates under limited data on human mobility, contact patterns, disease transmissibility, and tracing and isolation capacity, all of which are required to fully characterise introduction and sustained-transmission risk.[14]

Structural outbreak challenges not modelled: Insecurity, high population mobility, limited healthcare capacity, and the absence of a vaccine are cited as challenges to response. The analysis does not indicate whether these factors are formally incorporated into the model or treated as qualitative context.[72]

Heterogeneous preparedness: The analysis acknowledges heterogeneous healthcare access, accessibility constraints, and low-to-moderate insecurity outside Kinshasa as factors affecting response feasibility in identified high-risk zones, but these are discussed qualitatively rather than incorporated into the quantitative framework.[73]

Appendix-dependent detail: Methodology, additional data sources, and additional results are available only in the Appendix, which is not reproduced in the main document. The main text provides insufficient detail to assess specific parameter choices, calibration procedures, or sensitivity analyses.[74]

Data sources¶

Primary outbreak data: The analysis references the ongoing BVD outbreak in Ituri province, DRC, with confirmed cases in Nord-Kivu and Sud-Kivu noted as of June 4 as evidence of geographic spread beyond the initial focus. No surveillance line list, case count, or situation report data are described as direct model inputs in the main text.[75][76]

Auxiliary data: Population data are from WorldPop (worldpop.org) and mobility data are from FlowMinder (flowminder.org).[69] The sustained-transmission potential method is drawn from a previously published reference (Ref. 2), used as the methodological foundation.[67] Additional data sources are stated to be available in the Appendix, which is not reproduced in the main document.[74] No travel volume sample sizes, demographic inputs, or epidemiological parameters (e.g., CFR, serial interval) are reported in the main text.

Kinoshita, Suzuki, & Yoneoka (2026) Early assessment of potential airline-mediated importation risk during the 2026 DRC-Uganda Bundibugyo virus disease outbreak¶

Read the report

Overview¶

This analysis projects potential airline-mediated importation risk during the 2026 BVD outbreak in DRC and Uganda, using a contemporary airline network dataset and an importation hazard parameter calibrated from historical Ebola outbreak data.[4] The approach converts airline passenger-flow connectivity into country-level arrival probabilities over a defined time horizon. The main finding identifies major international hub countries — Belgium, France, South Africa, Kenya, and the United Arab Emirates — as the highest-probability destinations for potential importation until the end of June 2026.[7]

Estimate¶

This analysis produces importation probability estimates rather than outbreak size estimates. Airport-level arrival probabilities, interpreted as the probability of at least one airline-mediated importation until the end of June 2026 (t = 44), ranged from 0.247 to 0.937, with a mean of 0.431.[77] Higher arrival probabilities were concentrated among airports with shorter effective distances from FIH and stronger connectivity through major transit hubs: for example, 0.889 at Brussels Airport, 0.888 at Paris Charles de Gaulle Airport, and 0.871 at Johannesburg O.R. Tambo International Airport.[78] The highest country-level arrival probabilities were observed for Belgium, France, South Africa, Kenya, and the United Arab Emirates, with the top-ranked countries all exceeding 0.77; in sensitivity analyses the probabilities for these countries ranged from 0.38 to 0.98.[79] When the starting airport is changed to EBB (Uganda) in sensitivity analysis, the mean (standard deviation) of arrival probability ranged from 0.170 (0.049) to 0.616 (0.099).[80]

Methods¶

Effective-distance airline importation model: Potential airline-mediated importation risk was projected using a hazard-based model informed by contemporary airline transportation networks and a hazard parameter calibrated from the 2013–2016 West African Ebola virus disease epidemic, one of the few EBOD outbreaks with documented long-distance airline-mediated international exportations.[81] A directed, weighted air-traffic network was constructed using airport-level origin-destination passenger-flow data from OAG for May 2024, comprising 11,264 airports, 569,741 routes, and 329,326,078 passengers.[82] The shortest-path effective distance from FIH to each destination airport was computed using Dijkstra’s algorithm on the directed network.[83] The arrival hazard at each destination airport was modelled as λⱼ = θ/Dⱼ (where θ = 0.19, the primary hazard parameter), and the arrival probability within t days was computed as pⱼ(t) = 1 − exp(−tλⱼ).[84] Country-level arrival risk was summarized as the maximum airport-level probability within each country.[85] Sensitivity analyses tested θ = {0.06, 0.32} based on the 95% confidence interval of the historical parameter estimate.[86] An additional sensitivity analysis varied the effective-distance scaling parameter δ using epidemic growth rates r = {0.034, 0.05, 0.1} estimated as of 17 May 2026.[87]

Assumptions¶

Transferability of historical hazard parameter: The model assumes that the relationship between effective distance and EVD importation observed during the 2013–2016 West African epidemic is transferable to the current BVD outbreak. The estimated effective distance distribution was reported as very similar to that of the previous study, which the authors cite as supporting use of the same θ value on a comparable hazard scale.[88]

Effective-distance scaling: In the main analysis, the distance-scaling parameter δ is set to 1, assuming each additional travel step contributes one unit of distance (the Brockmann-Helbing scale), which has been empirically shown to predict global spread patterns of infectious diseases.[89] An alternative δ formulation incorporating the epidemic growth rate and outbound passenger flow from FIH is tested in sensitivity analysis, but the authors note this increases uncertainty.[90]

Hazard parameter sensitivity: The primary hazard value θ = 0.19 is varied to {0.06, 0.32} (the 95% CI bounds of the historical estimate) in sensitivity analyses. These sensitivity runs change absolute arrival probabilities but broadly preserve the overall geographic pattern, because effective distance determines the spatial pattern while the hazard parameter mainly scales the probabilities.[86][91]

Static airline network: The estimates rely on 2024 airline network data and therefore may not capture subsequent changes in routes, passenger volumes, airspace restrictions, or travel advisories, including those affecting routes through Russia and the Middle East.[92]

No post-PHEIC interventions: The projections do not explicitly account for post-PHEIC interventions, enhanced screening, or behavioural changes.[93]

Source airport scope: The analysis models global air travel originating from FIH as the primary source, treating EBB only in sensitivity analysis, and does not explicitly account for land-based spread within DRC or cross-border movements to neighbouring countries.[94]

Importation versus transmission: Estimated arrival probability is interpreted as importation risk rather than the risk of local transmission after arrival, on the basis that EBOD requires direct contact with infectious body fluids or contaminated materials, and that timely detection, isolation, contact tracing, and infection prevention and control can reduce onward transmission.[15]

Limitations¶

Reliance on a borrowed, cross-pathogen hazard parameter: Because no airline-mediated importation had been reported as of 28 May 2026, the hazard parameter was estimated from the 2013–2016 West African EVD epidemic rather than from current BVD data, and the model assumes this historical relationship is transferable to the present outbreak.[95]

Static airline data and absence of intervention effects: The projections rely on May 2024 airline network data and therefore may not capture subsequent route changes, passenger volume shifts, airspace restrictions, travel advisories, or the effect of post-PHEIC interventions, enhanced screening, and behavioural changes.[96]

Geographic scope limited to air travel from two airports: The analysis focuses on global air travel originating from FIH, with EBB considered only in sensitivity analysis, and does not explicitly account for land-based spread within DRC or cross-border movements to neighbouring countries.[94]

Importation risk only, not local transmission risk: Estimated arrival probability should be interpreted as importation risk rather than the risk of local transmission after arrival.[97] Countries with high-probability airport gateways should therefore be interpreted as priorities for awareness and preparedness, rather than as locations where local outbreaks are expected.[20]

Increased uncertainty in alternative δ formulation: The sensitivity analysis δ formulation incorporates the epidemic growth rate and outbound passenger flow, but the authors note that while this adds information about the current epidemic situation, it also increases uncertainty.[90]

Data sources¶

Primary outbreak data: Epidemiological context was drawn from publicly available reports: as of 4 June 2026, DRC had reported 363 confirmed cases including 62 deaths, and Uganda had reported 15 confirmed cases including one death.[98] Because no airline-mediated importation had been reported as of 28 May 2026, no exported-case numerator was available to calibrate the current-outbreak hazard.[99]

Auxiliary data: Airline passenger-flow data came from OAG (a global travel data provider listed in the IATA Strategic Partners Directory) for May 2024, covering 11,264 airports, 569,741 routes, and 329,326,078 passengers.[82] The importation hazard parameter (θ = 0.19) was calibrated from the 2013–2016 West African EVD epidemic and applied here as the primary value.[81][100] Epidemic growth rate estimates (r = {0.034, 0.05, 0.1}) used in sensitivity analyses were taken as of 17 May 2026.[87]

Epistorm (2026) Ebola Bundibugyo Virus — DRC · Spread Risk Assessment #4 · International Dissemination¶

Read the report

Overview¶

This analysis quantifies relative importation risk in the immediate cross-border region around the affected provinces and across international destinations, ranking those destinations under a mobility-based framework.[101][102] The modelling approach follows the GLEAM (Global Epidemic and Mobility) computational framework, estimating for each potential destination the probability that an infected individual originating in the affected area travels there, conditional on at least one exported case occurring, and producing a relative risk distribution normalised across all candidate destinations.[103] The main finding is that conditional international risk concentrates almost entirely within Africa (98%), with Tanzania accounting for 45.3%, South Sudan for 23.4%, Kenya for 10.4%, and the UAE for 4.5% of that conditional international risk.[104]

Estimate¶

This analysis does not estimate outbreak size (total cases, infections, or deaths). It quantifies conditional relative importation risk to international destinations, given that at least one international export occurs, and is intended to indicate surveillance priority ranking among international destinations by relative likelihood. Absolute likelihood of any international export, downstream local transmission, and outbreak burden at source are explicitly out of scope.[3]

The top-ranked destinations are Sumbawanga (TZA, RR 28.1%), Juba (SSD, RR 23.4%), Bukoba (TZA, RR 11.5%), Kisumu (KEN, RR 6.4%), and Dubai (ARE, RR 4.5%), together accounting for roughly 74% of the conditional international risk.[6]

By region, the distribution is: Africa 84.5% (Tanzania 45.3%, South Sudan 23.4%, Kenya 10.4%, Ethiopia 1.1%); Middle East 7.3% (UAE 4.5%, Saudi Arabia 1.4%, Qatar 0.6%); Europe 3.7% (UK 1.2%, Germany 0.5%, Netherlands 0.3%); Asia 2.9% (India 1.2%, China 1.0%, Korea 0.1%); Americas 1.3% (USA 0.98%, Canada 0.36%); Other 0.2%.[105]

These values are conditional on at least one international export occurring and describe how international exportation probability is distributed across destinations given that an international export happens, not the absolute probability that any specific destination will receive a case. The unconditional probability of international export remains small.[106]

Methods¶

GLEAM mobility-based relative risk model: This computational framework estimates, for each potential destination, the probability that an infected individual originating in the affected area travels there, conditional on at least one exported case occurring. The output is a relative risk distribution across all candidate destinations, normalised so that values represent the share of importation probability assigned to each location.[103]

Air travel and local mobility data integration: Global air travel patterns are derived from IATA and OAG, providing passenger-level origin-destination flows between international airports. These data are complemented by short-scale commuting patterns capturing daily movements between adjacent subpopulations, representing local spread through routine human activity within and across health zones.[107]

Destination set restriction and renormalisation: Following the 2 June 2026 reopening of the Bunia International Airport, the model factors increased domestic and regional mobility from the affected area. To support surveillance prioritisation, the destination set is restricted to non-local international locations, excluding the affected area, other DRC locations, and Rwanda and Burundi, with relative risk values renormalised to sum to one across the 208 remaining international destinations.[108][109] This approach is described as extending the same framework used in Reports #1 to #3 of this series.

Assumptions¶

Incubation period and travel window: The incubation period of Bundibugyo virus (2–21 days; average approximately 10 days) constrains the window within which an exported case could reasonably travel while infectious or pre-symptomatic. The model does not predict downstream transmission chains following an importation event, so risk estimates reflect only the probability of a single importation and do not account for the probability of sustained local transmission in the receiving location.[110]

Conditionality: All values are conditional on at least one international export occurring; they describe how international exportation probability is distributed across destinations given that an international export happens, not the absolute probability that any specific destination receives a case.[111]

Homogeneous traveller population: The mobility model does not incorporate sociodemographic attributes of travellers (age, economic status, occupation, or pre-existing medical conditions), any of which may modulate both exposure risk at origin and care-seeking behaviour at destination. Travel probability within a catchment area is treated as independent of these attributes and of specific location within the catchment, so estimates apply to traffic from the general population.[112]

Exclusion of operational movements: The importation risk associated with repatriation flights, responder evacuations, or other ad-hoc operational movements is not part of this analysis.[113]

Statistical independence of importation events: Case importations are modelled as statistically independent events. Real-world events involving multiple related cases (for example, a family cluster travelling together) are counted as a single importation event. To the extent that such clusters are common, the model will under-count the number of distinct exposure events while correctly counting the number of distinct geographic seedings.[114]

Airport reopening traffic volumes: The reopening of Bunia Airport on 2 June 2026 is factored by resuming mobility from the region, although actual traffic may differ from pre-pandemic baselines.[115]

Rwanda and Burundi as regional spillover: These two immediate regional neighbours are excluded from the international destination set on the assumption that their substantial mobility flows are better characterised as regional spillover than international dissemination, and because their border policy with respect to the outbreak remains uncertain.[116][109]

Limitations¶

Informal cross-border movement not captured: Mobility data may not fully capture informal cross-border movement, which is substantial within the immediate cross-border region. For the international tail considered in this report, the model relies on origin-destination flight data (IATA / OAG), which does not represent these informal flows.[12]

Sociodemographic homogeneity: The mobility model does not incorporate sociodemographic attributes of travellers — age, economic status, occupation, or pre-existing medical conditions — any of which may modulate both exposure risk at origin and care-seeking behaviour at destination. Travel probability is treated as independent of these attributes and of specific location within the catchment.[117]

Operational movements excluded: The importation risk associated with repatriation flights, responder evacuations, or other ad-hoc operational movements is not part of this analysis.[113]

Cluster travel undercounted as distinct events: Case importations are modelled as statistically independent events, so real-world family clusters or group travel are counted as a single importation event. To the extent such clusters are common, the model will under-count distinct exposure events while correctly counting distinct geographic seedings.[114]

Bunia airport baseline uncertainty: The Bunia Airport reopening is modelled by resuming mobility from the region, but actual traffic volumes may differ from the pre-pandemic baselines used.[115]

No downstream transmission modelled: The model does not predict downstream transmission chains following an importation event; risk estimates reflect the probability of a single importation only and do not account for the probability of sustained local transmission in the receiving location.[118]

Data sources¶

Primary outbreak data: The analysis notes a confirmed CFR of approximately 14% (DRC/Uganda combined) for the current outbreak, with the pathogen identified as Bundibugyo virus.[119] The source footprint is defined by 19 laboratory-confirmed cases in Uganda and a laboratory-confirmed travel-related case in Bukavu, South Kivu.[120]

Overview¶

Using International Air Transport Association (IATA) origin-destination passenger flows, this analysis estimates the relative risk of Bundibugyo virus disease importation into Europe under six outbreak expansion scenarios reflecting plausible pathways of geographical spread, including cross-border transmission and amplification in highly connected regional capitals.[5] Relative exposure patterns across European countries remained largely unchanged under localized transmission in eastern DRC and border-spillover scenarios, but expansion into South Sudan generated a first structural increase in importation pressure to Europe, while hypothetical amplification in Kampala, Kigali, and Kinshasa substantially increased importation pressure and reshaped exposure patterns.[8] Across all scenarios, France, Italy, and the United Kingdom remained among the most exposed countries.[9]

Estimate¶

This analysis does not produce an outbreak size estimate (i.e., a count of cases or infections). Its outputs are relative importation risk rankings for European countries and fold-change increases in importation pressure by European subregion under each scenario. Potential spread across the Ugandan border produced no change in either the relative exposure of European countries or their overall importation pressure compared with the currently reported localized transmission in eastern DRC, with Italy, France, Belgium, and the UK being the most exposed.[125] Expansion to South Sudan produced a first structural increase in importation pressure through Juba (5-fold increase for Northern Europe and 6-fold increase for Eastern Europe relative to localized DRC transmission), while still leaving the UK, France, and Italy among the highest-risk countries.[126] Kampala and Kigali amplification increased exposure in Northern Europe, with Kampala producing a >120-fold increase in importation pressure relative to localized DRC transmission; Kinshasa amplification primarily reinforced exposure in Western Europe (~80-fold increase).[127] In the sensitivity analysis using full-year 2024 travel data, the Kampala amplification scenario produces approximately 190-fold increase for Northern Europe.[128]

Methods¶

Air-travel importation risk model using IATA origin-destination flows: The analysis uses origin-destination passenger flows derived from the 2024 IATA database, which captures complete passenger itineraries including traffic travelling via connecting flights rather than only direct flight segments.[129] The relative importation risk to each European country is estimated as the proportion of total outgoing passenger flow from the selected seed airports directed to that country, where passenger flow from each origin airport in a given scenario is summed across all destination airports in the receiving country, then normalized to total passenger flow from source airports to all of Europe.[130] Risks are then stratified by European subregion.[131] This approach produces relative (not absolute) importation risk rankings and does not incorporate incubation period, detection probability, or infectious prevalence at source. A sensitivity analysis using average monthly data from the full year 2024 was conducted to account for potential seasonal effects; results were broadly consistent with the main analysis, with the exception of the Kampala amplification scenario, in which the increase in importation pressure for Northern Europe rises to approximately 190-fold when using full-year monthly averages.[132]

Assumptions¶

Proportional travel as a proxy for importation risk: The model assumes that the relative proportion of outbound passenger volume from seed airports to each European country is a valid proxy for relative importation risk. The relative importation risk to each destination country is estimated purely as the share of total outgoing passenger flow from the selected seed airports directed to that country, normalized to all Europe-bound flow.[130] This structural assumption means that no adjustment is made for incubation period, probability of travelling while infectious, detection at exit or entry, or per-capita incidence at source — the analysis is explicitly relative, not absolute.

Scenario seeding and airport selection: The six scenarios are designed to reflect plausible pathways of geographical spread from the currently affected area through porous borders.[133] Each scenario’s result depends entirely on which airports are included as seeds: adding or removing an airport changes the importation ranking. The analysis does not specify how the airport catchment areas were defined or how ground-to-air connectivity within source regions was accounted for.

Travel data representativeness: The analysis uses cumulative passenger traffic from May, June, and July 2024; this period showed moderate seasonal variability, with mean volumes ranging from 3% below to 16% above the monthly 2024 average across scenarios.[134] The analysis implicitly assumes 2024 travel patterns are representative of 2026 patterns during the outbreak period, including any travel disruptions or demand changes associated with the outbreak itself.

Capital-city amplification as a hypothetical: Scenarios 4–6 explore hypothetical amplification assuming community transmission in major urban hubs — Kampala, Kigali, and Kinshasa.[135] The analysis does not assign probabilities to these scenarios occurring; they are treated as illustrative of what importation pressure would look like if amplification did occur, rather than as probabilistic forecasts.

Limitations¶

Relative risk only, no absolute importation probability: The analysis produces relative rankings and fold-change comparisons but does not estimate the absolute probability that any infected traveller departs or arrives at a given destination. Patterns of importation risk to Europe may rapidly shift under uncertainty surrounding the geographical extent and evolution of the outbreak, particularly if transmission expands into major urban hubs with strong international connectivity near the affected area.[136] The analysis does not quantify this uncertainty in probabilistic terms.

Seasonal and temporal representativeness of travel data: The analyses use cumulative passenger traffic from May, June, and July 2024, a period that showed moderate seasonal variability relative to the 2024 annual average.[134] The authors do not discuss whether outbreak-related changes in travel behaviour (e.g., reduced tourism or business travel to affected regions) would alter the estimates.

No adjustment for detection, infectious period, or per-capita incidence: The method does not incorporate incubation period distributions, probability of travelling while infectious, exit or entry screening effectiveness, or source-area incidence rates. As a consequence, all scenarios implicitly assume equal per-traveller exportation risk from each seed airport, which the authors do not explicitly flag as a limitation but is a direct consequence of the proportional-flow methodology.

Hypothetical nature of amplification scenarios: Scenarios 4–6 explore hypothetical amplification assuming community transmission in major urban hubs.[135] The analysis does not assign likelihoods to these scenarios or model the transmission dynamics required to reach that state, limiting their direct operational applicability.

Data sources¶

Primary outbreak data: As of 29 May 2026, the outbreak involved 125 confirmed cases (17 deaths) and 906 suspected cases (223 deaths) across Ituri, North Kivu, and South Kivu provinces in Eastern DRC, and 9 confirmed cases (1 death) in Uganda.[137] These figures inform the scenario definitions (which areas to include as seeds) but are not directly used as a quantitative input to the model formula.

Methods overview

These pages are produced by a semi-automated evidence-synthesis pipeline that pairs AI models with human review at each major checkpoint: one model reads each source analysis on its own and grounds every extracted claim in a verbatim quoted passage; a second model takes the resulting structured comparison as input, and drafts the narrative. Human analysts then review and edit the output to ensure accuracy and completeness prior to publishing.

Identifying analyses. Analyses are identified through continuous monitoring of preprint servers, peer-reviewed publications, and grey literature. An analysis is included if it directly addresses the 2026 Bundibugyo virus disease (BVD) outbreak and provides a quantitative estimate relevant to a given topic (e.g., outbreak size, risk of spread). Each included source is logged, and if the authors later release an updated version, the newer version supersedes the earlier one.

Preparing the source text. Each source text, regardless of source type (e.g., PDF, HTML), is converted to Markdown. A human analyst then curates the converted file and removes sections that carry no evidentiary content, such as references, acknowledgments, author contributions, affiliations, and declarations of interest, to keep extraction focused and reduce the number of input tokens used.

Extracting structured evidence. The curated text is split into individually citeable units, one per sentence, and passed to Claude Sonnet 4.6. For each of a fixed set of dimensions (e.g., estimates, methods, limitations), the model answers from that one source alone and must ground every factual claim in a verbatim span of its text.

Synthesizing the narrative. OpenAI’s GPT-5.5 model reads the dimensions extracted for each analysis and synthesizes the information, covering the state of the evidence, how the estimates compare and why they diverge, the key uncertainties and assumptions shared across analyses, what would most reduce that uncertainty, and what it implies for the response.

Human review and publication. Human analysts edit the draft for accuracy, completeness, and tone prior to publishing.

Limitations. This pipeline surfaces only publicly available analyses; works that have not been made public are not captured.
