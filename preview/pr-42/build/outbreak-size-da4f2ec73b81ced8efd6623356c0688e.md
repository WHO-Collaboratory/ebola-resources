## Synthesizing evidence

### State of the evidence
This synthesis is grounded in six analyses published between 24 May and 18 July 2026: four peer-reviewed works and two live dashboards. McCabe et al. use death-based and travel-based back-calculation, Abbott et al. extends McCabe into a joint Bayesian renewal model fitting multiple surveillance streams, Mooring et al. projects future size under isolation scenarios, Chamla et al. models confirmed-case trajectories, Epistorm calibrates a stochastic transmission model to Ituri confirmed counts, and Kim et al. offers a critique of the travel-based approach.[^mccabe-2026-2][^abbott-2026-front-matter-2][^mooring-2026-abstract-1][^chamla-2026-methods-11][^epistorm-2026-3-methods-1][^kim-2026-main-7] The evidence base is growing but still relatively thin: only Abbott et al. directly estimates a current true-to-confirmed detection multiplier.


### How the estimates compare
Abbott et al. estimate 4,201–8,675 infections against 2,344 laboratory-confirmed cases on 18 July, or 1.8–3.7× the confirmed count.[^abbott-2026-results-1] Earlier, McCabe et al. estimated about 280–2,520 DRC cases against 1,031 suspected or confirmed reports on 27 May, or 0.3–2.4× the suspected-or-confirmed count, but that denominator includes suspected cases that may not be Bundibugyo infections, so the ratio is not directly comparable to Abbott's true-to-laboratory-confirmed multiplier.[^mccabe-2026-2][^mccabe-2026-3] Epistorm calibrated a stochastic model to Ituri's 717 confirmed cases on 13 June, allowing ascertainment as low as 20%, which implies up to five true symptomatic cases per confirmed case, while Chamla et al. deliberately modelled confirmed cases only by fixing reporting fraction to 1.0.[^epistorm-2026-3-methods-1][^chamla-2026-methods-11] This should not be read as a simple time trend in the detection multiplier, because scopes, dates, and estimands differ across analyses rather than a repeated application of the same method.

### Key uncertainties and assumptions
All outbreak size estimates depend on surveillance signals interpreted through strong assumptions. Death-based estimates depend on attribution of suspected deaths, CFR, and onset-to-death delays. Travel-based estimates depend on whether exported cases represent routine travel, and Kim et al. directly challenges that assumption, noting that excluding healthcare-seeking travel there were no confirmed imported cases in Uganda through 21 June.[^kim-2026-main-7] Abbott et al. adds more surveillance streams but notes that changing case definitions, retrospective testing, revised situation-report totals, and limited epidemiological dating can move all multiplier estimates together.[^abbott-2026-limitations-5][^abbott-2026-limitations-7] Projection models add uncertainty from assumptions about how many symptomatic cases are removed from transmission through isolation and treatment.[^mooring-2026-abstract-1][^epistorm-2026-abstract-4]

### What would reduce uncertainty
The most important addition would be onset-dated line lists linking suspected, confirmed, death, recovery, and contact-tracing records, because they would separate transmission growth from surveillance catch-up. Second would be current cross-border movement data that distinguishes routine travel from healthcare-seeking travel, including trip duration along with which health zones travellers originate from.[^kim-2026-main-18]

### Open questions
McCabe et al. leaves unresolved the size, growth rate, and geographic extent of the epidemic, and calls for epidemiological and genomic data to resolve them.[^mccabe-2026-27] Chamla et al. calls for iterative recalibration using line lists, contact-tracing data, and dynamic mobility estimates.[^chamla-2026-discussion-11] Kim et al. identifies cross-border movement dynamics and source-population characterisation as needing further study.[^kim-2026-main-18]

### What this means for the response
The evidence suggests confirmed counts should be treated as a lower bound, with the most explicit current estimate indicating roughly 2–4x infections per confirmed case. It still suggests true infections exceed confirmed cases, so confirmed counts alone understate burden, committed care needs, and transmission potential. It also suggests the detection gap cannot be inferred from raw case-count growth across reports; each true-size estimate must be read against the confirmed count at its own cutoff. Ituri and Nord Kivu remain the centre of the outbreak inside DRC. Uganda has already reported imported and locally acquired cases, and preparedness in other neighbouring countries remains a priority.[^chamla-2026-discussion-8]

:::{tab-item} Kim et al. (21 Jul 2026)
### Kim et al. (2026) Cross-border travel patterns affect magnitude estimates for the Ebola Bundibugyo epidemic

[Read the report](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44360-026-00178-y)

### Overview

This analysis estimates an upper bound on the likely burden of Bundibugyo virus disease (BVD) cases in the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) early in the 2026 epidemic, based on the number of cases imported and confirmed in Uganda.[^kim-2026-abstract-1] It expands a geographic spread approach to account for two specific contextual factors: that most cross-border movements between DRC and Uganda are short-duration trips, and that all cases imported and confirmed in Uganda were individuals who travelled specifically to seek healthcare.[^kim-2026-abstract-2] Incorporating these factors substantially affected burden estimates, highlighting both the potential severity of the epidemic in its early phase and the critical need to consider local epidemiological contexts when applying geographic spread approaches to reduce and be aware of potential biases in estimates for early epidemic responses.[^kim-2026-abstract-3]

### Estimate

The analysis reports estimates under multiple scenarios combining source population scope (Ituri alone vs. Ituri and Nord Kivu), number of assumed imported cases (3 vs. 0, the latter after excluding healthcare-seeking travellers), and travel-duration accounting (with vs. without). Table 1 in the paper presents estimates and 95% confidence intervals of the number of BVD cases in the DRC across these combinations, but the full numerical table is not included in the document as provided.[^kim-2026-main-5]

Accounting for the observed pattern of trip durations with three imported cases substantially increased central estimates and widened 95% confidence intervals compared to not accounting for travel durations within the framework and to estimates reported by McCabe et al.[^kim-2026-main-3] Under the zero-imported-cases scenario (after excluding healthcare-seeking travellers), central estimates and lower confidence limits were zero by definition; however, the upper confidence limits provided informative likely upper bounds for early BVD burden, intended for use in epidemic response planning.[^kim-2026-main-4] The interpretation offered is that if true BVD cases in the DRC were higher than these upper limits, non-zero imported cases would have been expected among individuals travelling to Uganda for general purposes.[^kim-2026-main-6] Specific numerical values for the central estimates and confidence intervals by scenario are not recoverable from the text of the document provided.

### Methods

**Geographic spread back-calculation with travel-duration adjustment:** This approach estimates the total number of BVD cases in the DRC source population by working backwards from the count of cases exported to and detected in Uganda, divided by the probability that an infected traveller would actually be detected while in Uganda. The method obtains the overall probability of detection while travelling as a weighted average over different durations of travel and different infection-to-detection intervals.[^kim-2026-methods-4] Travel duration data from the March 2020 IOM Uganda–DRC Border Flow Monitoring Dashboard were used for the baseline scenario, reclassified into three categories: less than 1 day (64.7%), 1 day to 1 week (25.9%), and longer than 1 week (9.4%).[^kim-2026-methods-5] The number of exported cases was divided by the overall probability of detection to estimate DRC cases, with 95% confidence intervals derived from the Poisson-likelihood-based confidence limits for the rate of exported cases — for example, the 95% CI for zero exported cases is (0, 1.92).[^kim-2026-methods-6] The method was applied to both the Ituri and Ituri-plus-Nord-Kivu source populations.

The analysis runs two principal scenarios regarding the number of imported cases to use as the numerator. With three imported cases and accounting for the observed pattern of trip durations, central estimates were substantially higher and confidence intervals wider compared to not accounting for travel durations.[^kim-2026-main-3] With zero imported cases — that is, after excluding all three imported cases because their travel was healthcare-seeking — central estimates and lower confidence limits were uninformative (zero by definition), but upper confidence limits provided informative likely upper bounds for early BVD burden.[^kim-2026-main-4] A sensitivity scenario used April 2019 IOM data with a different travel-duration distribution: less than 1 day (47.4%), 1 day to 1 week (45.4%), and longer than 1 week (7.2%).[^kim-2026-methods-7]

### Assumptions

**Healthcare-seeking travel and the equal-travel assumption:** The conventional geographic spread approach assumes individuals in the source population have an equal probability of travelling regardless of infection status; the analysis identifies this as violated in the DRC–Uganda context because all confirmed imported cases were healthcare-seeking travellers, meaning that excluding healthcare travel, there had been no confirmed imported cases in Uganda through 21 June 2026.[^kim-2026-main-7] This motivates the zero-imported-cases scenario as the preferred structural framing, with three imported cases treated as a comparison.

**Travel duration distributions:** The baseline assumes trip-duration proportions from the March 2020 IOM Dashboard (90.6% of trips lasting a week or less, 64.7% day trips); the alternative scenario uses April 2019 IOM data (92.8% lasting a week or less). Trip-duration data were not available for the period when early imported cases were confirmed in Uganda; short-duration trips are considered to have dominated cross-border movement during this period.[^kim-2026-main-8]

**Infection-to-detection interval:** The delay from infection to detection is modelled with a Gamma distribution (shape 2.52, rate 0.40) for the incubation period, shifted by an assumed onset-to-detection delay to produce median infection-to-detection intervals of 10, 15, and 20 days, following McCabe et al.[^kim-2026-methods-8] The analysis acknowledges that the time from infection to detection varies between individuals depending on biological and behavioural factors as well as healthcare availability for clinical assessment and diagnostic testing.[^kim-2026-methods-9]

**Fixed travel durations within categories:** Within the less-than-1-day and 1-day-to-1-week categories, fixed median travel durations of 0.5 and 4 days are assigned respectively; travellers in the longer-than-1-week category are assumed to remain in Uganda indefinitely — long enough to be detected there.[^kim-2026-methods-10]

**Source population and proportionality:** Estimates are expected to change proportionally to the assumed source population size and could be biased if assumed source population sizes did not accurately reflect the catchment population of those travelling to Uganda, since residents near the DRC–Uganda border likely have substantially different travel probabilities than those living further away, particularly given that border communities operate as dynamic and shared economic entities.[^kim-2026-main-9]

**Reduced travel probability among the infected:** If infected individuals were less likely to travel for non-healthcare-seeking purposes — for example due to feeling poorly — the burden estimate would scale up according to the inverse of the factor by which they were less likely to travel.[^kim-2026-methods-11]

### Limitations

**Violation of the equal-travel assumption:** The standard geographic spread approach assumes individuals have an equal probability of travelling regardless of infection status. This assumption is violated because all imported cases confirmed in Uganda were healthcare-seeking travellers, meaning the observed exported cases cannot serve as a representative signal of background travel.[^kim-2026-main-10][^kim-2026-methods-1]

**Unavailability of contemporaneous trip-duration data:** Trip-duration data were not available for the period when early imported cases were confirmed in Uganda, requiring the analysis to rely on 2019 and 2020 IOM data as proxies. Short-duration trips are considered to have dominated cross-border movement during this period, but this is based on expert communication rather than direct measurement.[^kim-2026-main-11]

**Source population size uncertainty:** Estimates are expected to change proportionally to the assumed source population size and could be biased if these sizes did not accurately reflect the catchment population of those travelling to Uganda. Those residing near the border probably have substantially different travel probabilities, and individuals who can afford to travel to seek healthcare abroad may also have different baseline infection risks compared with the general population.[^kim-2026-main-12][^kim-2026-main-13]

**Uninformative lower bound under the zero-imported-cases scenario:** When zero imported cases are used — the scenario reflecting the exclusion of healthcare-seeking travellers — central estimates and lower confidence limits are zero by definition, meaning the approach can only provide informative upper bounds rather than a full distributional estimate of true burden.[^kim-2026-main-4]

**Knowledge gap on cross-border dynamics:** A better understanding of the complex cross-border crossing dynamics would be needed to obtain more robust estimates of true case burden using this approach.[^kim-2026-main-14]

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:** All 15 BVD cases imported and confirmed in Uganda through 21 June 2026, all of whom were Congolese nationals who came to Uganda to seek medical care, form the primary signal for the back-calculation.[^kim-2026-main-1] The first three confirmed imported cases, available by 9 June 2026 when McCabe et al. was published, are the specific numerator used in the three-imported-cases scenario.[^kim-2026-methods-1]

**Auxiliary data:** Cross-border travel duration distributions come from the March 2020 IOM Uganda–DRC Border Flow Monitoring Dashboard (baseline scenario), providing proportions for less than 1 day (64.7%), 1 day to 1 week (25.9%), and longer than 1 week (9.4%).[^kim-2026-methods-5] The April 2019 IOM Uganda–DRC Border Flow Monitoring Dashboard provides an alternative travel-duration distribution used in the sensitivity scenario.[^kim-2026-methods-7] Daily traveller counts and source population sizes for Ituri (1,871 travellers/day; population 4,392,200) and Ituri plus Nord Kivu (4,339 travellers/day; population 13,392,200) were taken directly from McCabe et al.[^kim-2026-methods-2][^kim-2026-methods-3] The incubation period was modelled using a Gamma distribution (shape 2.52, rate 0.40) to derive infection-to-detection intervals with median times of 10, 15, and 20 days, following McCabe et al.[^kim-2026-methods-8]

:::{tab-item} Abbott et al. (18 Jul 2026)
### Abbott et al. (2026) Estimating the current size of the 2026 DRC Bundibugyo virus outbreak

[Read the report](https://epiforecasts.io/BVDOutbreakSize/dev/analysis)

### Overview

This analysis estimates the true size of the ongoing Bundibugyo virus disease (BVD) outbreak in the DRC by fitting a discrete-time renewal model jointly to multiple surveillance streams — suspected cases and deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and deaths, isolation-bed occupancy, treatment-centre flows, recovered cases, and exported cases in Uganda — using a single Bayesian posterior. It is a real-time joint Bayesian estimate of the current outbreak size, refreshed as new data arrive, using a discrete-time renewal process that fits the surveillance streams jointly in a single posterior.[^abbott-2026-front-matter-1][^abbott-2026-front-matter-2] As of the 18 July 2026 data cutoff, the model estimates approximately 4,201 to 8,675 true infections (90% credible interval) against 2,344 laboratory-confirmed cases, implying roughly 1.8 to 3.7 times as many infections as confirmed cases.

### Estimate

The headline estimate from the joint posterior, as of the 18 July 2026 data cutoff, is as follows.

The outbreak is estimated to have caused 30% 5,116–6,008, 60% 4,733–6,719, 90% 4,201–8,675 infections to date, reported and unreported. Against the 2,344 laboratory-confirmed cases by the cutoff, that is roughly 1.8–3.7× as many infections, so confirmed cases are estimated to capture only a small share of the outbreak.[^abbott-2026-results-1]

The initial reproduction number is estimated to have been 30% 1.75–2.15, 60% 1.58–2.42, 90% 1.35–2.90, and the latest to be 30% 1.07–1.24, 60% 0.98–1.35, 90% 0.83–1.64.[^abbott-2026-results-2]

The case-fatality ratio is estimated to be 30% 0.44–0.49, 60% 0.41–0.53, 90% 0.36–0.59.[^abbott-2026-results-3]

The outbreak is estimated to have begun on a start date of 30% 2026-03-14–2026-03-20, 60% 2026-03-09–2026-03-24, 90% 2026-02-28–2026-03-27, an elapsed age to the cutoff of 30% 120–126, 60% 116–131, 90% 113–140 days.[^abbott-2026-results-4]

The per-stream sensitivity models also produce individual outbreak-size posteriors, allowing comparison of how each data stream alone constrains the estimate, though specific numerical values for these per-stream results are not tabulated beyond what the joint model reports.

### Methods

**Joint discrete-time renewal model:** This is a real-time joint Bayesian extension of the McCabe et al. approach, running on a daily grid from the inferred seeding date to the data cutoff. The model is a discrete-time renewal process on a daily grid that fits the surveillance streams jointly in a single posterior: DRC suspected cases, suspected deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and confirmed deaths, and the cases and deaths exported to Uganda. It estimates the latent infections, symptom onsets and deaths over time, the reported and confirmed cases, and the time-varying reproduction number with its growth rate and doubling time, alongside the case-fatality ratio, the ascertainment of each surveillance system, and a short-term forecast of each stream over the coming week.[^abbott-2026-front-matter-6] It is a discrete-time renewal process with a time-varying reproduction number fitted to more of the available data streams than the original McCabe et al. report.[^abbott-2026-origins-of-this-work-1] The primary geographic scope is DRC nationally, with Uganda exports used as an additional constraining data stream. A genetic bound on the time to the most recent common ancestor floors the cryptic duration from below. Every delay (generation interval, incubation period, onset-to-report, onset-to-confirmation and onset-to-detection abroad) is sampled from a prior centred on published Ebola estimates, discretised with double interval censoring, so delay uncertainty propagates through the posterior.[^abbott-2026-origins-of-this-work-2] Sensitivity analyses include a delay sensitivity run and a molecular clock sensitivity run using an exponential growth parametrisation. The model jointly estimates the outbreak size and the fraction of cases each surveillance system reports.[^abbott-2026-origins-of-this-work-3]

**Per-stream single-model fits:** Alongside the joint model, single-stream models are written for each count-based stream (exported cases, suspected deaths, suspected cases, laboratory-confirmed cases, confirmed deaths and deaths among exports), so each stream's posterior over the outbreak size can be compared with the joint.[^abbott-2026-dated-per-day-poisson-the-clock-stops-1] These cover DRC nationally and Uganda exports, using the same renewal model structure but conditioning on one stream at a time, allowing the analysis to assess which data sources drive the headline estimate and how consistent they are.

### Assumptions

**Generation interval:** Assumed to follow a Gamma distribution with mean 15.3 days and SD 9.3 days, derived from the WHO Ebola Response Team 2014 estimate of the EVD serial interval, used as a generation-time proxy. The distribution maps to a Gamma shape near 2.71 and scale near 5.65, with priors centred on those values.[^abbott-2026-harmless-3]

**Incubation period and delay distributions:** The incubation period prior is centred on the MacNeil et al. (2010) estimate from the 2007 Uganda BVD outbreak (mean 6.3 days). The source reports no interval on the spread, so the SD prior is the authors' own choice.[^abbott-2026-trajectory-edge-artifact-rather-than-the-3] Onset-to-report follows a Gamma sampled from a Bayesian reanalysis of the 2012 Isiro line list, with implied mean about 4 days.[^abbott-2026-trajectory-edge-artifact-rather-than-the-4] Onset-to-death is the convolution of two atomic Gamma components — onset-to-admission and admission-to-death — each with priors centred on the reanalysis posteriors, with implied mean about 13 days.[^abbott-2026-trajectory-edge-artifact-rather-than-the-5]

**Case-fatality ratio:** The prior is Beta(6.6, 13.4), with mean and interval roughly 15–54%, based on the CDC summary for the two previous BVD outbreaks (55 deaths in 169 cases) and the reanalysis baseline of 0.25 (CrI 0.16–0.38) for non-HCW confirmed cases. The mean matches the CDC figure and the corrected central CFR in the 20 May McCabe et al. report.[^abbott-2026-trajectory-edge-artifact-rather-than-the-6]

**Ascertainment:** The DRC and Uganda ascertainment fractions share a logit-scale hyperprior with mean centred on a reporting fraction of 75%, reflecting the active case-finding of a declared Ebola response rather than baseline passive surveillance.[^abbott-2026-eps-floors-the-sd-so-a-1]

**Molecular clock and genetic seeding bound:** The TMRCA from ten sequenced genomes is placed at a mean of 25 March 2026; the clock is fixed at the substitutions/site/year rate of the 2013–2016 West African Ebola epidemic, as the temporal sampling range is too short to estimate the clock. The TMRCA is treated as a lower bound on outbreak age.[^abbott-2026-harmless-4] Clock uncertainty and cross-outbreak clock uncertainty are not propagated.[^abbott-2026-harmless-5]

**Cryptic phase and single zoonotic seed:** The outbreak is assumed to have started from a single seed case introduced by a zoonotic spillover. From that seed, the outbreak grew deterministically through an unobserved cryptic exponential phase, with a prior centred on three cryptic doublings.[^abbott-2026-harmless-6] The genetic reanalysis reports epidemic doubling time as 15.2 to 24.5 days across substitution-rate assumptions, with centre near 20 days; a log-normal prior on the growth rate is centred on a 20-day doubling time.[^abbott-2026-harmless-7]

**Cross-border travel:** Cross-border traveller volume uses a Normal prior centred on the McCabe et al. figure of 1,871 daily outbound travellers with SD 200. The source population is fixed at 4,392,200.[^abbott-2026-methods-8]

**Stream independence:** Surveillance streams are fitted as conditionally independent given latent incidence but observe overlapping people, which can understate uncertainty. Whether the streams imply mutually consistent outbreak sizes is not assessed.[^abbott-2026-limitations-2]

**No spatial structure:** The model uses a single zoonotic seed and has no spatial structure beyond the Ituri / Nord Kivu split.[^abbott-2026-limitations-1]

**Weakly informed priors:** Nearly all of the delays, the case-fatality ratio, and the laboratory assumptions are set by priors informed at best by a handful of literature sources, often from other outbreaks, and in places by the authors' own prior judgement. The data do little to move them, so posteriors largely track their priors.[^abbott-2026-limitations-3]

### Limitations

**Reliance on borrowed and weakly informed priors:** Nearly all of the delays, the case-fatality ratio and the laboratory assumptions are set by priors informed at best by a handful of literature sources, often from other outbreaks, and in places by prior judgement rather than anything from this outbreak. The data do little to move them, so posteriors largely track their priors.[^abbott-2026-limitations-3] The model fits between-report increments, so the trajectory informs the change in the reproduction number over the window but is uninformative about the delays, the surveillance dispersion or the reporting fractions on their own.[^abbott-2026-limitations-4]

**Aggregate data and no epidemiological dating:** Only report-date totals are available with no counts by symptom onset or any other epidemiologically relevant date. The timing of the underlying epidemic is therefore weakly identified and is recovered only through the assumed delays.[^abbott-2026-limitations-5] The DRC data are situation-report totals; the Uganda data are three export cases with one death. No line list or information on case definitions or reporting completeness is available. Every estimate is a model-based extrapolation under strong assumptions, not a measurement.[^abbott-2026-limitations-6]

**Revisions to situation reports not modelled:** A later situation report can revise an earlier total up or down as suspects are reclassified and newly-reporting health zones are added, and ascertainment probably rose over the window. This revision process is not modelled.[^abbott-2026-limitations-7]

**Stream independence overstates confidence:** Streams are fitted as conditionally independent given latent incidence but observe overlapping people, which can understate uncertainty. Whether the streams imply mutually consistent outbreak sizes is not assessed.[^abbott-2026-limitations-2]

**Inherited structural simplifications:** The model inherits McCabe et al.'s epidemiological assumptions: a single zoonotic seed, an assumed generation interval, no spatial structure beyond the Ituri / Nord Kivu split, and no depletion of susceptibles. The onset-to-death delay is grounded on Isiro 2012 and the genetic seeding bound on an external clock rate, neither propagating cross-outbreak or clock uncertainty.[^abbott-2026-limitations-8]

**Intervention ramp weakly identified:** With only a few situation reports straddling the response breakpoint, the ramp effect and the pre-ramp reproduction number are not well separated.[^abbott-2026-limitations-9]

**National bed model cannot capture local saturation:** The treatment-centre model carries one national bed capacity and one national demand, so it cannot represent local saturation. On 13 June, Ituri was at 93.9% occupancy while Sud-Kivu was at 21.9%, and the national bed shortfall understates local unmet need.[^abbott-2026-limitations-10]

**Code not independently replicated:** The model code, priors and analysis were drafted by a language model from the McCabe et al. report and the companion delay reanalysis, then reviewed and revised. The work has not been independently replicated against the authors' code.[^abbott-2026-limitations-11]

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:**

DRC data come from the situation reports of the Institut National de Santé Publique (INSP). Each report gives the national cumulative suspected cases and deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and deaths, and the specimens received and analysed by the laboratory, at the report date.[^abbott-2026-methods-2] As of the 18 July 2026 cutoff: 2,344 confirmed cases and 930 confirmed deaths.[^abbott-2026-methods-3] Suspected cases are frozen at the 26 May vintage: 1,077 suspected cases and 246 suspected deaths.[^abbott-2026-methods-4]

Uganda data are the cases and one death exported across the border, taken from the WHO situation reports and Disease Outbreak News.[^abbott-2026-methods-5] Three exported cases (last dated 23 May 2026) and one export death (14 May 2026) are used.[^abbott-2026-methods-6]

From 13 June the reports add a Tableau 6 patient-movement table for the treatment centres, providing daily admissions, in-care deaths, rule-outs and absconded flows, and a split of occupancy into confirmed and suspected sub-rows.[^abbott-2026-methods-7]

**Auxiliary data:**

A BEAST time tree of the first ten sequenced genomes places the TMRCA at a mean of 25 March 2026; the molecular clock is fixed at the substitutions/site/year rate of the 2013–2016 West African Ebola epidemic.[^abbott-2026-harmless-1]

Cross-border traveller volume and source population come from McCabe et al.; the source population is fixed (4,392,200) and the traveller volume is given a Normal prior around the McCabe et al. figure of 1,871 daily outbound travellers with SD 200.[^abbott-2026-methods-8]

The onset-to-death delay is taken from a Bayesian reanalysis of the 2012 Isiro Bundibugyo virus outbreak line list.[^abbott-2026-origins-of-this-work-2] The incubation period is the Bundibugyo virus estimate from the 2007 Uganda outbreak (mean 6.3 days, 95% CI 5.2–7.3; MacNeil et al., 2010).[^abbott-2026-trajectory-edge-artifact-rather-than-the-1] The generation interval is a Gamma prior based on the Ebola virus disease serial interval used as a generation-time proxy (mean 15.3 days, SD 9.3 days; WHO Ebola Response Team 2014).[^abbott-2026-harmless-2] The CFR prior is based on CDC summary data for the two previous BVD outbreaks: 55 deaths in 169 cases.[^abbott-2026-trajectory-edge-artifact-rather-than-the-2]

This analysis estimates the burden of the Bundibugyo virus disease (BVD) outbreak in Ituri Province, DRC, and projects forward trajectories under three counterfactual isolation scenarios. ### Estimate

The analysis reports estimates for Ituri Province only across two snapshot dates and three forward scenarios.

**GLEAM stochastic metapopulation model with care-pathway SEIHFR:** This is a stochastic metapopulation transmission model built on the GLEAM framework, incorporating transmission in the hospital, community, and through funerals, and explicitly modelling isolation protocols through Ebola treatment centres.[^epistorm-2026-abstract-1] The framework couples an Africa-wide metapopulation network by daily commuting matrices and origin-destination passenger flows from IATA Passenger Intelligence Services and OAG Aviation Analytics.[^epistorm-2026-3-methods-3] The compartmental structure follows a Legrand-like SEIHFR scheme, with three potential outcomes at symptom onset modelling the impact of isolation and safe-burial protocols.[^epistorm-2026-3-methods-4] The model is applied to Ituri Province as the primary estimation geography, with seeding there and spread to other basins only through mobility coupling.

### Chamla et al. (2026) Size of the 2026 Ebola outbreak and risk of cross-border spillover from Bundibugyo virus in Ituri Province, DR Congo, and its implications for preparedness: a recalibrated stochastic modelling study

[Read the report](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/laninf/article/PIIS1473-3099(26)00320-8/fulltext)

### Overview

This analysis estimates the short-term epidemic trajectory of the 2026 BDBV outbreak in Ituri Province, DRC, and the probability of cross-border spillover into neighbouring countries. It uses a stochastic SEIRD (susceptible, exposed, infectious, recovered, dead) ensemble model calibrated to 598 cumulative laboratory-confirmed cases as of 8 June 2026, with simulation filtering to retain model runs consistent with that anchor.[^chamla-2026-summary-1] Under the central scenario (R0 = 1.71), the model projects a median of 990 cumulative confirmed cases by week 12 (24 June 2026; 90% prediction interval 709 to 1,293) and 174 deaths, with cross-border importation into Uganda assessed as nearly certain (94.2%) and into South Sudan as highly probable (69.3%).[^chamla-2026-summary-2]

### Estimate

Under the central transmissibility scenario (mean accepted R0 = 1.71), the ensemble projected a median of 648 cumulative confirmed cases by week 10 (8 June 2026; 50% PI 560 to 732), rising to 990 by week 12 (24 June 2026; 50% PI 858 to 1,141 and 90% PI 709 to 1,293), with a projected median of 174 deaths at week 12.[^chamla-2026-results-2]

Under the low-transmissibility scenario (mean accepted R0 = 1.42), cumulative confirmed cases reached a median of 870 by week 12 (50% PI 752 to 1,004 and 90% PI 641 to 1,133; 160 deaths) and 3,662 by week 24 (50% PI 2,884 to 4,484 and 90% PI 2,094 to 6,037; 673 deaths).[^chamla-2026-results-3]

Under the high-transmissibility scenario (mean accepted R0 = 2.08), confirmed cases reached a median of 1,364 (50% PI 1,154 to 1,579 and 90% PI 975 to 1,807; 202 deaths) by week 12, rising to 66,420 (50% PI 56,560 to 78,937 and 90% PI 41,718 to 95,881; 10,644 deaths) by week 24.[^chamla-2026-results-4]

Under the central scenario, week 24 projects a median of 8,210 cumulative confirmed cases (50% PI 6,636 to 10,287 and 90% PI 5,063 to 13,498) and approximately 1,440 deaths if transmission were merely sustained.[^chamla-2026-results-5]

The observed confirmed-case trajectory is consistent with the central scenario; the high scenario is retained as an upper-bound contingency should control measures lapse.[^chamla-2026-results-6] Importantly, this analysis calibrates exclusively to laboratory-confirmed cases and does not produce a separate "true outbreak size" estimate that exceeds the confirmed count; the reporting fraction was set at 1.0 because laboratory-confirmed cases are, by definition, ascertained, and the earlier suspected-case reporting fraction assumption is no longer required.[^chamla-2026-methods-11] No detection multiplier is calculated or reported.

### Methods

**Stochastic SEIRD ensemble model:** A discrete-time SEIRD framework with daily transitions implemented as binomial draws using standard mass-action incidence, applied to the DRC outbreak (Ituri and Nord Kivu provinces combined). Three transmissibility scenarios span published BDBV estimates: low R0 = 1.42, central R0 = 1.71, and high R0 = 2.08.[^chamla-2026-methods-4][^chamla-2026-methods-5][^chamla-2026-methods-6] Calibration was performed by simulation filtering (approximate rejection sampling, not formal Bayesian inference): replicates were accepted if cumulative laboratory-confirmed cases at day 68 (8 June 2026) fell within ±30% of 598 (range 419 to 777), retaining 400 calibration-passing replicates per scenario.[^chamla-2026-methods-7] Sensitivity analyses examined travel-while-infectious (5% to 25%), reporting completeness (0.60 to 0.90), and catchment size (250,000 to 500,000).[^chamla-2026-methods-8]

**Daily-hazard spillover model:** Importation risk to Uganda, South Sudan, Rwanda, and Burundi was modelled as a daily Poisson hazard driven by calibrated infectious prevalence from the SEIRD model, country-specific cross-border mobility, point-of-entry detection sensitivity from WHO Joint External Evaluation assessments, and travel-while-infectious parameters (Uganda 12%; South Sudan 15%; Rwanda and Burundi 8%), with full equations and parameter values given in an appendix.[^chamla-2026-methods-9] Importation probability was computed as 1 − exp(cumulative 12-week hazard), yielding the probability of at least one importation for each country.[^chamla-2026-methods-10]

### Assumptions

**Transmissibility scenarios:** Three R0 values (low 1.42, central 1.71, high 2.08) span published BDBV estimates. Response-intensity reductions applied from 15 May 2026 are 20% (low scenario), 25% (central), and 15% (high), reflecting differing assumptions about operational effectiveness.[^chamla-2026-methods-6][^chamla-2026-methods-14]

**Latent and infectious periods:** The latent period is fixed at 9.7 days and the infectious period at 5.0 days, in line with the broader filovirus literature. These are not varied across scenarios.[^chamla-2026-methods-15]

**Case-fatality ratio:** Drawn from a uniform distribution (0.15 to 0.25), centred on the observed confirmed-case ratio of approximately 19% (115 of 598 as of 8 June 2026) and consistent with the historical BDBV range.[^chamla-2026-methods-16]

**Ascertainment and reporting fraction:** Set at 1.0 for laboratory-confirmed cases on the structural assumption that confirmed cases are fully ascertained by definition. This eliminates the need to estimate an under-detection multiplier but means the model tracks the confirmed trajectory only, not total infections.[^chamla-2026-methods-11]

**Homogeneous mixing and catchment size:** The model assumes homogeneous mixing within a single catchment of 350,000 across seven affected health zones, including Ituri and Nord Kivu. Spatial heterogeneity is not captured, acknowledged by the authors as a stated limitation.[^chamla-2026-methods-17]

**Calibration tolerance:** Simulation filtering accepts replicates if cumulative confirmed cases at day 68 fall within ±30% of 598 (range 419 to 777). The ±30% tolerance was chosen pragmatically given residual data uncertainty, and is not derived from formal inference.[^chamla-2026-methods-18][^chamla-2026-methods-19]

**Cross-border mobility and travel-while-infectious parameters:** The spillover model uses country-specific travel-while-infectious parameters (Uganda 12%; South Sudan 15%; Rwanda and Burundi 8%) alongside WHO Joint External Evaluation-based detection sensitivity. The authors acknowledge these parameters were not empirically derived for this outbreak and represent the principal sources of uncertainty in spillover projections.[^chamla-2026-methods-3][^chamla-2026-methods-20]

**No vaccination compartment:** No vaccinated compartment was included, reflecting the absence of a licensed BDBV-specific vaccine.[^chamla-2026-methods-21]

**Model type:** These projections are described by the authors as exploratory preparedness-oriented estimates derived from a stochastic scenario-based modelling framework, rather than predictions generated from formally fitted epidemiological models using comprehensive parameter estimation and identifiability analyses.[^chamla-2026-summary-5]

### Limitations

**Homogeneous mixing and absent spatial structure:** The model oversimplifies spatial heterogeneity across health zones, does not separately model healthcare-worker transmission, and applies response intensity uniformly rather than varying it by place, time, access, or community acceptance.[^chamla-2026-discussion-1]

**Single-point calibration anchor:** The model is anchored on a single recent confirmed-case datum (598 on 8 June 2026), making the fit sensitive to that anchor.[^chamla-2026-discussion-1]

**Simulation filtering is not formal inference:** Calibration by approximate rejection sampling is acknowledged as not constituting formal Bayesian inference, and informal cross-border mobility estimates are described as imprecise.[^chamla-2026-discussion-1]

**Uniform and static response intensity:** The response-intensity factor was applied uniformly from 15 May, but real-world response effectiveness varies by place, time, access, and community acceptance. Live mobility data and behavioural adaptation were not incorporated.[^chamla-2026-discussion-2]

**Surveillance dynamics and residual confounding:** The post-declaration case-finding surge probably contributed to rapid apparent growth between 15 and 19 May; the analysis addressed this by lowering the reporting fraction and widening the calibration window, but residual confounding between transmission intensity and surveillance intensity remains.[^chamla-2026-discussion-3]

**Early-phase data quality:** Calibration initially relied on early-phase surveillance counts from the first weekly WHO External Situation Report, where the suspected-case total was only partially resolved into laboratory-confirmed cases and death counts reflected surveillance signals rather than confirmed mortality alone. Misclassification, delayed laboratory throughput, and ongoing active case finding could each shift the calibration anchor in either direction. **Need for iterative recalibration:** The projections require iterative recalibration as new data emerge.[^chamla-2026-discussion-5]

### McCabe et al. (2026) Estimation of the Ebola outbreak size in the Democratic Republic of the Congo

[Read the report](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/laninf/article/PIIS1473-3099(26)00299-9/fulltext)

### Overview

This analysis produces early estimates of the epidemic size in the Democratic Republic of the Congo as of May 27, 2026, using two independent, scenario-based methods applied under plausible assumptions about surveillance, severity, and epidemic growth.[^mccabe-2026-1] The first method back-calculates cases from reported deaths using historical case fatality ratios and onset-to-death delays; the second estimates outbreak size from confirmed imported cases in Uganda combined with cross-border travel volumes. The two methods yield broadly consistent results, suggesting that approximately 280–2,520 cases of Bundibugyo virus disease may have occurred in DRC as of May 27, 2026.[^mccabe-2026-2]

### Estimate

**Back-calculation from deaths:** Assuming 30% of suspected and confirmed deaths are due to Ebola, a CFR of 33%, and a doubling time of 10 days, the mean estimated outbreak size is 451 cases (95% CI 396–511) as of May 27, 2026.[^mccabe-2026-12] The estimated outbreak size increases with assumptions of a higher proportion of deaths due to Ebola, a lower CFR, and faster epidemic growth.[^mccabe-2026-13] The full scenario range for this method is 306–2,521 cases.[^mccabe-2026-2]

**Travel-based back-calculation:** Assuming Ituri Province as the source population and a doubling time of 10 days, the mean estimated outbreak size is 945 cases (95% CI 196–2,274).[^mccabe-2026-14] The full scenario range for this method is 282–1,345 cases.[^mccabe-2026-2]

**Cross-method synthesis:** Together, the two methods suggest that approximately 280–2,520 cases of Bundibugyo virus disease might have occurred in DRC as of May 27, 2026, with considerable uncertainty around these estimates.[^mccabe-2026-2]

### Methods

**Back-calculation from deaths:** This method links reported suspected and confirmed deaths — assuming a proportion are attributable to Ebola — with estimates of the case fatality ratio (33%, 95% CI 26–40%) and the distribution of time from symptom onset to death (gamma distribution, mean 11.37 days, SD 5.41) derived from previous Bundibugyo virus outbreaks.[^mccabe-2026-6] The method further assumes exponential epidemic growth after a single zoonotic spillover event.[^mccabe-2026-7] It applies to DRC, with transmission assumed concentrated in Ituri and Nord Kivu. Sensitivity analyses vary the epidemic growth rate (main scenario: 10-day doubling time; sensitivity 1: 7-day doubling time; sensitivity 2: 14-day doubling time) and the proportion of suspected and confirmed deaths attributable to Ebola (main scenario: 30%; an additional analysis assumes 100%).[^mccabe-2026-8]

**Travel-based back-calculation (geographical spread model):** This method extends the approach described by Imai and colleagues (2020) during the COVID-19 pandemic, combining information on three confirmed imported BVD cases reported in Uganda as of May 27, 2026, estimates of daily international travel volumes from Ituri and Nord Kivu provinces to Uganda, assumptions about epidemic doubling time, and the presumption that all cases in symptomatic travellers will be detected at the time of recovery or death.[^mccabe-2026-9] The main analysis uses Ituri Province as the source population (n=4,392,200); a sensitivity run expands this to include Nord Kivu (combined n=12,593,600).[^mccabe-2026-10] An additional sensitivity analysis assumes only one imported case rather than three.[^mccabe-2026-11]

### Assumptions

**Proportion of deaths attributable to Ebola:** The percentage of deaths attributable to Ebola is assumed to lie between 30% and 100%.[^mccabe-2026-17] The main scenario uses 30%, with 100% explored in a supplementary analysis. This is a key driver: the estimated outbreak size increases with assumptions of a higher proportion of deaths due to Ebola.[^mccabe-2026-13]

**CFR and onset-to-death distribution:** The CFR is fixed at 33% (95% CI 26–40%) and the onset-to-death distribution is a gamma with mean 11.37 days and SD 5.41, both derived from previous Bundibugyo virus outbreaks.[^mccabe-2026-6] The assumption that these parameters are consistent with those from prior outbreaks carries substantial uncertainty.[^mccabe-2026-17]

**Epidemic growth rate:** The current epidemic growth rate is assumed to correspond to a doubling time of between 7 and 14 days, loosely based on early genomic analyses.[^mccabe-2026-17] The main scenario uses a 10-day doubling time; sensitivity analyses use 7- and 14-day doubling times.

**Single zoonotic spillover and exponential growth:** The model assumes exponential epidemic growth after a single zoonotic spillover event.[^mccabe-2026-7] No spatial structure is modelled.

**Geographic concentration of transmission:** Transmission is assumed to be largely concentrated in Ituri and Nord Kivu provinces.[^mccabe-2026-17]

**Travel volumes and traveller detection:** The geographical spread model presumes that all cases in symptomatic travellers will be detected at the time of recovery or death.[^mccabe-2026-15] The scale and patterns of population movement from these provinces to Uganda are assumed to be accurately captured in point-of-entry data.[^mccabe-2026-17]

**Medical-care-seeking bias in imported cases:** At least two of the three imported cases reportedly traveled to Uganda specifically to seek medical care, which the authors flag as a reason to interpret the sensitivity analysis expanding the source population to Nord Kivu with caution.[^mccabe-2026-18]

### Limitations

**Early-stage analysis under scarce data:** The analysis is an early assessment, primarily conducted to inform operational planning, and the estimates may change as more data and information become available.[^mccabe-2026-19]

**Uncertain assumptions with substantial individual uncertainty:** Each of the key assumptions — including the geographic concentration of transmission, the accuracy of travel flow data, the proportion of deaths attributable to Ebola, the applicability of historical CFR and onset-to-death parameters, and the epidemic doubling time — carries substantial uncertainty that may influence the estimates.[^mccabe-2026-20]

**Wide confidence intervals from small numerators:** The travel-based method rests on only three observed imported cases, producing a mean estimate of 945 cases with a 95% CI of 196–2,274[^mccabe-2026-14] — a very wide interval reflecting the small numerator.

**Incomplete and evolving case data:** Testing expanded rapidly for routine and retrospective assessment of suspected cases, deaths are harder to retrospectively assess with investigations ongoing, and suspected cases and deaths are no longer reported in recent situation reports[^mccabe-2026-21] — all of which limit the reliability of the input death counts.

**Medical-care-seeking bias in exported cases:** The findings from the sensitivity analysis expanding the source population should be interpreted with caution, as at least two of the three imported cases reportedly travelled to Uganda specifically to seek medical care[^mccabe-2026-18], making them non-representative of the broader travelling population.

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:** The analysis uses 1,031 suspected or confirmed cases and 240 suspected or confirmed deaths reported from 14 health zones across Ituri, Nord Kivu, and Sud Kivu as of May 27, 2026.[^mccabe-2026-3] It also uses 3 confirmed imported cases in Uganda from Ituri as of May 27, 2026 (with a total of 16 Uganda cases confirmed as of June 4, 2026).[^mccabe-2026-5]

**Auxiliary data:** The CFR (33%, 95% CI 26–40%) and the onset-to-death distribution (gamma, mean 11.37 days, SD 5.41) are derived from previous Bundibugyo virus outbreaks.[^mccabe-2026-6] Daily international travel volume estimates from Ituri and Nord Kivu to Uganda are used as inputs to the geographical spread model.[^mccabe-2026-15] The source population sizes are fixed values: Ituri Province at 4,392,200 and Ituri plus Nord Kivu at 12,593,600.[^mccabe-2026-10] The geographical spread method itself extends the analytical framework of Imai and colleagues (2020), developed during the COVID-19 pandemic.[^mccabe-2026-16]

:::{tab-item} Mooring et al. (24 May 2026)
### Mooring et al. (2026) Modeled Scenario Projections for the Ebola Disease Outbreak Caused by Bundibugyo Virus, 2026

[Read the report](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/75/wr/mm7522e1.htm)

### Overview

This CDC analysis uses a branching process transmission model to project the future trajectory of the 2026 BVD outbreak over a 90-day horizon, estimating how large the outbreak could become under different assumptions about the current number of deaths and different levels of patient isolation. The model was calibrated to three putative cumulative death counts and projected for four possible intervention scenarios ranging from poor (20%) to extremely high (95%) levels of isolation and treatment of symptomatic persons.[^mooring-2026-abstract-1] The central finding is that without high levels of patient isolation, the outbreak is likely to grow very large: with poor isolation levels of patients with BVD (20%) and no other interventions, the likelihood of an outbreak that exceeds 20,000 cases within 3 months is 65%.[^mooring-2026-abstract-2]

### Estimate

This analysis produces forward projections rather than a current true case count estimate; it does not provide a detection multiplier or a point estimate of true cases at the data cutoff. The projections are expressed as probabilities of exceeding case or death thresholds within 90 days of May 24, 2026, under three death-calibration scenarios and four isolation scenarios.

Under the **50 deaths calibration**: assuming 20% of infected persons were successfully isolated beginning May 24, 2026, projections showed ≥20,000 cumulative cases in 65% of simulations, ≥10,000 cumulative cases in 85% of simulations, and ≥4,000 cumulative deaths in 69% of simulations.[^mooring-2026-results-1] At 70% isolation, 94% of simulations projected <10,000 cases and only 1% projected ≥20,000 cases; similarly, 90% of simulations projected <2,000 deaths and only 3% projected ≥4,000 deaths.[^mooring-2026-results-2]

Under the **100 deaths calibration**: very large outbreaks were likely in the scenario in which only 20% of patients were isolated (76% of simulations projected ≥20,000 cases and 87% projected ≥4,000 deaths).[^mooring-2026-results-3] In the scenario in which 70% of infected persons were isolated, 73% of simulations projected <2,000 cumulative deaths by August 22, 2026, and 10% projected ≥4,000 deaths.[^mooring-2026-results-4]

Under the **200 deaths calibration**: even with 70% of infected persons isolated, 42% of simulations projected ≥10,000 cases by August 22, 2026.[^mooring-2026-results-5]

The median basic reproductive number R₀ = 2.51 (IQI = 2.27−2.82) in the main analysis calibrated to 50 deaths.[^mooring-2026-discussion-1]

### Methods

**Branching process model:** A branching process model with the capacity to model transmission-reducing nonpharmaceutical interventions was calibrated to three putative cumulative death counts and projected for four possible intervention scenarios.[^mooring-2026-abstract-1] Each simulated outbreak was initialized with one infected person representing a zoonotic spillover event; this person infected a randomly generated number of additional persons based on assumptions about the basic reproductive number (R₀), and any infected persons were added to the simulation at times selected according to the distribution of intervals from one infection to the next, in turn able to cause further infections.[^mooring-2026-methods-1] The model was adapted from one applied to previous viral hemorrhagic fever outbreaks, including a Marburg virus disease outbreak in Ethiopia in 2025.[^mooring-2026-methods-2]

**Calibration to cumulative deaths:** The model was calibrated to three different numbers of cumulative deaths (50, 100, and 200) to account for uncertainty in the current number of deaths caused by BVD; a simulated outbreak was considered compatible with the real-world outbreak if it reached the assumed number of cumulative deaths by May 24, 2026, and if the first death occurred on or before April 24, 2026. Outbreaks were simulated until 500 simulations met these criteria.[^mooring-2026-methods-3] Assumptions for the cumulative number of BVD deaths as of May 24, 2026, were based on publicly available situation reports from DRC.[^mooring-2026-methods-4]

**Scenario projections:** Four intervention scenarios were assessed for each calibration, each implementing a different level of isolation (i.e., percentage of symptomatic infected persons detected, isolated, and treated: 20% [poor], 50% [moderate], 70% [high], and 95% [extremely high]).[^mooring-2026-methods-5] Each simulation reported the cumulative number of cases and cumulative number of deaths from the date of spillover until August 22, which would be 90 days after interventions began.[^mooring-2026-methods-6] The analysis applies to the DRC outbreak broadly, without explicit subnational stratification.

### Assumptions

**Zoonotic seed and single-origin start:** Each simulated outbreak was initialized with one infected person, who represented the person first infected from a zoonotic source (a spillover event).[^mooring-2026-methods-8] This structural assumption of a single spillover constrains inferences about outbreak onset timing.

**Basic reproductive number (R₀):** Each simulated infected person infected a randomly generated number of additional persons based on assumptions about R₀, the average number of persons in a susceptible population infected by an infected person.[^mooring-2026-methods-9] The median R₀ = 2.51 (IQI = 2.27−2.82) in the main analysis calibrated to 50 deaths.[^mooring-2026-discussion-1] Basic reproductive number estimates for Ebola disease vary widely across outbreaks, and the true value of R₀ for this outbreak might be higher or lower than the values used in this analysis.[^mooring-2026-discussion-4] The analysis did not provide evidence that R₀ for this outbreak is unusually large.[^mooring-2026-discussion-5]

**Delay distributions:** Intervals from infection to symptom onset, symptom onset to death, and symptom onset to recovery were held constant for all infections within each simulated outbreak but varied among simulated outbreaks.[^mooring-2026-methods-10] The specific distributional forms and parameter values are not detailed in this document beyond that they were drawn from published Ebola estimates.

**Cumulative deaths at calibration date:** The model was calibrated to three different numbers of cumulative deaths (50, 100, and 200) to account for uncertainty in the current number of deaths caused by BVD.[^mooring-2026-methods-11] The true number of BVD deaths that occurred through May 24, 2026, is unknown; some deaths from BVD might not have been confirmed, and it is possible that other deaths might have been incorrectly attributed to BVD.[^mooring-2026-discussion-6]

**Isolation intervention mechanics:** On the intervention start date, the designated percentage of symptomatic persons was selected to begin isolating, with an average delay of 2 days until isolation and treatment; the same percentage of persons who later developed signs or symptoms was selected to begin isolating with an average delay of 2 days from symptom onset. Simulated persons in isolation were prevented from causing any onward transmission, and the model implicitly assumed that isolated persons who died were safely buried.[^mooring-2026-methods-12]

**No behavioral change:** Changes in behavior that reduce risk for infection (e.g., avoiding contact with ill persons) were not included in the model and might help limit outbreak size.[^mooring-2026-discussion-7]

**No immunity accumulation:** The model did not account for transmission reductions attributable to an increase in the proportion of the population with infection-induced immunity. Given the population size of the communities where this outbreak is occurring, this limitation is unlikely to affect the validity of the projections over the time span and numerical ranges of cases presented in this analysis; however, the model could project unrealistically large outbreaks if applied to longer periods.[^mooring-2026-discussion-8]

**No relapses:** The model did not include infection relapses after recovery, which is unlikely to affect this analysis, but relapses could be important drivers of the course of the epidemic over a longer period.[^mooring-2026-discussion-9]

### Limitations

**Uncertainty in current death counts:** The true number of BVD deaths that occurred through May 24, 2026, is unknown. Some deaths from BVD might not have been confirmed; similarly, it is possible that other deaths might have been incorrectly attributed to BVD.[^mooring-2026-discussion-6] The analysis addresses this by calibrating to three different death counts (50, 100, 200) rather than resolving the uncertainty.

**R₀ uncertainty:** Basic reproductive number estimates for Ebola disease vary widely across outbreaks, and the true value of R₀ for this outbreak might be higher or lower than the values used in this analysis. High-quality data on changes in the number of cases and deaths over time are essential to more precisely estimate R₀.[^mooring-2026-discussion-10]

**Behavioral change not modelled:** Changes in behavior that reduce risk for infection (e.g., avoiding contact with ill persons) were not included in the model and might help limit outbreak size.[^mooring-2026-discussion-7]

**No immunity depletion:** The model did not account for transmission reductions attributable to an increase in the proportion of the population with infection-induced immunity, which is unlikely to affect projections over the time span and numerical ranges presented but could cause the model to project unrealistically large outbreaks if applied to longer periods.[^mooring-2026-discussion-8]

**No relapses:** The model did not include infection relapses after recovery, which is unlikely to affect this analysis, but relapses could be important drivers of the course of the epidemic over a longer period.[^mooring-2026-discussion-9]

### Data sources

**Primary outbreak data:** Assumptions for the cumulative number of BVD deaths as of May 24, 2026, were based on publicly available situation reports from DRC.[^mooring-2026-methods-4] The DRC national public health institute reported 10 confirmed deaths and 223 suspected deaths as of May 24, 2026.[^mooring-2026-discussion-2] A subsequent report with data as of June 2, 2026, reported a cumulative total of 62 confirmed deaths in DRC.[^mooring-2026-discussion-3] These reports drove the three calibration targets (50, 100, and 200 deaths), used to bracket uncertainty about true death counts.

**Auxiliary data (parameters from prior outbreaks):** Assumptions about parameters were based on published estimates from previous Ebola outbreaks; estimates specific to BVD were used when available.[^mooring-2026-methods-7] The analysis does not specify by name which published studies or datasets were used for delay distributions or other parameters. No travel data, demographic data, or line list data from the current outbreak are mentioned as inputs.
