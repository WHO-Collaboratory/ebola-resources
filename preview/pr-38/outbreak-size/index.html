Outbreak Size

Latest analysis data cutoff: 6 July 2026. Page refreshed: 9 July 2026.

At a glance

Over time the under-ascertainment ratio has narrowed from roughly 40–90× against 10 confirmed cases on 20 May, to 10–19× against 101 confirmed cases on 22 May, to 2.0–3.7× against 1,708 confirmed cases on 6 July. This is based on four separate analyses, whose current-size estimates converge on substantial under-ascertainment. The evidence suggests surveillance has been catching up but true infections still exceed confirmed counts, with low-to-moderate confidence.

Synthesizing evidence¶

State of the evidence¶

The evidence base consists of four analyses between 20 May to 6 July 2026 employing a range of methods. McCabe et al. use export-based and death-based back-calculation, Epistorm uses exportation-informed ABC with mobility data, Abbott et al. extends McCabe into a live joint Bayesian renewal model, and Mooring et al. projects future size under isolation scenarios rather than estimating a cutoff multiplier.[1][2][3][4] The evidence is growing, but still thin for the question of current outbreak size.

How the estimates compare¶

The clearest signal is a narrowing detection gap. McCabe et al. estimate roughly 400–900 cases on 20 May, implying about 40–90× the 10 confirmed cases then identified, while Epistorm’s 22 May medians imply about 10–19× the 101 confirmed cases under alternative export-detection scenarios.[5][6][7][8] By 6 July, Abbott et al. estimate 3,358–6,377 infections against 1,708 confirmed cases, or 2.0–3.7×, using more surveillance streams and later data.[9] Differences reflect date, geography, export assumptions, and whether suspected deaths, confirmed cases, genetics, or mobility data drive the inference.

Key uncertainties and assumptions¶

All outbreak size estimates depend on sparse early signals being interpreted through strong assumptions. Shared uncertainties include export detection and travel volumes, BVD delay distributions and CFR borrowed from previous outbreaks, incomplete or revised surveillance counts, and limited epidemiological dating of cases or deaths.[10][11][12][13] If these shared assumptions fail in the same direction, all estimates could move together.

What would reduce uncertainty¶

The most important addition would be a line list dated by symptom onset, testing, reporting, outcome, and location, because timing is currently inferred mainly through assumed delays.[13][14] Second would be better classification of suspected deaths and cases, including retrospective testing, because death-based and confirmed-case streams anchor several estimates. Third would be more complete cross-border movement and export-detection information; Epistorm notes that additional exports would tighten posterior intervals and help identify the plausible ascertainment regime.[15]

What this means for the response¶

The evidence suggests substantial under-detection early on, with surveillance improving enough that the confirmed-count gap appears smaller by July. It still suggests true infections exceed confirmed cases, so confirmed counts alone understate burden, committed care needs, and transmission potential. Geographic focus remains centered on Ituri and neighbouring eastern DRC, with exportations to Uganda and through regional corridors informing preparedness beyond the immediate outbreak zone.[16] Abbott et al. also suggests national capacity summaries can mask local pressure.[17]

Abbott et al. (2026) Estimating the current size of the 2026 DRC Bundibugyo virus outbreak¶

Read the report

Overview¶

This analysis estimates the true cumulative size of the 2026 BVD outbreak in DRC, accounting for undetected infections not captured in surveillance counts. It is a real-time joint Bayesian estimate, refreshed as new data arrive, using a discrete-time renewal process that fits multiple surveillance streams jointly in a single posterior: DRC suspected cases, suspected deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and confirmed deaths, and cases and deaths exported to Uganda.[18][19] The headline finding is that the outbreak is estimated to have caused approximately 3,358–6,377 infections (90% credible interval) by the data cutoff, which is roughly 2.0–3.7 times the 1,708 laboratory-confirmed cases reported at that date.[9]

Estimate¶

The headline joint-model estimate of cumulative infections is: 30% credible interval 4,174–4,811; 60% credible interval 3,835–5,269; 90% credible interval 3,358–6,377 infections to date, reported and unreported.[20]

Against the 1,708 laboratory-confirmed cases by the cutoff, that is roughly 2.0–3.7× as many infections, so confirmed cases are estimated to capture only a small share of the outbreak.[21]

The initial reproduction number is estimated at 30% interval 1.47–1.67, 60% interval 1.37–1.82, 90% interval 1.25–2.13; the latest reproduction number is estimated at 30% interval 1.21–1.53, 60% interval 1.06–1.71, 90% interval 0.82–2.20.[22]

The case-fatality ratio is estimated at 30% interval 0.40–0.46, 60% interval 0.37–0.49, 90% interval 0.32–0.56.[23]

The data moves the cumulative infection count estimate by only 0.04 prior interquartile ranges from its prior, the outbreak age by −0.47, and the doubling time by 0.03; the largest move is in the outbreak age.[24]

Methods¶

Discrete-time renewal process fitted jointly to multiple surveillance streams: The model is a discrete-time renewal process on a daily grid that fits surveillance streams jointly in a single posterior — DRC suspected cases, suspected deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and confirmed deaths, and cases and deaths exported to Uganda — and estimates the latent infections, symptom onsets and deaths over time, the reported and confirmed cases, the time-varying reproduction number with its growth rate and doubling time, alongside the case-fatality ratio, the ascertainment of each surveillance system, and a short-term forecast of each stream over the coming week.[25] This work began as a replication of the McCabe et al. report and has since evolved into a real-time joint Bayesian extension, a discrete-time renewal process with a time-varying reproduction number fitted to more of the available data streams than the original.[3] The model covers DRC nationally (with an Ituri / Nord Kivu structural split) and incorporates Uganda exports as an additional likelihood stream. Alongside the joint model, single-stream models are fitted for each count-based stream (exported cases, suspected deaths, suspected cases, laboratory-confirmed cases, confirmed deaths, and deaths among exports), so each stream’s posterior over the outbreak size can be compared with the joint; other model variants cut the data to an earlier date or drop certain counts.[26]

Two-phase seeding with a genetically-floored outbreak age: A single zoonotic import grows through an unobserved cryptic exponential phase to a magnitude set by a wide prior on the doubling count, at the growth rate the genetic estimate informs, before the renewal process takes over; the established reproduction number is derived forward from that growth rate, and the genetic time to the most recent common ancestor floors the cryptic duration from below.[27] A BEAST time tree of the first ten sequenced genomes places the TMRCA at a mean of 25 March 2026, and the molecular clock rate is fixed at 1.2×10⁻³ substitutions/site/year from the 2013–2016 West African Ebola epidemic.[28]

Sensitivity analyses: Two sensitivity variants are run: a delay sensitivity and a clock sensitivity, both re-fit from the joint model structure with modified inputs.[29] These vary the delay distributions and the molecular clock assumption respectively, and their results are compared to the main joint fit.

Assumptions¶

Generation interval: The generation interval is assumed to be Gamma-distributed, with shape near 2.71 and scale near 5.65, centred on the Ebola virus disease serial interval (mean 15.3 days, SD 9.3 days; WHO Ebola Response Team 2014) used as a generation-time proxy. Priors are centred on those values, with spreads carrying the source’s reported uncertainty on the mean.[30]

Incubation period and delay distributions: The incubation period mean prior reproduces the MacNeil et al. (2010) 95% CI from the 2007 Uganda outbreak (mean 6.3 days); the SD prior is the authors’ own choice.[31] Onset-to-report follows a Gamma with implied mean about 4 days, from a Bayesian reanalysis of the 2012 Isiro line list; shape prior α ~ Normal⁺(1.18, 0.28) and scale prior θ ~ Normal⁺(3.69, 1.20).[32] Onset-to-death is the convolution of onset-to-admission and admission-to-death Gammas, with implied mean about 13 days.[33] The report-to-receipt laboratory delay has no per-sample outbreak data grounding it, so the prior is the authors’ own choice: mean ~ Normal⁺(4.5, 1.0) days, SD ~ Normal⁺(4.0, 0.75) days.[34]

Case-fatality ratio: The CFR prior is Beta(6.6, 13.4), with mean 0.33 and 95% interval roughly 15–54%, matching the CDC figure of 55 deaths in 169 cases across the two previous BVD outbreaks, and informed by a BDBV reanalysis reporting a baseline of 0.47 (95% CrI 0.31–0.65) for non-HCW confirmed cases.[35][36]

Ascertainment: The DRC and Uganda ascertainment fractions share a logit-scale hyperprior with mean centred on a reporting fraction of 75%, reflecting the active case-finding of a declared Ebola response rather than baseline passive surveillance.[37]

Reproduction number and time-variation: The reproduction number is held flat at the established R₀ until a month before the first WHO situation report, then follows a non-centred Gaussian random walk on the log scale with weekly knots, with the month-long lead allowing Rt to start moving before the outbreak was formally reported.[38] An outbreak response effect is modelled as a logistic ramp at the first WHO situation report on 18 May 2026, assumed to take about three weeks to take full effect and constrained to be non-positive (can only reduce transmission).[39]

Cryptic phase and outbreak seed: The outbreak is assumed to start from a single zoonotic spillover; it then grows deterministically through an unobserved cryptic exponential phase, doubling m times (prior m ~ Normal⁺(3, 3)) before sustained transmission is established, over a duration m·τ where τ is the doubling time.[40][41] The intervention ramp effect and the pre-ramp reproduction number are weakly identified given only a few situation reports straddling it.[42]

Molecular clock and genetic bound: The molecular clock rate is fixed at 1.2×10⁻³ substitutions/site/year from the 2013–2016 West African Ebola epidemic; the TMRCA is treated as a lower bound on the outbreak age, and adding sequences can only push it earlier, never later.[43] The clock rate is fixed and neither cross-outbreak nor clock uncertainty is propagated.[44]

Cross-border travel: McCabe et al. Table 3 records mean weekly passenger counts across seven points of entry; the Ituri-side daily total of 1,871 is a sample mean across roughly 15–21 point-of-entry-weeks. A Normal prior centred on 1,871 with SD 200 (~10% CV), truncated at zero, is used; the source population is kept fixed at the census value.[45]

Stream independence: Streams are fitted as conditionally independent given latent incidence but observe overlapping people, which can understate uncertainty. Whether the streams imply mutually consistent outbreak sizes is not assessed.[46]

Non-BVD background: The laboratory positivity (only 210/755 ≈ 0.28 of analysed specimens are positive) identifies the background, which is inferred to be the majority of the suspect pool.[47]

Prior informativeness: Nearly all of the delays, the CFR, and the laboratory assumptions are set by priors informed at best by a handful of literature sources, often from other outbreaks, and in places by prior judgement rather than anything from this outbreak. The data do little to move them, so posteriors largely track their priors.[48]

Limitations¶

Reliance on weakly-informed priors: Nearly all of the delays, the case-fatality ratio, and the laboratory assumptions are set by priors informed at best by a handful of literature sources, often from other outbreaks, and in places by prior judgement rather than anything specific to this outbreak. The data do little to move them, so these posteriors largely track their priors. The between-report increments inform the change in the reproduction number over the window but are uninformative about the delays, the surveillance dispersion, or the reporting fractions on their own.[49]

Aggregate data and weak timing signal: Only report-date totals are available; there are no counts by symptom onset or any other epidemiologically relevant date. The timing of the underlying epidemic is therefore weakly identified, and is recovered only through the assumed delays.[13] The DRC data are situation-report totals of suspected cases and deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and deaths, and specimens received and analysed; there is no line list and no information on case definitions or reporting completeness. Every estimate is a model-based extrapolation under strong assumptions, not a measurement.[14][50]

Revisions not modelled: A later situation report can revise an earlier total up or down as suspects are reclassified and newly-reporting health zones are added, and ascertainment probably rose over the window. This revision process is not modelled.[51]

Stream independence: Surveillance streams are fitted as conditionally independent given latent incidence but observe overlapping people, which can understate uncertainty. Whether the streams imply mutually consistent outbreak sizes is not assessed.[46]

Intervention ramp weakly identified: With only a few situation reports straddling the outbreak response ramp, the ramp effect and the pre-ramp reproduction number are not well separated.[42]

National bed model cannot capture local saturation: The treatment-centre model carries one national bed capacity and one national demand, so it cannot represent local saturation. On 13 June Ituri was at 93.9% occupancy while Sud-Kivu was at 21.9%, meaning the national bed shortfall understates local unmet need.[17]

Inherited epidemiological assumptions: The model inherits McCabe et al.'s epidemiological assumptions: a single zoonotic seed, an assumed generation interval, no spatial structure beyond the Ituri / Nord Kivu split, and no depletion of susceptibles. The onset-to-death delay is grounded on Isiro 2012 and the genetic seeding bound on an external clock rate, neither propagating cross-outbreak or clock uncertainty.[52]

Code not independently replicated: The model code, priors, and analysis were drafted by a language model from the McCabe et al. report and the companion delay reanalysis, then reviewed and revised under human oversight. The work has not been independently replicated against the authors’ code.[53]

Data sources¶

Primary outbreak data:

DRC data come from the situation reports of the Institut National de Santé Publique (INSP). Each report gives the national cumulative suspected cases and deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and deaths, and the specimens received and analysed by the laboratory, at the report date.[54] At the data cutoff (6 July 2026), the analysis records 1,708 confirmed cases and 580 confirmed deaths.[55] Suspected deaths were frozen at 246 and suspected cases at 1,077, both as of 26 May 2026.[56] From SitRep 013 (27 May), INSP began reclassifying suspects, so the cumulative suspected count falls; the analysis freezes it at its last stable vintage (26 May) and instead fits the daily new-suspect count published from 4 June.[57]

Uganda data are the cases and the one death exported across the border, taken from WHO situation reports and Disease Outbreak News.[58] Three exported cases (last dated 23 May 2026) and one export death (last dated 14 May 2026) are recorded.[59]

A genetic TMRCA bound is used, placing the most recent common ancestor at 25 March 2026 (103 days before the cutoff).[60]

Auxiliary data:

The cross-border traveller volume and source population come from McCabe et al.; the source population is fixed and the traveller volume is given a Normal prior around the McCabe et al. figure.[61] The prior mean for daily outbound travellers is 1,871 (SD 200), and the source population is fixed at 4,392,200.[62][63]

The incubation period is drawn from the Bundibugyo virus estimate from the 2007 Uganda outbreak (mean 6.3 days, 95% CI 5.2–7.3, n=24; MacNeil et al., 2010).[64]

The onset-to-report delay comes from a Bayesian reanalysis of the 2012 Isiro Bundibugyo virus outbreak line list (Funk and Abbott, 2026), taking the onset-to-admission delay as a Gamma with implied mean about 4 days.[65]

The CFR prior is informed by the CDC summary of 55 deaths in 169 cases (~33%) across the two previous BVD outbreaks.[66]

The generation interval is a Gamma distribution based on the Ebola virus disease serial interval used as a generation-time proxy (mean 15.3 days, SD 9.3 days; WHO Ebola Response Team 2014).[67]

The confirmed onset-to-sample delay is grounded on the NEJM DRC 2026 Bundibugyo virus cohort (Akilimali et al., 2026; N=129 confirmed-positive), which estimated a mean of 7.4 days (95% CrI 5.3–13.5) and median of 4.8 days (95% CrI 3.46–7.84).[68]

Mooring et al. (2026) Modeled Scenario Projections for the Ebola Disease Outbreak Caused by Bundibugyo Virus, 2026¶

Read the report

Overview¶

This CDC analysis uses a branching process transmission model to project the future trajectory of the 2026 BVD outbreak over a 90-day horizon, estimating how large the outbreak could become under different assumptions about the current number of deaths and different levels of patient isolation. The model was calibrated to three putative cumulative death counts and projected for four possible intervention scenarios ranging from poor (20%) to extremely high (95%) levels of isolation and treatment of symptomatic persons.[4] The central finding is that without high levels of patient isolation, the outbreak is likely to grow very large: with poor isolation levels of patients with BVD (20%) and no other interventions, the likelihood of an outbreak that exceeds 20,000 cases within 3 months is 65%.[69]

Estimate¶

This analysis produces forward projections rather than a current true case count estimate; it does not provide a detection multiplier or a point estimate of true cases at the data cutoff. The projections are expressed as probabilities of exceeding case or death thresholds within 90 days of May 24, 2026, under three death-calibration scenarios and four isolation scenarios.

Under the 50 deaths calibration: assuming 20% of infected persons were successfully isolated beginning May 24, 2026, projections showed ≥20,000 cumulative cases in 65% of simulations, ≥10,000 cumulative cases in 85% of simulations, and ≥4,000 cumulative deaths in 69% of simulations.[70] At 70% isolation, 94% of simulations projected <10,000 cases and only 1% projected ≥20,000 cases; similarly, 90% of simulations projected <2,000 deaths and only 3% projected ≥4,000 deaths.[71]

Under the 100 deaths calibration: very large outbreaks were likely in the scenario in which only 20% of patients were isolated (76% of simulations projected ≥20,000 cases and 87% projected ≥4,000 deaths).[72] In the scenario in which 70% of infected persons were isolated, 73% of simulations projected <2,000 cumulative deaths by August 22, 2026, and 10% projected ≥4,000 deaths.[73]

Under the 200 deaths calibration: even with 70% of infected persons isolated, 42% of simulations projected ≥10,000 cases by August 22, 2026.[74]

The median basic reproductive number R₀ = 2.51 (IQI = 2.27−2.82) in the main analysis calibrated to 50 deaths.[75]

Methods¶

Branching process model: A branching process model with the capacity to model transmission-reducing nonpharmaceutical interventions was calibrated to three putative cumulative death counts and projected for four possible intervention scenarios.[4] Each simulated outbreak was initialized with one infected person representing a zoonotic spillover event; this person infected a randomly generated number of additional persons based on assumptions about the basic reproductive number (R₀), and any infected persons were added to the simulation at times selected according to the distribution of intervals from one infection to the next, in turn able to cause further infections.[76] The model was adapted from one applied to previous viral hemorrhagic fever outbreaks, including a Marburg virus disease outbreak in Ethiopia in 2025.[77]

Calibration to cumulative deaths: The model was calibrated to three different numbers of cumulative deaths (50, 100, and 200) to account for uncertainty in the current number of deaths caused by BVD; a simulated outbreak was considered compatible with the real-world outbreak if it reached the assumed number of cumulative deaths by May 24, 2026, and if the first death occurred on or before April 24, 2026. Outbreaks were simulated until 500 simulations met these criteria.[78] Assumptions for the cumulative number of BVD deaths as of May 24, 2026, were based on publicly available situation reports from DRC.[79]

Scenario projections: Four intervention scenarios were assessed for each calibration, each implementing a different level of isolation (i.e., percentage of symptomatic infected persons detected, isolated, and treated: 20% [poor], 50% [moderate], 70% [high], and 95% [extremely high]).[80] Each simulation reported the cumulative number of cases and cumulative number of deaths from the date of spillover until August 22, which would be 90 days after interventions began.[81] The analysis applies to the DRC outbreak broadly, without explicit subnational stratification.

Assumptions¶

Zoonotic seed and single-origin start: Each simulated outbreak was initialized with one infected person, who represented the person first infected from a zoonotic source (a spillover event).[82] This structural assumption of a single spillover constrains inferences about outbreak onset timing.

Basic reproductive number (R₀): Each simulated infected person infected a randomly generated number of additional persons based on assumptions about R₀, the average number of persons in a susceptible population infected by an infected person.[83] The median R₀ = 2.51 (IQI = 2.27−2.82) in the main analysis calibrated to 50 deaths.[75] Basic reproductive number estimates for Ebola disease vary widely across outbreaks, and the true value of R₀ for this outbreak might be higher or lower than the values used in this analysis.[84] The analysis did not provide evidence that R₀ for this outbreak is unusually large.[85]

Delay distributions: Intervals from infection to symptom onset, symptom onset to death, and symptom onset to recovery were held constant for all infections within each simulated outbreak but varied among simulated outbreaks.[86] The specific distributional forms and parameter values are not detailed in this document beyond that they were drawn from published Ebola estimates.

Cumulative deaths at calibration date: The model was calibrated to three different numbers of cumulative deaths (50, 100, and 200) to account for uncertainty in the current number of deaths caused by BVD.[87] The true number of BVD deaths that occurred through May 24, 2026, is unknown; some deaths from BVD might not have been confirmed, and it is possible that other deaths might have been incorrectly attributed to BVD.[88]

Isolation intervention mechanics: On the intervention start date, the designated percentage of symptomatic persons was selected to begin isolating, with an average delay of 2 days until isolation and treatment; the same percentage of persons who later developed signs or symptoms was selected to begin isolating with an average delay of 2 days from symptom onset. Simulated persons in isolation were prevented from causing any onward transmission, and the model implicitly assumed that isolated persons who died were safely buried.[89]

No behavioral change: Changes in behavior that reduce risk for infection (e.g., avoiding contact with ill persons) were not included in the model and might help limit outbreak size.[90]

No immunity accumulation: The model did not account for transmission reductions attributable to an increase in the proportion of the population with infection-induced immunity. Given the population size of the communities where this outbreak is occurring, this limitation is unlikely to affect the validity of the projections over the time span and numerical ranges of cases presented in this analysis; however, the model could project unrealistically large outbreaks if applied to longer periods.[91]

No relapses: The model did not include infection relapses after recovery, which is unlikely to affect this analysis, but relapses could be important drivers of the course of the epidemic over a longer period.[92]

Limitations¶

Uncertainty in current death counts: The true number of BVD deaths that occurred through May 24, 2026, is unknown. Some deaths from BVD might not have been confirmed; similarly, it is possible that other deaths might have been incorrectly attributed to BVD.[88] The analysis addresses this by calibrating to three different death counts (50, 100, 200) rather than resolving the uncertainty.

R₀ uncertainty: Basic reproductive number estimates for Ebola disease vary widely across outbreaks, and the true value of R₀ for this outbreak might be higher or lower than the values used in this analysis. High-quality data on changes in the number of cases and deaths over time are essential to more precisely estimate R₀.[93]

Behavioral change not modelled: Changes in behavior that reduce risk for infection (e.g., avoiding contact with ill persons) were not included in the model and might help limit outbreak size.[90]

No immunity depletion: The model did not account for transmission reductions attributable to an increase in the proportion of the population with infection-induced immunity, which is unlikely to affect projections over the time span and numerical ranges presented but could cause the model to project unrealistically large outbreaks if applied to longer periods.[91]

No relapses: The model did not include infection relapses after recovery, which is unlikely to affect this analysis, but relapses could be important drivers of the course of the epidemic over a longer period.[92]

Data sources¶

Primary outbreak data: Assumptions for the cumulative number of BVD deaths as of May 24, 2026, were based on publicly available situation reports from DRC.[79] The DRC national public health institute reported 10 confirmed deaths and 223 suspected deaths as of May 24, 2026.[94] A subsequent report with data as of June 2, 2026, reported a cumulative total of 62 confirmed deaths in DRC.[95] These reports drove the three calibration targets (50, 100, and 200 deaths), used to bracket uncertainty about true death counts.

Auxiliary data (parameters from prior outbreaks): Assumptions about parameters were based on published estimates from previous Ebola outbreaks; estimates specific to BVD were used when available.[96] The analysis does not specify by name which published studies or datasets were used for delay distributions or other parameters. No travel data, demographic data, or line list data from the current outbreak are mentioned as inputs.

Epistorm (2026) Ebola Bundibugyo Virus — DRC · Spread Risk Assessment #2¶

Read the report

Overview¶

This analysis estimates the true size of the 2026 BVD outbreak in DRC by using detected international exportations as observational evidence within an Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC) framework coupled to a global mobility model. The report re-estimates the underlying outbreak size using detected importations within the ABC framework, across three importation-detection-efficiency scenarios (100%, 66%, and 50%, corresponding to 4, 6, and 8 true exported cases given the four observed).[2] Across these scenarios, the estimated underlying outbreak size ranges from a posterior median of 1,047 infections (90% CrI: 443–2,043) under 100% detection to 1,944 infections (90% CrI: 1,056–3,224) under 50% detection.[7]

Estimate¶

Under the most conservative scenario (100% importation detection), the posterior median outbreak size is 1,047 infections (90% CrI: 443–2,043).[97] Under the mid-range scenario (66% importation detection), the median is 1,495 infections (90% CrI: 739–2,644).[98] Under the most expansive scenario (50% importation detection), the median rises to 1,944 infections (90% CrI: 1,056–3,224).[99] All estimates apply to the multi-province source footprint (Ituri, North Kivu, and Kampala) as of 22 May 2026. Across all three scenarios, the lower 5th percentile of the posterior remains well above 400 infections, consistent with the surveillance gaps documented in the WHO situation report and the reported healthcare-worker deaths.[100] The confirmed case count at the same cutoff date was 101 laboratory-confirmed cases, implying detection multipliers of approximately 10× at the conservative-scenario median, 15× at the mid-range median, and 19× at the expansive-scenario median relative to confirmed cases — though the analysis does not state these multipliers explicitly.

Methods¶

ABC outbreak-size inference coupled to a mobility model: The number of infections at the origin is inferred using an Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC) approach. Given the observed international exportations as data, the posterior distribution over outbreak size is approximated by repeatedly simulating outbreaks of varying sizes, generating synthetic exportation events under the mobility model, and retaining parameter values whose simulated outputs are consistent with the observations.[101] The modeling approach follows the GLEAM (Global Epidemic and Mobility) computational framework, which, for each potential destination, estimates the probability that an infected individual originating in the affected area travels there, conditional on at least one exported case occurring; the output is a relative risk distribution across all candidate destinations.[102] Global air travel patterns are derived from IATA and OAG passenger-level origin-destination flows, complemented by short-scale commuting patterns capturing daily movements between adjacent subpopulations.[103] The method is applied to the multi-province source footprint of Ituri, North Kivu, and Kampala. Three importation-detection-efficiency scenarios — 100%, 66%, and 50% — are reported to bound sensitivity to undercounting, corresponding to true export counts of 4, 6, and 8 given the four independent events observed.[104]

Assumptions¶

Importation-detection efficiency: The outbreak-size inference is sensitive to the assumed importation-detection-efficiency scenario; actual importation-detection efficiency in Uganda is not directly observed and may vary by port of entry, healthcare-seeking pathway, and clinical presentation.[12] The three scenarios (100%, 66%, 50%) are not themselves derived from data but are chosen to bracket plausible ranges of undetected exportation.

Incubation period: The incubation period of BVD (2–21 days; average ~10 days based on the 2007 Uganda outbreak) conditions the inference, constraining the window within which an exported case could reasonably travel while infectious or pre-symptomatic.[105] This parameter is fixed rather than varied across scenarios.

Independence of importation events: Case importations are modeled as statistically independent events. Real-world events involving multiple related cases traveling together are counted as a single importation event; to the extent that such clusters are common, the model will under-count the number of distinct exposure events while correctly counting the number of distinct geographic seedings.[106]

Traveler homogeneity: The mobility model does not incorporate sociodemographic attributes of travelers (age, economic status, occupation, or pre-existing medical conditions), any of which may modulate both exposure risk at origin and care-seeking behavior at destination. Travel probability within a catchment area is treated as independent of these attributes and of specific location within the catchment.[107]

Scope of mobility data: Mobility data may not fully capture informal cross-border movement, which is substantial in the Ituri–Uganda corridor.[108]

No downstream transmission modeled: The model does not predict downstream transmission chains following importation events; risk estimates reflect the probability of a single importation and do not account for the probability of sustained local transmission in the receiving location. Relative importation risk estimates are conditional on at least one exported case occurring and describe how exportation probability is distributed across destinations given that exportation happens, not the absolute probability that any particular destination will receive a case.[109]

Limitations¶

Sensitivity to unobserved detection efficiency: The outbreak-size inference is sensitive to the assumed importation-detection-efficiency scenario, and actual importation-detection efficiency in Uganda is not directly observed and may vary by port of entry, healthcare-seeking pathway, and clinical presentation.[12] The three scenarios bracket this uncertainty but cannot resolve it.

Informal cross-border movement not fully captured: Mobility data may not fully capture informal cross-border movement, which is substantial in the Ituri–Uganda corridor,[108] potentially understating the volume of exposure events that could produce exportations.

Traveler heterogeneity not modelled: The model does not incorporate sociodemographic attributes of travelers — age, economic status, occupation, or pre-existing medical conditions — any of which may modulate both exposure risk at origin and care-seeking behavior at destination. Travel probability within a catchment area is treated as independent of these attributes and of specific location within the catchment.[107]

Cluster travel counted as single importation: Real-world events involving multiple related cases traveling together are counted as a single importation event. To the extent that such clusters are common, the model will under-count the number of distinct exposure events while correctly counting the number of distinct geographic seedings.[110]

No downstream transmission modelled: The model does not predict downstream transmission chains following importation events: risk estimates reflect the probability of a single importation and do not account for the probability of sustained local transmission in the receiving location.[111]

Posterior intervals will remain wide until more exports are observed: As additional exported cases are confirmed, posterior intervals will tighten and the most consistent ascertainment regime will become identifiable,[15] implying that the current wide credible intervals are a direct consequence of the small number of observed exportations.

Data sources¶

Primary outbreak data: As of 22 May 2026, DRC had reported 101 laboratory-confirmed cases, 904 suspected cases, and 119 suspected deaths across health zones in Ituri (Bunia, Rwampara, Nyankunde, Mongbwalu) and North Kivu provinces.[8] Four independent importation events constitute the primary observational evidence for the ABC inference: three to Kampala (two travelers detected on 15–16 May and one retrospective case traced to 14 May travel) and one to Bukavu, South Kivu; two additional Kampala cases are local contacts of travelers and do not constitute independent importations.[112]

Overview¶

This analysis applies two independent approaches to estimate the true size of the BVD outbreak in the DRC as of 20 May 2026: the first uses population movement data alongside evidence of exported cases detected in Uganda; the second back-calculates cases from reported suspected deaths, combined with estimates of the case fatality ratio and onset-to-death distribution from previous Bundibugyo outbreaks.[1] Both methods yield broadly consistent results, suggesting that approximately 400 to 900 cases of BVD may have occurred in the DRC, with values above 1,000 not being excludable given current data.[5]

Estimate¶

Method 1 (travel-based back-calculation), main scenario (Ituri source population): Under a 10-day detection window, the estimate is 470 (95% CI: 58 to 1,306); under a 15-day window, 313 (95% CI: 39 to 870); under a 20-day window, 235 (95% CI: 29 to 652).[117] Under the sensitivity analysis expanding the source population to Ituri and Nord Kivu, the corresponding estimates are 617 (95% CI: 76 to 1,718), 412 (95% CI: 51 to 1,145), and 309 (95% CI: 38 to 858).[118]

Method 2 (back-calculation from deaths): The main scenario (14-day doubling time) yields 860 (95% CI: 721 to 1,015) cases at CFR = 26%, 678 (95% CI: 568 to 800) at CFR = 33%, and 559 (95% CI: 469 to 660) at CFR = 40%.[119] The fast-growth sensitivity (7-day doubling time) yields 1,386 (95% CI: 1,160 to 1,636) at CFR = 26%, 1,092 (95% CI: 914 to 1,289) at CFR = 33%, and 901 (95% CI: 754 to 1,062) at CFR = 40%.[120] The slow-growth sensitivity (21-day doubling time) yields 730 (95% CI: 612 to 862) at CFR = 26%, 575 (95% CI: 482 to 679) at CFR = 33%, and 474 (95% CI: 398 to 560) at CFR = 40%.[121]

Cross-method synthesis: Both methods yield broadly consistent results, suggesting approximately 400 to 900 cases may have occurred in the DRC as of 20 May 2026, with values above 1,000 not being excludable given current data.[5]

Methods¶

Travel-based back-calculation (Method 1): This analysis uses the same approach as Imai et al. (2020).[122] It estimates the number of BVD cases in the DRC using: the reported number of confirmed BVD cases in Uganda (two as of 20 May 2026); the estimated daily number of international travellers from Ituri and Nord Kivu Provinces to Uganda; and assumptions about how long after infection individuals are detected.[123] The main scenario uses Ituri Province alone as the source population; a sensitivity run expands the source to Ituri and Nord Kivu combined. Three detection windows are considered — 10, 15, and 20 days — approximately corresponding to the incubation period plus the onset-to-detection period.[124]

Back-calculation from deaths (Method 2): This method back-calculates the number of BVD cases from the total number of suspected BVD deaths reported (131 as of 20 May 2026), using previously published estimates of the distribution of time from symptom onset to death, and examines the sensitivity of estimates to the assumed doubling time and start date of the epidemic, and to estimates of the CFR.[125] It uses an analytical formulation linking cumulative cases (by symptom onset date) to cumulative deaths, under the assumptions that the outbreak was seeded by a single zoonotic case and has been growing exponentially since.[126] This method applies to DRC at the provincial level with Ituri as the implied primary source area. Three growth scenarios are examined: a main scenario with a 14-day doubling time, and two sensitivity analyses with 7- and 21-day doubling times, each crossed with three CFR values.[127]

Assumptions¶

Detection window (Method 1): Method 1 relies on characterising the detection window, which is challenging for Bundibugyo: the mean incubation period has been estimated at 6–11 days, but the onset-to-detection period is not well characterised for this species.[128] Three values are tested — 10, 15, and 20 days — defined approximately as the incubation period plus the onset-to-detection period.[129]

Travel probability and source population (Method 1): The daily probability of travel to Uganda is calculated as the ratio of daily outbound international travellers from the source population to the source population size.[130] Two source population definitions are considered: Ituri alone, and Ituri and Nord Kivu combined.[131] Ituri-side PoEs (Goli, Ntoroko Main, Odramacaku, Vurra) yield a total mean of 1,871 daily passengers.[132]

Epidemic structure and growth (Method 2): The outbreak is assumed to have been seeded by a single zoonotic case in the human population and to have grown exponentially since, with a doubling time varied across three scenarios.[133] In the absence of data on the timing of cases or deaths, the growth rate and date of origin cannot be estimated from data, so a range of plausible assumptions for both is explored.[134] The main scenario uses a 14-day doubling time; sensitivity analyses use 7 and 21 days.

Onset-to-death distribution (Method 2): The delay from symptom onset to death is assumed to be gamma-distributed with mean 11.37 days and SD 5.41 days, from Rosello et al., implying shape parameter α = 4.42 and rate parameter β = 0.388/day.[135] Estimates of the CFR and onset-to-death distribution for BVD are scarce, and uncertainty in the estimated distribution of the onset-to-death delay is not accounted for.[10]

Case fatality ratio: Three CFR values are examined — 26%, 33%, and 40% — spanning the central estimate and 95% confidence bounds derived from CDC data on the two previous BVD outbreaks (55 deaths, 169 cases).[136]

Deaths as a more complete surveillance stream: Method 2 relies on reported suspected deaths, which are assumed to be more completely captured than cases.[137] The method relies on suspected BVD deaths detected through syndromic surveillance, which is imperfect, and these figures could be either an over- or underestimate of deaths actually attributable to BVD.[138]

Limitations¶

Narrow numerator for Method 1: There is high uncertainty in Method 1 estimates, as these are based on only two exported cases, which travelled specifically for the purposes of seeking care.[139] This small numerator drives wide confidence intervals across all six scenarios.

Methodological simplifications in Method 2: Method 2 uses a simple deterministic epidemic model to explore the relationship between epidemic growth rate and epidemic size to date; it does not fully capture stochasticity or overdispersion (super-spreading) in the underlying processes and therefore underestimates true uncertainty.[140]

Travel data completeness and representativeness: The border crossing data are not comprehensive and may miss informal crossings, in which case the epidemic size might be lower than the estimates presented. The PoE numbers also cover other social service-seeking activities including educational, family, and religious travel, and include border community movements, which may introduce bias.[11]

Inability to estimate growth rate or epidemic start from current data: In the absence of data on the timing of cases or deaths, the growth rate and date of origin of the current epidemic cannot be estimated; a range of plausible assumptions is therefore explored, and these could be refined as more detailed data become available.[141]

Borrowed epidemiological parameters: Estimates of the CFR and time from symptom onset to death for BVD are scarce; the analysis uses limited available evidence from previous outbreaks and did not account for uncertainty in the estimated distribution of the onset-to-death delay.[10]

Syndromic death reporting quality: Method 2 relies on suspected BVD deaths detected through syndromic surveillance, which is imperfect; these figures could be either an over- or underestimate of deaths actually attributable to BVD.[138]
