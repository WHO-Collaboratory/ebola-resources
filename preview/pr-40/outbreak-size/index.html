Outbreak Size

Latest analysis data cutoff: 11 July 2026. Page refreshed: 16 July 2026.

At a glance

Over time the under-ascertainment ratio has narrowed from roughly 40–90× against 10 confirmed cases on 20 May, to 2.0–3.9× against 1,926 confirmed cases on 11 July, based on a 90% credible interval of 3,938–7,525 infections. This is grounded in three separate analyses whose outbreak size estimates converge on substantial underascertainment. Evidence suggests surveillance has been catchung up, but true infections still exceed confirmed counts, with low-to-moderate confidence.

Synthesizing evidence¶

State of the evidence¶

This is synthesis is grounded in three separate analyses published between 20 May to 14 July 2026. McCabe et al. use export-based and death-based back-calculation, Abbott et al. extends McCabe into a live joint Bayesian renewal model, and Mooring et al. projects future size under isolation scenarios rather than estimating a cutoff multiplier.[1][2][3] The evidence base is growing but still thin for the question of current outbreak size.

How the estimates compare¶

The clearest signal is a narrowing detection gap. McCabe et al. estimate about 400–900 cases on 20 May against 10 laboratory-confirmed cases, implying about 40–90× the 10 confirmed cases then identified.[4][5] By 11 July, Abbott et al. estimate 3,938–7,525 infections against 1,926 confirmed cases, or 2.0-3.9×, using more surveillance streams and more recent data.[6] This should not be read as a simple time trend in true incidence, because the later estimate uses later surveillance streams and a different model rather than a repeated application of the same method. Mooring et al. aligns qualitatively on large outbreak potential but is not comparable as a detection multiplier because it reports probabilities of future threshold exceedance under isolation scenarios.[7][8]

Key uncertainties and assumptions¶

All outbreak size estimates depend on sparse early signals being interpreted through strong assumptions. McCabe et al. relies on two exported cases and suspected deaths, with uncertain detection windows, CFR, onset-to-death delays, and border movement.[9][10] Abbott et al. adds more streams but notes that most delays, CFR, and laboratory assumptions remain weakly informed and often driven by prior assumptions.[11] Assumptions about a single zoonotic seed, limited spatial structure, and no susceptible depletion also recur across models.[12][13]

What would reduce uncertainty¶

The most important addition would be line-list data dated by symptom onset, testing, reporting, outcome, and location, because timing is currently inferred mainly through assumed delays.[14][10] Second would be better classification of suspected deaths and cases, including retrospective testing, because death-based and confirmed-case streams anchor several estimates.[15] Third, more complete cross-border movement and export detection data would tighten travel-based back-calculation.[8]

Open questions¶

Abbott et al. raise the question of whether the different data streams tell a consistent story.[16] Mooring et al. names high-quality time-series data on cases and deaths as essential to refine transmission estimates.[17]

What this means for the response¶

The evidence suggests substantial under-detection early on, with surveillance improving enough that the confirmed-count gap appears smaller by July. It still suggests true infections exceed confirmed cases, so confirmed counts alone understate burden, committed care needs, and transmission potential. It also suggests that Ituri and connected Nord Kivu–Uganda movement corridors remain central to interpreting under-detection.[18][19]

Abbott et al. (2026) Estimating the current size of the 2026 DRC Bundibugyo virus outbreak¶

Read the report

Overview¶

This analysis estimates the total number of infections in the ongoing BVD outbreak in DRC — including those never reported — using a discrete-time renewal process fitted jointly to multiple surveillance data streams. It is a real-time joint Bayesian estimate of the current outbreak size, refreshed as new data arrive, fitting the DRC suspected cases, suspected deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and confirmed deaths, and the cases and deaths exported to Uganda simultaneously in a single posterior.[2][20] The headline estimate, as of the 11 July 2026 data cutoff, is 3,938–7,525 cumulative infections (90% credible interval), against 1,926 laboratory-confirmed cases — roughly 2.0–3.9 times as many infections as confirmed cases.[6]

Estimate¶

The headline cumulative infection estimate from the joint posterior, as of the 11 July 2026 data cutoff, is: 30% CrI 4,811–5,623; 60% CrI 4,480–6,204; 90% CrI 3,938–7,525 infections to date, reported and unreported. Against the 1,926 laboratory-confirmed cases at the cutoff, this is roughly 2.0–3.9 times as many infections, so confirmed cases are estimated to capture only a small share of the outbreak.[6]

The initial reproduction number is estimated at 30% CrI 1.89–2.29; 60% CrI 1.71–2.59; 90% CrI 1.43–3.27, and the latest reproduction number at 30% CrI 1.41–1.62; 60% CrI 1.30–1.78; 90% CrI 1.13–2.19.[21] The case-fatality ratio is estimated at 30% CrI 0.45–0.50; 60% CrI 0.42–0.54; 90% CrI 0.36–0.60.[22] The outbreak start is estimated at 30% CrI 2026-03-12 to 2026-03-19; 60% CrI 2026-03-08 to 2026-03-23; 90% CrI 2026-02-28 to 2026-03-27.[23]

Methods¶

Real-time joint Bayesian renewal model: The model is a discrete-time renewal process on a daily grid that fits multiple surveillance streams jointly in a single posterior: DRC suspected cases, suspected deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and confirmed deaths, and cases and deaths exported to Uganda. It estimates latent infections, symptom onsets and deaths over time, the time-varying reproduction number with its growth rate and doubling time, the case-fatality ratio, the ascertainment of each surveillance system, and a short-week-ahead forecast of each stream.[24] This began as a replication of McCabe et al. and evolved into a real-time joint Bayesian estimate fitting more available data streams than the original, with a time-varying reproduction number.[25] The DRC streams are fitted on the incidence scale as between-vintage increments across successive situation reports, which sharpens the reproduction number estimate; McCabe et al. condition on a single cumulative total.[26] The model jointly estimates outbreak size and the fraction of cases each surveillance system reports; McCabe et al. have no ascertainment component.[27] A two-phase seeding structure uses a wide prior on the number of cryptic doublings before the renewal process begins, with the genetic time to most recent common ancestor (TMRCA) used as a lower bound on cryptic duration.[28] All delays — generation interval, incubation period, onset-to-report, onset-to-confirmation, and onset-to-detection abroad — are sampled from priors centred on published Ebola estimates, discretised with double interval censoring, so delay uncertainty propagates.[29] Sensitivity analyses include a frozen re-fit cutting the data one week before the current cutoff, a delay sensitivity run, and a clock sensitivity run using the Exponential Growth rather than Skygrid TMRCA prior. The model is also set beside the McCabe et al. scenario estimates as an external sense-check, and a forward projection from a frozen fit is compared against the Chamla et al. confirmed-case projection and subsequently observed data.[30]

Assumptions¶

Generation interval: A Gamma distribution with mean 15.3 d and SD 9.3 d, taken from the WHO Ebola Response Team 2014 estimate of the EVD serial interval used as a generation-time proxy. This maps to a Gamma shape near 2.71 and scale near 5.65, with priors centred on those values carrying the source’s reported uncertainty on the mean.[31]

Incubation and delay distributions: The incubation period uses the MacNeil et al. (2010) BVD estimate from the 2007 Uganda outbreak (mean 6.3 d, n=24); the mean prior reproduces the 95% CI with a half-normal SD prior as the authors’ own choice.[32] Onset-to-report follows a Gamma with priors centred on the Funk and Abbott (2026) reanalysis of the 2012 Isiro line list (implied mean about 4 d; shape α ∼ Normal⁺(1.18, 0.28), scale θ ∼ Normal⁺(3.69, 1.20)).[33] Onset-to-death is the convolution of two Gamma components — onset-to-admission and admission-to-death — each sampled on natural shape and scale, with priors from the same reanalysis; the implied mean is about 13 d.[34]

Case-fatality ratio: A Beta(6.6, 13.4) prior with mean 0.33 and 95% interval roughly 0.15–0.54, matching the CDC figure of 55 deaths in 169 cases and the corrected central CFR in the 20 May McCabe et al. report. The prior is informative.[35]

Ascertainment: DRC and Uganda ascertainment fractions share a logit-scale hyperprior centred on 75%, reflecting active case-finding under a declared Ebola response rather than baseline passive surveillance.[36]

Cryptic phase and outbreak seed: The outbreak is assumed to have started from a single zoonotic spillover, with the initial infection count near one (Normal⁺(0.1, 0.1)). From that seed the outbreak is assumed to have grown deterministically through an unobserved cryptic exponential phase, doubling m times (prior Normal⁺(3, 3)) at the growth rate informed by the genetic estimate, before the renewal process takes over.[37][38]

Genetic TMRCA as a one-sided bound: The TMRCA is treated as a lower bound on outbreak age — adding sequences can only push it earlier. The TMRCA day is treated as a right-censored, noisy reading of the total outbreak age, a suggestion attributed to N. Ferguson.[39]

Time-varying reproduction number and intervention ramp: Rt is held flat at the established R₀ until a month before the first WHO situation report, then follows a weekly Gaussian random walk on the log scale. The month-long lead allows Rt to start moving before the first report.[40] The outbreak response adds a sampled effect shaped by a logistic ramp at the first WHO situation report on 18 May 2026, assumed to take about 21 days to take effect and constrained to reduce transmission only (δ ∼ Normal⁻(0, 0.4)).[41]

Cross-border travel: Daily outbound travellers from Ituri to Uganda carry a Normal prior centred on 1,871 with SD 200 (≈10% CV), from McCabe et al. Table 3. The source population is kept fixed at the census value of 4,392,200.[42]

Stream independence: Surveillance streams are fitted as conditionally independent given latent incidence but observe overlapping people, which can understate uncertainty. Whether the streams imply mutually consistent outbreak sizes is not assessed.[16]

Non-BVD background: An additive non-BVD background rate follows a lognormal random walk on weekly knots, so a suspected case need not be a true BVD infection. The background is identified by the laboratory positivity (approximately 28% of analysed specimens were positive), which is inferred to be the majority of the suspect pool.[43]

Limitations¶

Reliance on weakly-informed priors: Nearly all of the delays, the case-fatality ratio, and the laboratory assumptions are set by priors informed at best by a handful of literature sources, often from other outbreaks, and in places by the authors’ own prior judgement rather than data from this outbreak. The data do little to move them, so these posteriors largely track their priors.[11]

Aggregate data and weak timing signal: Only cumulative totals at the report date are available, with no counts by symptom onset or any other epidemiologically relevant date. The timing of the underlying epidemic is therefore weakly identified and is recovered only through the assumed delays.[14] The DRC data are situation-report totals; no line list or information on case definitions or reporting completeness is available. The laboratory testing series gives partial information on testing capacity but is incomplete and stops at the cutoff. Every estimate is a model-based extrapolation under strong assumptions, not a measurement.[44]

Revisions not modelled: A later situation report can revise an earlier total up or down as suspects are reclassified and newly-reporting health zones are added, and ascertainment probably rose over the window. This revision process is not modelled.[45]

Stream independence may understate uncertainty: Streams are fitted as conditionally independent given latent incidence but observe overlapping people, which can understate uncertainty. Whether the streams imply mutually consistent outbreak sizes is not assessed.[16]

Intervention ramp is weakly identified: With only a few situation reports straddling the ramp, the ramp effect and the pre-ramp reproduction number are not well separated.[46]

National bed model cannot capture local saturation: The treatment-centre model carries one national bed capacity and one national demand, so it cannot represent local saturation. On 13 June, Ituri was at 93.9% occupancy while Sud-Kivu was at 21.9%, and the national bed shortfall understates local unmet need.[19]

Inherited structural assumptions from McCabe et al.: The model inherits McCabe et al.'s assumptions of a single zoonotic seed, an assumed generation interval, no spatial structure beyond the Ituri/Nord Kivu split, and no depletion of susceptibles. The onset-to-death delay is grounded on Isiro 2012 and the genetic seeding bound on the outbreak-specific clock rate, not propagating clock uncertainty.[12]

LLM-driven implementation not independently replicated: The model code, priors, and analysis were drafted by a language model from the McCabe et al. report and the companion delay reanalysis, then reviewed and revised under human oversight. The work has not been independently replicated against the authors’ own code.[47]

Data sources¶

Primary outbreak data: DRC data come from the situation reports of the Institut National de Santé Publique (INSP). Each report gives the national cumulative suspected cases and deaths, laboratory-confirmed cases and deaths, and the specimens received and analysed by the laboratory, at the report date.[48] At the data cutoff of 11 July 2026, confirmed cases totalled 1,926 and confirmed deaths 702.[49] Suspected deaths totalled 246 and suspected cases 1,077, both as of 26 May 2026 (the last stable vintage before reclassification began).[50] Uganda export data are the three cases and one death exported across the border, taken from WHO situation reports and Disease Outbreak News.[51] From 13 June, daily patient-movement data (admissions, in-care deaths, rule-outs, and absconded flows) are read from the treatment-centre Tableau 6 in the INSP situation reports.[52]

Auxiliary data: Cross-border traveller volume and source population come from McCabe et al.; the source population is fixed and the traveller volume is given a Normal prior around the McCabe et al. figure.[53] A BEAST X v10.6.0 analysis of 139 sequenced genomes places the TMRCA at a mean of 15 March 2026 (95% HPD 09 Feb–12 Apr) under the Skygrid prior, with an evolutionary rate of approximately 1.1×10⁻³ substitutions/site/year.[54] The incubation period prior uses the Bundibugyo virus estimate from the 2007 Uganda outbreak (mean 6.3 d, 95% CI 5.2–7.3, n=24; MacNeil et al., 2010).[55] Onset-to-report and onset-to-death delays use a Bayesian reanalysis of the 2012 Isiro Bundibugyo virus outbreak line list (Funk and Abbott, 2026), taking the onset-to-admission delay with priors centred on the reanalysis posterior (implied mean about 4 d).[56] The confirmed onset-to-sample delay is grounded on the NEJM DRC 2026 BVD cohort (N=129), estimated as a Gamma with mean 7.4 d (95% CrI 5.3–13.5) and median 4.8 d (95% CrI 3.46–7.84).[57] The CFR prior mean of 33% is anchored in the CDC summary of 55 deaths in 169 cases across the two previous BVD outbreaks.[58]

Mooring et al. (2026) Modeled Scenario Projections for the Ebola Disease Outbreak Caused by Bundibugyo Virus, 2026¶

Read the report

Overview¶

This CDC analysis uses a branching process transmission model to project the future trajectory of the 2026 BVD outbreak over a 90-day horizon, estimating how large the outbreak could become under different assumptions about the current number of deaths and different levels of patient isolation. The model was calibrated to three putative cumulative death counts and projected for four possible intervention scenarios ranging from poor (20%) to extremely high (95%) levels of isolation and treatment of symptomatic persons.[3] The central finding is that without high levels of patient isolation, the outbreak is likely to grow very large: with poor isolation levels of patients with BVD (20%) and no other interventions, the likelihood of an outbreak that exceeds 20,000 cases within 3 months is 65%.[7]

Estimate¶

This analysis produces forward projections rather than a current true case count estimate; it does not provide a detection multiplier or a point estimate of true cases at the data cutoff. The projections are expressed as probabilities of exceeding case or death thresholds within 90 days of May 24, 2026, under three death-calibration scenarios and four isolation scenarios.

Under the 50 deaths calibration: assuming 20% of infected persons were successfully isolated beginning May 24, 2026, projections showed ≥20,000 cumulative cases in 65% of simulations, ≥10,000 cumulative cases in 85% of simulations, and ≥4,000 cumulative deaths in 69% of simulations.[59] At 70% isolation, 94% of simulations projected <10,000 cases and only 1% projected ≥20,000 cases; similarly, 90% of simulations projected <2,000 deaths and only 3% projected ≥4,000 deaths.[60]

Under the 100 deaths calibration: very large outbreaks were likely in the scenario in which only 20% of patients were isolated (76% of simulations projected ≥20,000 cases and 87% projected ≥4,000 deaths).[61] In the scenario in which 70% of infected persons were isolated, 73% of simulations projected <2,000 cumulative deaths by August 22, 2026, and 10% projected ≥4,000 deaths.[62]

Under the 200 deaths calibration: even with 70% of infected persons isolated, 42% of simulations projected ≥10,000 cases by August 22, 2026.[63]

The median basic reproductive number R₀ = 2.51 (IQI = 2.27−2.82) in the main analysis calibrated to 50 deaths.[64]

Methods¶

Branching process model: A branching process model with the capacity to model transmission-reducing nonpharmaceutical interventions was calibrated to three putative cumulative death counts and projected for four possible intervention scenarios.[3] Each simulated outbreak was initialized with one infected person representing a zoonotic spillover event; this person infected a randomly generated number of additional persons based on assumptions about the basic reproductive number (R₀), and any infected persons were added to the simulation at times selected according to the distribution of intervals from one infection to the next, in turn able to cause further infections.[65] The model was adapted from one applied to previous viral hemorrhagic fever outbreaks, including a Marburg virus disease outbreak in Ethiopia in 2025.[66]

Calibration to cumulative deaths: The model was calibrated to three different numbers of cumulative deaths (50, 100, and 200) to account for uncertainty in the current number of deaths caused by BVD; a simulated outbreak was considered compatible with the real-world outbreak if it reached the assumed number of cumulative deaths by May 24, 2026, and if the first death occurred on or before April 24, 2026. Outbreaks were simulated until 500 simulations met these criteria.[67] Assumptions for the cumulative number of BVD deaths as of May 24, 2026, were based on publicly available situation reports from DRC.[68]

Scenario projections: Four intervention scenarios were assessed for each calibration, each implementing a different level of isolation (i.e., percentage of symptomatic infected persons detected, isolated, and treated: 20% [poor], 50% [moderate], 70% [high], and 95% [extremely high]).[69] Each simulation reported the cumulative number of cases and cumulative number of deaths from the date of spillover until August 22, which would be 90 days after interventions began.[70] The analysis applies to the DRC outbreak broadly, without explicit subnational stratification.

Assumptions¶

Zoonotic seed and single-origin start: Each simulated outbreak was initialized with one infected person, who represented the person first infected from a zoonotic source (a spillover event).[13] This structural assumption of a single spillover constrains inferences about outbreak onset timing.

Basic reproductive number (R₀): Each simulated infected person infected a randomly generated number of additional persons based on assumptions about R₀, the average number of persons in a susceptible population infected by an infected person.[71] The median R₀ = 2.51 (IQI = 2.27−2.82) in the main analysis calibrated to 50 deaths.[64] Basic reproductive number estimates for Ebola disease vary widely across outbreaks, and the true value of R₀ for this outbreak might be higher or lower than the values used in this analysis.[72] The analysis did not provide evidence that R₀ for this outbreak is unusually large.[73]

Delay distributions: Intervals from infection to symptom onset, symptom onset to death, and symptom onset to recovery were held constant for all infections within each simulated outbreak but varied among simulated outbreaks.[74] The specific distributional forms and parameter values are not detailed in this document beyond that they were drawn from published Ebola estimates.

Cumulative deaths at calibration date: The model was calibrated to three different numbers of cumulative deaths (50, 100, and 200) to account for uncertainty in the current number of deaths caused by BVD.[75] The true number of BVD deaths that occurred through May 24, 2026, is unknown; some deaths from BVD might not have been confirmed, and it is possible that other deaths might have been incorrectly attributed to BVD.[76]

Isolation intervention mechanics: On the intervention start date, the designated percentage of symptomatic persons was selected to begin isolating, with an average delay of 2 days until isolation and treatment; the same percentage of persons who later developed signs or symptoms was selected to begin isolating with an average delay of 2 days from symptom onset. Simulated persons in isolation were prevented from causing any onward transmission, and the model implicitly assumed that isolated persons who died were safely buried.[77]

No behavioral change: Changes in behavior that reduce risk for infection (e.g., avoiding contact with ill persons) were not included in the model and might help limit outbreak size.[78]

No immunity accumulation: The model did not account for transmission reductions attributable to an increase in the proportion of the population with infection-induced immunity. Given the population size of the communities where this outbreak is occurring, this limitation is unlikely to affect the validity of the projections over the time span and numerical ranges of cases presented in this analysis; however, the model could project unrealistically large outbreaks if applied to longer periods.[79]

No relapses: The model did not include infection relapses after recovery, which is unlikely to affect this analysis, but relapses could be important drivers of the course of the epidemic over a longer period.[80]

Limitations¶

Uncertainty in current death counts: The true number of BVD deaths that occurred through May 24, 2026, is unknown. Some deaths from BVD might not have been confirmed; similarly, it is possible that other deaths might have been incorrectly attributed to BVD.[76] The analysis addresses this by calibrating to three different death counts (50, 100, 200) rather than resolving the uncertainty.

R₀ uncertainty: Basic reproductive number estimates for Ebola disease vary widely across outbreaks, and the true value of R₀ for this outbreak might be higher or lower than the values used in this analysis. High-quality data on changes in the number of cases and deaths over time are essential to more precisely estimate R₀.[17]

Behavioral change not modelled: Changes in behavior that reduce risk for infection (e.g., avoiding contact with ill persons) were not included in the model and might help limit outbreak size.[78]

No immunity depletion: The model did not account for transmission reductions attributable to an increase in the proportion of the population with infection-induced immunity, which is unlikely to affect projections over the time span and numerical ranges presented but could cause the model to project unrealistically large outbreaks if applied to longer periods.[79]

No relapses: The model did not include infection relapses after recovery, which is unlikely to affect this analysis, but relapses could be important drivers of the course of the epidemic over a longer period.[80]

Data sources¶

Primary outbreak data: Assumptions for the cumulative number of BVD deaths as of May 24, 2026, were based on publicly available situation reports from DRC.[68] The DRC national public health institute reported 10 confirmed deaths and 223 suspected deaths as of May 24, 2026.[81] A subsequent report with data as of June 2, 2026, reported a cumulative total of 62 confirmed deaths in DRC.[82] These reports drove the three calibration targets (50, 100, and 200 deaths), used to bracket uncertainty about true death counts.

Auxiliary data (parameters from prior outbreaks): Assumptions about parameters were based on published estimates from previous Ebola outbreaks; estimates specific to BVD were used when available.[83] The analysis does not specify by name which published studies or datasets were used for delay distributions or other parameters. No travel data, demographic data, or line list data from the current outbreak are mentioned as inputs.

McCabe et al. (2026) Estimation of the size of the Ebola outbreak caused by Bundibugyo virus in the Democratic Republic of the Congo: May 20, 2026 update¶

Read the report

Overview¶

This analysis applies two independent approaches to estimate the true size of the BVD outbreak in the DRC as of 20 May 2026: the first uses population movement data alongside evidence of exported cases detected in Uganda; the second back-calculates cases from reported suspected deaths, combined with estimates of the case fatality ratio and onset-to-death distribution from previous Bundibugyo outbreaks.[1] Both methods yield broadly consistent results, suggesting that approximately 400 to 900 cases of BVD may have occurred in the DRC, with values above 1,000 not being excludable given current data.[4]

Estimate¶

Method 1 (travel-based back-calculation), main scenario (Ituri source population): Under a 10-day detection window, the estimate is 470 (95% CI: 58 to 1,306); under a 15-day window, 313 (95% CI: 39 to 870); under a 20-day window, 235 (95% CI: 29 to 652).[84] Under the sensitivity analysis expanding the source population to Ituri and Nord Kivu, the corresponding estimates are 617 (95% CI: 76 to 1,718), 412 (95% CI: 51 to 1,145), and 309 (95% CI: 38 to 858).[85]

Method 2 (back-calculation from deaths): The main scenario (14-day doubling time) yields 860 (95% CI: 721 to 1,015) cases at CFR = 26%, 678 (95% CI: 568 to 800) at CFR = 33%, and 559 (95% CI: 469 to 660) at CFR = 40%.[86] The fast-growth sensitivity (7-day doubling time) yields 1,386 (95% CI: 1,160 to 1,636) at CFR = 26%, 1,092 (95% CI: 914 to 1,289) at CFR = 33%, and 901 (95% CI: 754 to 1,062) at CFR = 40%.[87] The slow-growth sensitivity (21-day doubling time) yields 730 (95% CI: 612 to 862) at CFR = 26%, 575 (95% CI: 482 to 679) at CFR = 33%, and 474 (95% CI: 398 to 560) at CFR = 40%.[88]

Cross-method synthesis: Both methods yield broadly consistent results, suggesting approximately 400 to 900 cases may have occurred in the DRC as of 20 May 2026, with values above 1,000 not being excludable given current data.[4]

Methods¶

Travel-based back-calculation (Method 1): This analysis uses the same approach as Imai et al. (2020).[89] It estimates the number of BVD cases in the DRC using: the reported number of confirmed BVD cases in Uganda (two as of 20 May 2026); the estimated daily number of international travellers from Ituri and Nord Kivu Provinces to Uganda; and assumptions about how long after infection individuals are detected.[90] The main scenario uses Ituri Province alone as the source population; a sensitivity run expands the source to Ituri and Nord Kivu combined. Three detection windows are considered — 10, 15, and 20 days — approximately corresponding to the incubation period plus the onset-to-detection period.[91]

Back-calculation from deaths (Method 2): This method back-calculates the number of BVD cases from the total number of suspected BVD deaths reported (131 as of 20 May 2026), using previously published estimates of the distribution of time from symptom onset to death, and examines the sensitivity of estimates to the assumed doubling time and start date of the epidemic, and to estimates of the CFR.[92] It uses an analytical formulation linking cumulative cases (by symptom onset date) to cumulative deaths, under the assumptions that the outbreak was seeded by a single zoonotic case and has been growing exponentially since.[93] This method applies to DRC at the provincial level with Ituri as the implied primary source area. Three growth scenarios are examined: a main scenario with a 14-day doubling time, and two sensitivity analyses with 7- and 21-day doubling times, each crossed with three CFR values.[94]

Assumptions¶

Detection window (Method 1): Method 1 relies on characterising the detection window, which is challenging for Bundibugyo: the mean incubation period has been estimated at 6–11 days, but the onset-to-detection period is not well characterised for this species.[95] Three values are tested — 10, 15, and 20 days — defined approximately as the incubation period plus the onset-to-detection period.[96]

Travel probability and source population (Method 1): The daily probability of travel to Uganda is calculated as the ratio of daily outbound international travellers from the source population to the source population size.[97] Two source population definitions are considered: Ituri alone, and Ituri and Nord Kivu combined.[98] Ituri-side PoEs (Goli, Ntoroko Main, Odramacaku, Vurra) yield a total mean of 1,871 daily passengers.[99]

Epidemic structure and growth (Method 2): The outbreak is assumed to have been seeded by a single zoonotic case in the human population and to have grown exponentially since, with a doubling time varied across three scenarios.[100] In the absence of data on the timing of cases or deaths, the growth rate and date of origin cannot be estimated from data, so a range of plausible assumptions for both is explored.[101] The main scenario uses a 14-day doubling time; sensitivity analyses use 7 and 21 days.

Onset-to-death distribution (Method 2): The delay from symptom onset to death is assumed to be gamma-distributed with mean 11.37 days and SD 5.41 days, from Rosello et al., implying shape parameter α = 4.42 and rate parameter β = 0.388/day.[102] Estimates of the CFR and onset-to-death distribution for BVD are scarce, and uncertainty in the estimated distribution of the onset-to-death delay is not accounted for.[10]

Case fatality ratio: Three CFR values are examined — 26%, 33%, and 40% — spanning the central estimate and 95% confidence bounds derived from CDC data on the two previous BVD outbreaks (55 deaths, 169 cases).[103]

Deaths as a more complete surveillance stream: Method 2 relies on reported suspected deaths, which are assumed to be more completely captured than cases.[104] The method relies on suspected BVD deaths detected through syndromic surveillance, which is imperfect, and these figures could be either an over- or underestimate of deaths actually attributable to BVD.[15]

Limitations¶

Narrow numerator for Method 1: There is high uncertainty in Method 1 estimates, as these are based on only two exported cases, which travelled specifically for the purposes of seeking care.[9] This small numerator drives wide confidence intervals across all six scenarios.

Methodological simplifications in Method 2: Method 2 uses a simple deterministic epidemic model to explore the relationship between epidemic growth rate and epidemic size to date; it does not fully capture stochasticity or overdispersion (super-spreading) in the underlying processes and therefore underestimates true uncertainty.[105]

Travel data completeness and representativeness: The border crossing data are not comprehensive and may miss informal crossings, in which case the epidemic size might be lower than the estimates presented. The PoE numbers also cover other social service-seeking activities including educational, family, and religious travel, and include border community movements, which may introduce bias.[8]

Inability to estimate growth rate or epidemic start from current data: In the absence of data on the timing of cases or deaths, the growth rate and date of origin of the current epidemic cannot be estimated; a range of plausible assumptions is therefore explored, and these could be refined as more detailed data become available.[106]

Borrowed epidemiological parameters: Estimates of the CFR and time from symptom onset to death for BVD are scarce; the analysis uses limited available evidence from previous outbreaks and did not account for uncertainty in the estimated distribution of the onset-to-death delay.[10]

Syndromic death reporting quality: Method 2 relies on suspected BVD deaths detected through syndromic surveillance, which is imperfect; these figures could be either an over- or underestimate of deaths actually attributable to BVD.[15]

Data sources¶

Primary outbreak data: A total of 516 suspected cases including 131 deaths reported as of 20 May 2026 in the DRC.[107] Eight laboratory-confirmed cases in Ituri Province (from 13 samples) and two confirmed cases in Kampala, Uganda, among travellers from Ituri Province.[5] The 131 suspected deaths serve as the numerator for Method 2, and the 2 exported confirmed cases in Uganda serve as the numerator for Method 1.

Auxiliary data: Point of entry (PoE) data shared by WHO on 16 May 2026, covering traveller screening at PoEs along the Uganda–DRC border: Busanza, Busunga, Goli, Mpondwe, Ntoroko Main, Odramacaku, and Vurra.[108] Data were received from four sitreps, each covering a one-week period: epiweek 10 (2–8 March 2026), epiweek 11 (9–15 March 2026), epiweek 15 (6–12 April 2026), and epiweek 18 (27 April–3 May 2026).[109][110] The onset-to-death distribution is taken from Rosello et al. (eLife), estimated from the 2012 Bundibugyo outbreak in Isiro, DRC: gamma-distributed with mean 11.37 days and standard deviation 5.41 days.[111] CFR values of 26%, 33%, and 40% are derived from CDC data on the two previous BVD outbreaks (55 deaths, 169 cases), spanning the central estimate and 95% confidence bounds.[103]

Methods overview

These pages are produced by a semi-automated evidence-synthesis pipeline that pairs AI models with human review at each major checkpoint: one model reads each source analysis on its own and grounds every extracted claim in a verbatim quoted passage; a second model takes the resulting structured comparison as input, and drafts the narrative. Human analysts then review and edit the output to ensure accuracy and completeness prior to publishing.

Identifying analyses. Analyses are identified through continuous monitoring of preprint servers, peer-reviewed publications, and grey literature. An analysis is included if it directly addresses the 2026 Bundibugyo virus disease (BVD) outbreak and provides a quantitative estimate relevant to a given topic (e.g., outbreak size, risk of spread). Each included source is logged, and if the authors later release an updated version, the newer version supersedes the earlier one.

Preparing the source text. Each source text, regardless of source type (e.g., PDF, HTML), is converted to Markdown. A human analyst then curates the converted file and removes sections that carry no evidentiary content, such as references, acknowledgments, author contributions, affiliations, and declarations of interest, to keep extraction focused and reduce the number of input tokens used.

Extracting structured evidence. The curated text is split into individually citeable units, one per sentence, and passed to Claude Sonnet 4.6. For each of a fixed set of dimensions (e.g., estimates, methods, limitations), the model answers from that one source alone and must ground every factual claim in a verbatim span of its text.

Synthesizing the narrative. OpenAI’s GPT-5.5 model reads the dimensions extracted for each analysis and synthesizes the information, covering the state of the evidence, how the estimates compare and why they diverge, the key uncertainties and assumptions shared across analyses, what would most reduce that uncertainty, and what it implies for the response.

Human review and publication. Human analysts edit the draft for accuracy, completeness, and tone prior to publishing.

Limitations. This pipeline surfaces only publicly available analyses; works that have not been made public are not captured.
