Outbreak Size

Key findings

The most recent current-size analysis estimates 2,778–14,014 infections (90% credible interval) as of 14 June, versus 808 laboratory-confirmed cases; earlier analyses from May 2026 estimated roughly 215–3,224 cases or infections across scenario intervals. Five analyses are currently incorporated, all of which converge on material under-detection despite differing dates, scopes, and methods. The evidence suggests response planning should assume a substantially larger outbreak than confirmed counts alone indicate, with low-to-moderate confidence.

Synthesizing evidence¶

State of the evidence¶

The evidence base is thin but growing: five rapid analyses dated 22 May to 17 June 2026, including travel-based back-calculations, death back-calculation, ABC mobility inference, a joint Bayesian renewal model, and one CDC branching-process projection rather than a direct current-size estimate.[1][2][3][4][5] The analyses use rely upon overlapping surveillance and exported case data, but the main modelling approaches are sufficiently different to make convergence meaningful.

How the estimates compare¶

Late-May current-size estimates mostly put the true burden in the hundreds to low thousands: BlueDot estimated 359 cases, rising to 718 under export under-detection sensitivity; McCabe et al. estimated about 280–2,520; and Epistorm estimated posterior medians of 1,047–1,944 infections with 90% credible intervals up to 3,224.[6][3][7] Abbott et al.’s later 14 June estimate is larger, 2,778–14,014 infections, and is not directly comparable to May estimates because it uses later data and fits multiple surveillance streams jointly.[8] Mooring et al. do not estimate current true size, but its projections are consistent with an outbreak already large enough to generate high short-term risk under a low isolation scenario.[5]

Key uncertainties and assumptions¶

The largest uncertainties are the number and detectability of exported cases, cross-border movement volumes, geographic source population, CFR and delay distributions borrowed from past BVD or Ebola outbreaks, and the growth trajectory before detection.[9][10] A shared structural limitation is that several estimates depend on aggregate situation-report counts rather than line lists with onset, detection, death, and location dates.[11] Confidence is low-to-moderate because method convergence is offset by sparse export events, changing surveillance definitions, and strong prior assumptions.

What would reduce uncertainty¶

The most useful update would be a reconciled line list linking suspected, confirmed, fatal, recovered, exported, and hospitalized cases by onset, report, death or recovery date, health zone, and classification history. Better point-of-entry and informal mobility data, investigation of whether exports were routine travellers or healthcare-seeking cases, and additional genomic dating would directly test the assumptions that these estimates rely upon.[12][13]

What this means for the response¶

The evidence suggests confirmed counts are a lower bound and that operational planning should assume substantial undetected transmission in eastern DRC, especially around Ituri and connected cross-border corridors.[14][6] It also suggests cross-border preparedness remains central, with Epistorm flagging elevated onward-importation risk to nearby Ugandan and regional destinations and Abbott et al. estimating bed demand above national reported capacity.[15][16][17]
